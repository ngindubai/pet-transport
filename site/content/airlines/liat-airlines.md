LIAT (Leeward Islands Air Transport) has been the primary inter-island carrier for the Eastern Caribbean for decades, connecting islands from Trinidad and Tobago in the south to Puerto Rico in the north. However, the airline has faced significant operational challenges.

## LIAT's current status

LIAT entered administration in 2020 following the COVID-19 pandemic's devastating impact on Caribbean aviation. The airline has since operated in a reduced and restructured form. Before making any booking or planning involving LIAT, we strongly recommend checking current operational status directly at liat.com or through a Caribbean travel agent.

Routes, frequencies, and pet policies all depend on the current operational state of the airline.

## Pets on LIAT when operational

Historically, LIAT has permitted small pets in the cabin on its inter-island turboprop services (typically ATR aircraft). The small aircraft size and island-hopping nature of the routes mean policies are pragmatic rather than highly formalised.

Cargo live animal services have been available for larger animals on some routes. Contact the LIAT cargo team for details.

## Alternative Caribbean inter-island carriers

If LIAT is not operating your required route or is not accepting pets, consider:

**Caribbean Airlines (BW):** Operates throughout the Caribbean and accepts pets on applicable routes. Hub at Port of Spain (POS), Trinidad.

**InterCaribbean Airways:** Operates inter-island turboprop services and may accommodate small animals.

**WestJet and Air Canada:** Serve major Eastern Caribbean islands from Canada with hold pet services.

**American Airlines and JetBlue:** Connect several Eastern Caribbean islands to US hubs with hold pet services.

## Travelling with a pet in the Eastern Caribbean

Each island nation has its own pet import requirements. Most require a health certificate, current rabies vaccination, and in some cases an import permit. Countries with strict biosecurity (notably Barbados and St Kitts and Nevis) can require advance permits and inspections.

Always research your specific destination island's rules before travel.
