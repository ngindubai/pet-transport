Search "DEFRA approved pet transport" and most of what comes back is marketing copy: a badge on a website, a line in a quote email, a claim with nothing behind it. DEFRA doesn't hand out a single "approved" stamp that a company either has or doesn't. It runs a graded authorisation system, Type 1 and Type 2, tied to journey length and time, and a genuinely authorised transporter can tell you their authorisation number on request. If a company can't, that's the whole answer to whether the badge on their homepage means anything.

Here's what the system actually requires, how the two tiers differ, and how to check a transporter's claim yourself before you hand over a deposit.

## What does DEFRA approval mean for pet transport in 2026?

The legal basis is the Animal Welfare (Transport) (England) Order 2006, which implements retained EU welfare-in-transport standards for commercial animal journeys, dogs and cats included, not just farm livestock. Any business that transports animals commercially, for payment, as opposed to an owner driving their own pet, has to hold a transporter authorisation issued by APHA (the Animal and Plant Health Agency) once a journey crosses defined thresholds.

Below 65km, no authorisation is required. Above that distance, commercially, a transporter needs Type 1 at minimum. The rule exists to catch exactly the situation most owners picture when they book a pet courier: a van, a driver, a fee, and a journey long enough that welfare conditions genuinely matter, temperature, rest, water access, how the animal is secured.

"DEFRA approved" isn't a marketing phrase with a fixed legal meaning. What has a fixed legal meaning is holding a current Type 1 or Type 2 authorisation number issued by APHA's Welfare in Transport (WIT) team, and being able to produce it.

## Type 1 vs Type 2 authorisation: which covers what?

**Type 1** applies to commercial animal journeys over 65km and up to 8 hours. It's the baseline authorisation, covering the majority of domestic pet transport: a courier taking a dog from London to Manchester, or collecting a cat for delivery to an export vet ahead of an international flight. Requirements include a suitable, cleanable vehicle, a competent driver, and basic record-keeping on the journey undertaken.

**Type 2** applies once a journey runs over 8 hours. This is where the requirements step up meaningfully:

- **An approved vehicle** fitted with a satellite navigation system that logs the journey in real time, not just a driver's word that the route was followed.
- **A named, competent driver and attendant**, each holding a Certificate of Competence specific to the species being transported.
- **A journey log** submitted in advance, detailing planned stops, rest periods, and contingency arrangements if something goes wrong en route.
- **Contingency planning** for breakdowns, extreme weather, or a journey running over its planned time.

A London to Edinburgh run, or a domestic leg feeding a pet toward a departure airport from the far side of the country, can tip into Type 2 territory depending on route and stops. A transporter genuinely equipped for Type 2 work will hold that specific authorisation, not just Type 1. Booking a Type 1-only operator for a journey that actually needs Type 2 oversight isn't just a technicality: it means none of the extra journey-logging and contingency requirements apply, on a trip long enough that they exist for a reason.

**A worked example.** Say a family in Penzance is relocating to Frankfurt and their cargo booking departs from Heathrow. Cornwall to west London is roughly 280 miles and, with a reasonable rest stop, comfortably clears 8 hours of road time. That single domestic leg needs a Type 2-authorised transporter: an approved, GPS-logged vehicle, a driver and attendant both holding a species-specific Certificate of Competence, and a journey log filed before departure covering planned stops and contingency arrangements. A Manchester-to-Heathrow run for the same booking, by contrast, sits comfortably inside Type 1 territory at under 4 hours. Same international move, same cargo booking at the other end, two different domestic authorisation requirements depending purely on where the journey starts.

## How do you actually verify a transporter is DEFRA approved?

Two practical routes, depending on which tier you're checking.

**For Type 2 (long journey) authorisation:** APHA publishes an updated register most months as a PDF, listing every current Type 2 authorised transporter by company name and authorisation number. It's public, searchable, and dated, so cross-check the month against how recently you're booking; authorisations lapse and get renewed, and a stale copy of the list floating around online isn't proof of current status.

**For Type 1, or if a company doesn't appear on the published Type 2 list:** contact APHA's Welfare in Transport team directly, WIT@apha.gov.uk or 03000 200 301, and give them the authorisation number the transporter has quoted you. This is the step most owners skip, because it takes an extra phone call, and it's exactly the step that separates a real authorisation from a number invented for a website.

The practical version of this checklist: ask for the authorisation number before you book, not after. A legitimate transporter gives it without hesitation, because it's a matter of public record. Hesitation, or a vague answer about "being fully insured and compliant" without a number, is the tell.

**What the register actually shows.** Each monthly PDF lists the operator's trading name, the authorisation number itself, its expiry date, and sometimes the species it covers, since a Type 2 authorisation for cattle transport doesn't automatically extend to companion animals. Match all three, name, number, expiry, against what the transporter has told you. A number that belongs to a different company, or one that expired two renewal cycles ago, is worth a direct question before you book, not an assumption that it's just an admin lag.

**A quote that mentions authorisation but not a number is worth a follow-up question.** Genuine operators tend to state the authorisation number on their own website or paperwork unprompted, precisely because it's a selling point they're entitled to make. A company that talks about being "government approved" or "fully licensed" in general terms, without a specific number attached, hasn't necessarily done anything wrong, but it's a gap worth closing with a direct question before committing a deposit.

## What additional credentials matter alongside DEFRA?

DEFRA authorisation covers one thing precisely: the road journey inside Great Britain meets welfare-in-transport law. It says nothing about international documentation, airline cargo booking, or destination-country compliance, and treating it as a catch-all credential is a mistake.

For a genuinely international move, four separate credentials do four separate jobs:

**DEFRA Type 1 or Type 2 authorisation** covers the domestic collection and delivery leg, if the journey is commercial and over 65km.

**IPATA membership** applies to a coordinating agency managing the full international move: airline cargo booking, export vet appointments, government endorsements, destination clearance. It's a professional body membership, not a government licence, but it sets a baseline standard IPATA requires its members to meet.

**IATA Cargo Agent accreditation** matters specifically for whoever is booking the actual airline cargo space. Not every IPATA agency holds this directly; some work through a separate accredited cargo agent.

**Companies House registration** is the basic business-legitimacy check: is this a real, active, registered company, not a sole trader operating under a name with no paper trail.

A legitimate operator handling a complex international route will typically hold more than one of these, because they cover different parts of the journey. A company that can only point to one, and deflects when asked about the others, is worth a second look.

This is also where quotes start to make more sense. A full-service relocation company charging £1,500 for a UK-to-Germany move isn't pricing a single service; it's pricing a DEFRA-authorised domestic collection, an IPATA-coordinated export vet appointment, an IATA cargo booking, and destination clearance, four separate compliance layers bundled into one fee. A courier quoting £200 for what sounds like the same move is very likely covering only the domestic leg, the DEFRA-regulated part, and leaving the international documentation entirely to the owner. Neither price is wrong on its own terms; the mismatch happens when an owner compares the two quotes as if they cover the same scope of work.

## What happens if a transporter isn't authorised?

Operating a commercial animal transport business above the 65km or 8-hour thresholds without the correct authorisation is a breach of the Animal Welfare (Transport) (England) Order 2006. Practically, for an owner, the consequence isn't abstract: it means there's no regulatory body standing behind the journey.

If a compliant, authorised transporter mishandles an animal, causes injury through poor vehicle conditions, or breaches welfare standards en route, APHA has the power to investigate and act on the authorisation itself, suspension, revocation, further conditions. That's a real enforcement mechanism with teeth.

If an unauthorised operator does the same thing, the owner's only route is a civil claim against the company directly, with no regulatory investigation, no authorisation to suspend, and often no clear paper trail proving what actually happened during the journey. The authorisation number isn't paperwork for its own sake. It's the thing that gives a welfare failure somewhere to go.

## FAQs about DEFRA approved pet transport

**What is the difference between Type 1 and Type 2 DEFRA authorisation?**

Type 1 covers commercial animal journeys over 65km and up to 8 hours. Type 2 covers journeys over 8 hours and carries extra requirements: an approved vehicle with a satellite navigation logging system, a named competent driver and attendant, and a detailed journey log submitted in advance. Most UK domestic pet transport falls under Type 1; long-haul journeys to a departure airport or across the country typically require Type 2.

**Does a company that only flies my pet as airline cargo need DEFRA authorisation?**

No. DEFRA transporter authorisation covers the road journey, not the airline cargo leg. An IPATA agency or full-service relocation company still needs its own domestic collection vehicle to hold Type 1 or Type 2 authorisation if the journey is commercial and exceeds 65km, but the flight itself is governed by IATA Live Animals Regulations and the airline's own policy, not DEFRA.

**How do I check the DEFRA WIT register myself?**

APHA publishes an updated list of Type 2 long-journey authorised transporters most months as a PDF, searchable by company name and authorisation number. For a Type 1 check, or if a company isn't on the published Type 2 list, contact the Welfare in Transport team directly at WIT@apha.gov.uk or 03000 200 301 and quote the authorisation number the transporter gives you.

**Is DEFRA authorisation the only credential I should check?**

No. DEFRA authorisation confirms a domestic transporter meets legal welfare-in-transport standards, nothing more. For international moves, also check IPATA membership for a coordinating agency, IATA Cargo Agent accreditation for anyone booking airline cargo space directly, and Companies House registration for basic business legitimacy. None of these four credentials substitutes for another; a legitimate operator on a complex route will usually hold more than one.

**What happens if a transporter isn't authorised and something goes wrong?**

An unauthorised commercial transporter operating above the 65km or 8-hour thresholds is breaking the law, which means there's no regulatory body to complain to if your pet is injured, the vehicle isn't fit for purpose, or the journey is mishandled. Your only recourse is a civil claim against the company directly, without the backing of an APHA investigation. Authorisation exists precisely so that a welfare failure has a route to enforcement.
