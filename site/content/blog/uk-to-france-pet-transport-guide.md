A dog can cross into France without a single dose of wormer. That surprises most owners who've already booked the vet in for tapeworm treatment before a Dover ferry, because unlike Ireland, Finland, Malta or Norway, France doesn't ask for it on the way in. The rule everyone half-remembers only applies on the way home, and getting that backwards is the single most common mistake we see on this route.

Here's what actually gets a pet from the UK into France in 2026, what it costs depending on how you travel, and where the genuine trade-offs sit between driving it yourself and paying someone else to do it.

## The realistic 2026 cost breakdown

There's no single number for this route. The honest range is roughly GBP 350 to GBP 1,200, and where you land in it depends almost entirely on the method.

**Self-drive via Eurotunnel**, the cheapest reliable option, breaks down like this for a single dog or cat:

- Animal Health Certificate: GBP 89 to 200 (specialist mobile AHC vets now undercut traditional practices; a same-week appointment through a large veterinary chain can run closer to GBP 250 to 350)
- Eurotunnel car fare: from GBP 69 each way on a restricted day-trip ticket, up to GBP 163 to 229 each way for a standard flexible ticket, more again in the school-holiday peak
- Eurotunnel pet fare: GBP 24 each way per animal
- Fuel and tolls, London to the Kent coast and on to a French destination: roughly GBP 60 to 150 depending on distance

Add it up and a modest, off-peak return trip for one dog sits around GBP 350 to 500. Book late in August, take a long standard-class ticket, and add a second pet, and GBP 800 to 900 is realistic.

**A DEFRA-authorised courier** is the other end of the range. Door-to-door professional transport for a single dog on the UK-France corridor typically runs GBP 400 to 800, and can reach GBP 1,000 to 1,200 where the collection or delivery point is far from the tunnel, such as the Scottish Highlands or the south of France beyond Lyon. You're paying for someone else to hold the paperwork, do the driving, and manage the crossing, not for the transport itself to be dramatically longer or more complex than a ferry crossing would suggest.

Neither figure includes kennel boarding, a pet-friendly hotel stopover, or a second pet on a separate line of the AHC, all of which push costs up further.

Multiple pets change the maths in your favour, though. A single AHC can cover up to five animals travelling together, dogs, cats and ferrets in any combination, for one vet consultation fee rather than five. Two dogs on one certificate, one Eurotunnel car fare and two GBP 24 pet fares comes out only modestly more than a single dog travelling alone. It's one of the few places where taking more animals doesn't multiply the bill.

## Eurotunnel, Dover-Calais and the other ferry options

Eurotunnel Le Shuttle is the default for most owners driving their own pet to France, and for good reason: 35 minutes under the Channel, the animal stays in the car the whole time, and there's no deck, no lounge, no separation from the owner.

The process has a fixed shape. Check in at the pet reception at Folkestone (or Calais for the return leg) between one and two hours before your booked departure, not less. A member of staff scans the microchip and checks the AHC and vaccination record against the pet's identity before you're cleared to board. DEFRA and Border Force carry out the UK-side checks at Folkestone; French customs checks happen at Coquelles on arrival. Get this wrong, turn up with an AHC that's expired or a chip that doesn't scan, and you won't board. It happens more often with older AHCs where the owner miscounted the 10-day window than with anything else.

Ferries are the other route, and the experience varies more than owners expect. P&O's Dover-Calais crossing keeps dogs in the vehicle by default, at no extra cost, with the option to upgrade to a dedicated pet lounge with outdoor access for around GBP 12 per dog each way plus GBP 6 per accompanying passenger. DFDS runs the same Dover-Calais and Dover-Dunkirk pattern (dogs stay in the car), but its Newhaven-Dieppe route offers onboard kennels from GBP 20 each way, the cheapest dedicated pet space on any cross-Channel service, with the caveat that DFDS doesn't supply bedding, so bring your own. Brittany Ferries, which mostly serves longer western France and Spain routes rather than the short Dover hop, has the strongest pet-cabin offering of the three, with dedicated kennels and pet-friendly cabins from roughly GBP 35 to 50 each way.

Which is actually cheaper for you depends far more on your car fare and travel date than on the pet-specific fee. A midweek off-peak Eurotunnel crossing can undercut a peak-season ferry booking by well over GBP 100, and vice versa in the quiet season.

## What to physically have ready at the border

Paper matters more than most owners expect on this route, because a smartphone photo of a certificate doesn't count. Bring the original, signed AHC, not a scan or a screenshot, since staff at both the Eurotunnel pet reception and the ferry check-in desks need to see the vet's wet signature and the practice stamp. Keep the pet's vaccination record card or booklet with it, since the AHC references the rabies vaccination batch number and date rather than repeating the full history. A printed copy of the booking confirmation helps too, mainly because queues move faster when everything's in one folder rather than being found on a phone with patchy signal in a Kent car park. None of this is complicated. It just has to be there, in order, before you reach the front of the queue.

## What France actually asks for on paper

Three things, and only three things, need to be in place before the border: a microchip, a current rabies vaccination, and an Animal Health Certificate.

The microchip has to meet ISO 11784 or 11785 and must be implanted before, or on the same day as, the rabies vaccination; do it the other way round and the vaccination doesn't count for travel purposes. First-time rabies vaccinations need a 21-day wait before the animal is legally allowed to cross, which catches out owners moving on a tight relocation deadline more than any other single rule. Get the timing wrong here and there's no shortcut, no letter, no fee that fixes it. The 21 days simply has to pass.

The AHC itself is issued and signed directly by a UK Official Veterinarian (an OV, a vet registered on the government's list for this purpose) and does not need a separate stamp or endorsement from the Animal and Plant Health Agency for pet travel, despite that being a common assumption carried over from commercial export paperwork. What does matter is timing: the certificate is only valid if issued no more than 10 days before you cross into the EU. Book the vet appointment too early, on the assumption that "sooner is safer," and you'll invalidate it before you've even left home.

And the tapeworm point, again, because it's worth repeating precisely: France does not require tapeworm treatment for entry. GOV.UK's own guidance on taking pets abroad lists the countries that do, Ireland, Northern Ireland (unless the pet already holds a Northern Ireland travel document), Finland, Malta and Norway, and France isn't among them. Tapeworm treatment only becomes relevant on the return leg into Great Britain, where it must be given by a vet no less than 24 hours and no more than 120 hours before arrival, and recorded either in an EU pet passport or in Part 2, Section X of the same AHC used for the outbound trip. Cats are exempt from this requirement entirely, in both directions.

France also imposes no restricted entry points and no quarantine for correctly documented pets, which is more relaxed than several other EU destinations on this site. What it does restrict is dog breed by category. Category 1 dogs, unregistered pit bull types, boerbulls, and Tosas without a pedigree, cannot be imported or owned in France at all. Category 2 breeds, including American Staffordshire Terriers, pedigree Tosas and Rottweilers, can travel, but the owner needs a French dog licence (permis de detention), proof of civil liability insurance, a sterilisation certificate, and a behavioural evaluation carried out in France. This catches owners out because none of it shows up on a UK checklist. If your dog falls into either category, sort the French paperwork before you book the crossing, not after.

## Can you fly instead, and is it ever worth it?

Direct flights between the UK and France are short, an hour to Paris, a little over two to Nice or the south, and that brevity makes flying tempting. The catch is that almost every mainstream airline on this route, including Air France and British Airways, requires dogs and most cats to travel as manifested cargo rather than in the cabin or hold as accompanied baggage, which means booking through a cargo agent, a separate crate that meets IATA standards, and a fee on top of the airfare that frequently outstrips the ticket price itself.

So when does it actually make sense? Mostly when the road journey is the real cost, not the crossing. An owner driving from Aberdeen or Inverness to a new home near Toulouse is looking at close to two full days behind the wheel each way before the tunnel is even reached. In that specific case, a short flight into Toulouse or Nice, even with a cargo surcharge, can come out cheaper and considerably kinder to the animal than three days of motorway driving stacked either side of a Channel crossing. For anyone starting within a few hours of Kent, driving via Eurotunnel is almost always both cheaper and less stressful for the pet.

## DEFRA-authorised courier or self-drive: the real trade-off

This is the decision most owners actually agonise over, and it isn't really about money, even though money is what gets discussed first.

Self-driving means you control the schedule, you're with your pet the entire way, and it costs less. It also means you're the one responsible for the AHC timing, the microchip check, the vaccination record and the tapeworm rule for the way home, with no professional double-checking any of it before you reach the tunnel. Miss one date and you're turned away at check-in with a car full of luggage and nowhere to go that afternoon.

A DEFRA-authorised transporter removes that risk. Commercial pet transporters operating journeys over 65 kilometres in the UK need a Type 1 transporter authorisation for journeys up to 8 hours, or a Type 2 authorisation for anything longer, under the retained EU rules on animal transport (Regulation (EC) 1/2005). A straightforward UK-France run, London to Calais and on to a nearby French address, typically falls inside the Type 1 window; couriers who also run longer multi-country legs (say, on to Spain or Portugal) tend to hold the more stringent Type 2 authorisation, which covers both. Either way, ask to see the authorisation number before booking, not after.

What you're paying the extra couple of hundred pounds for isn't a faster crossing. It's someone else owning the paperwork risk, someone else driving through the small hours if that's when the crossing's booked, and, for anxious or reactive dogs, a driver used to managing an unsettled animal rather than a family trying to do it for the first time in a car park at Folkestone. For a confident, well-travelled pet and an owner who's happy to double-check dates themselves, self-driving is the better economics almost every time. For a nervous first-time traveller, an elderly pet, or an owner who simply can't take the days off to drive, the courier fee buys back a lot of certainty.

Take a practical case. A retired couple relocating from Yorkshire to a house near Bordeaux, with an anxious 11-year-old spaniel who has never crossed the Channel, are looking at roughly two days of driving each way plus the tunnel crossing itself if they do it themselves; add fuel, an overnight stop, and the AHC, and the self-drive cost lands close to GBP 550, before accounting for the stress on an elderly dog unused to long car journeys. A courier quote for the same route, door to door, tends to land between GBP 700 and 900. The gap is real money, but for that particular dog and that particular couple, it's buying a professional handler, a fixed schedule, and one less thing to manage during a house move that already has enough moving parts.
