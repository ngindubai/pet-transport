Most people searching for a pet transport service are actually looking for a feeling: someone else taking on the complexity so they can focus on their own move. That's entirely reasonable. International pet relocation involves regulatory paperwork, cargo booking, government endorsements, customs clearance, and timing everything to an often-unforgiving calendar. What tends to be less clear from most quotes is exactly which of those tasks your money is paying for, and which you are still expected to arrange yourself.

This guide covers what a pet transport service in 2026 typically includes, what it does not include, how airport-to-airport and door-to-door options differ in practice, where the hidden costs sit, who is accountable if something goes wrong, and how to read a quote before handing over a deposit.

## What's actually included in a pet transport service?

A standard international pet transport service in 2026 covers six main things.

**Route planning.** The agent identifies the best cargo routing for your pet, factoring in airline live animal restrictions (some carriers ban brachycephalic breeds outright; others operate seasonal heat embargoes between May and September), transit times, connection distances, and the animal handling facilities at any intermediate airports. On a route like UK to Australia, where the pet typically connects via Singapore (SIN) or Dubai (DXB), the quality of the transit holding facility matters. A good agent knows which hubs have IATA CEIV-accredited live animal centres and routes accordingly.

**Paperwork coordination.** The agent reviews your pet's documents and advises on what needs to be done, in what order, and by when. They typically don't complete the official vet health certificate themselves (that must come from a government-authorised official veterinarian), but they will tell your vet exactly what format is required, brief them on any destination-specific requirements they may not know, and often liaise directly with the surgery to catch errors before travel day. This is where experienced agents earn their fee. A health certificate with the wrong endorsement sequence, or a titre test result recorded in the wrong field, can mean rejection at the border.

**An IATA-compliant travel crate.** Most agents include sourcing or supplying the crate. It must meet IATA Live Animals Regulations (LAR) specification: minimum internal dimensions based on the animal's measurements, ventilation on at least three sides, and construction standards that prevent escape or injury. Getting the crate size wrong is a ground-level error with real consequences. An undersized crate is refused at the cargo terminal on the day of travel, and finding a correct-sized replacement at short notice at an airport is expensive and stressful.

**Airline cargo booking.** The agent books a confirmed live animal acceptance slot with the airline. This is not a standard freight booking. It requires the airline to accept the species, the crate dimensions, the route conditions (temperature, connection time, transit facility), and the health documentation, all in advance. Agents with established carrier relationships often have access to limited live animal allocations on busy routes.

**Customs clearance at the destination.** When your pet arrives at the destination airport, it cannot leave until customs and biosecurity inspection is complete. The agent coordinates with a local partner or their own destination office to handle this. For straightforward moves, it takes a few hours. For destinations with mandatory quarantine, the animal goes directly to a government-approved facility until the quarantine period ends.

**Ground transport (door-to-door only).** For door-to-door services, the agent coordinates collection from your home and delivery to the destination address. The UK ground leg is typically handled by a DEFRA-authorised subcontractor. This is covered under a separate section below.

What the service does not include is covered under third-party costs below, but the short version is: the official vet, the government, and the destination-country quarantine facility all charge their own fees, none of which are the transport agent's to absorb.

## Airport-to-airport vs door-to-door: where does the price jump?

The cost difference between airport-to-airport and door-to-door service can be £500 to £1,500 depending on the route, and most of that gap is in the ground logistics rather than the air portion.

Airport-to-airport means you deliver the pet to the cargo terminal at the departure airport (in the UK, typically Heathrow or Manchester), the agent handles the air portion and destination customs clearance, and you or someone you have arranged collects from the destination airport. The air leg and customs are included. The home-to-airport and airport-to-home legs are yours to arrange.

Door-to-door means the agent collects from your address, handles everything in between, and delivers to the destination. For most people doing an international move, this is the practical option. You are unlikely to have your own vehicle at both airports.

Several variables widen the price gap between the two:

**Distance from a major hub airport.** If you are more than 60 miles from Heathrow, a dedicated collection involves significant mileage and time for the driver. The ground cost scales with distance.

**Remote destinations.** In countries with a single major international airport, the destination leg may involve a 3-4 hour ground transfer to your address after customs clearance. That is included in a door-to-door quote and excluded from airport-to-airport.

**Quarantine countries.** In Australia and New Zealand, no agent can deliver your pet to your home on arrival day. The animal goes directly to a government quarantine facility: Mickleham Post-Entry Quarantine near Melbourne for most Australian imports, or an MPI Transitional Facility in Auckland or Christchurch for New Zealand. Door-to-door pricing for these routes includes the quarantine facility handover and the final delivery after the quarantine period ends. Confirm this is in the quote before signing.

**Multiple animals.** More animals means more crates, potentially multiple vehicles, and more coordination. Volume discounts exist, but the total scales with headcount.

## Which costs are pass-through and which are the agent's fee?

Not every line on a pet transport quote is the agent's service fee. A significant portion of any international move involves costs the agent pays to third parties and recharges to you, sometimes at exact cost and sometimes with a handling margin.

| Cost item | Nature |
|---|---|
| Official vet health certificate | Vet fee, passed through |
| Government endorsement fee (APHA/USDA APHIS/FAVV) | Government fee, passed through |
| Rabies titre test (FAVN or RNATT) | Lab and vet fee, passed through |
| Import permit fee | Destination government fee, passed through |
| Airline cargo rate | Airline fee, often passed through with a small margin |
| Terminal handling (HARC Heathrow, dnata ACIC, etc.) | Cargo facility fee, passed through |
| Quarantine board | Government facility fee, passed through |
| IATA crate | Often a service item or retail sale |
| Documentation management | Agent service fee |
| Agent coordination fee | Agent service fee |
| Ground transport (collection and delivery) | May be subcontracted, passed through with a margin |

Pass-through costs are not negotiable with the agent because they do not set them. The Heathrow Animal Reception Centre (HARC) published fee schedule is fixed. The USDA APHIS endorsement fee is set by the USDA. What you can compare between agents is their own coordination fee and, to a lesser degree, their margin on airline cargo rates.

This matters when comparing quotes. Two quotes at similar total figures may include very different things. One may be an airport-to-airport price with government fees excluded. The other may be door-to-door with everything bundled. A line-by-line breakdown is the only reliable way to compare.

Budget an additional 20-25% on top of any headline quote to cover pass-through costs that were not specified. That buffer usually covers government endorsement fees, terminal handling, and fuel surcharges applied by airlines at booking.

## Who's accountable when something goes wrong en route?

This is the part most agents address carefully in their terms and conditions, and it is worth understanding before you commit.

**For the air cargo portion**, the Montreal Convention (the 1999 consolidated convention on international air carriage, ratified by the UK and most countries worldwide) governs carrier liability. Under the Montreal Convention, an airline's liability for loss or damage to cargo, including live animals, is capped at 19 Special Drawing Rights (SDR) per kilogram of the animal's actual weight. At June 2026 exchange rates, that is approximately £20-22 per kilogram. A 30kg dog has a maximum airline liability of around £620 if the worst happens. For a pet with years of history and attachment behind it, that figure is not meaningful compensation.

**For ground transport in the UK**, DEFRA Type 1 and Type 2 transporter authorisations require operators to use appropriate vehicles, trained handlers, and to comply with the Welfare of Animals During Transport (England) Order 2006. Any injury to an animal while in the ground operator's physical custody is a matter for their public liability insurance. Most agents hold cover in the range of £1-5 million for third-party claims arising from their operations.

**The practical gap** is transit insurance taken out by the owner. An agent's contractual liability is usually limited, and airline liability is capped by international convention. Pet transit insurance, purchased separately by the owner before travel, covers the animal's declared value, emergency vet costs during transit, and sometimes trip cancellation costs if the pet is unfit to fly on the day. It does not typically cover delays caused by regulatory problems, documentation errors, or airline scheduling changes.

Ask any agent for their recommended policy or an indication of the cover level. But buy it yourself, and read the exclusions carefully before travelling.

## Reading a pet transport quote: line by line

Most problems with pet transport quotes come down to assumptions on both sides: the owner assumed something was included that was not. Here is a straightforward checklist.

**Ask what is explicitly included:**
- IATA crate (purchased outright or hired for the journey?)
- Health certificate vet appointment and fee
- Government endorsement fee and handling time
- Airline cargo booking and cargo rate (on which airline?)
- Terminal handling at the departure airport
- Customs and import clearance at the destination
- Delivery to the destination address
- Transit insurance (or is this your responsibility?)

**Ask what is explicitly excluded:**
- Titre test if required for the destination
- Import permit fee
- Quarantine fees (for Australia, New Zealand, Taiwan, and others)
- Rebooking costs if the airline cancels or the pet fails health checks on the day
- Additional vet visits (tapeworm treatment, health recheck if travel date shifts)

**Ask about credentials:**
- Does the company hold a DEFRA Type 1 or Type 2 transporter authorisation for UK ground legs? Under the Welfare of Animals During Transport (England) Order 2006, this is required for commercial journeys over 65km by road. Type 1 covers journeys up to 8 hours. Type 2 covers all journeys including those over 8 hours and includes Type 1 as well. (Source: APHA Welfare in Transport guidance, updated 2024.)
- Are they an IATA-approved cargo agent or IPATA member? IPATA is the International Pet and Animal Transportation Association, a voluntary professional body for specialist animal shippers. Neither credential replaces DEFRA authorisation for UK ground legs, but both indicate sector-specific experience.
- Who handles the ground leg at the destination? A named in-country partner, or a locally sourced handler depending on availability?

**Red flags:**
- A single lump sum with no breakdown. You cannot compare quotes or verify what you are buying.
- A total that does not mention government endorsement fees, terminal handling, or import permits. These are real costs that must appear somewhere in the accounting.
- No crate sizing guidance. A crate that is too small will be refused at the cargo terminal, and on the day of travel that is the owner's problem if the agent did not specify correctly.
- No clear statement of who handles destination customs clearance and how long it takes.

A reputable agent gives you a written quote with every line itemised and a clear note of third-party costs that may vary. If you receive a quote without that breakdown, ask for one in writing before paying anything.

## Five questions to ask before booking

1. Is the quote airport-to-airport or door-to-door, and what exactly does that mean for my specific origin address and destination country?
2. Which third-party costs are not included, and can you give me a realistic estimate of those?
3. Do you hold DEFRA Type 1 or Type 2 transporter authorisation for the UK ground leg? Can you share the authorisation number?
4. Who is your named ground agent at the destination, and what is their IATA or local equivalent accreditation?
5. If my pet is refused boarding on the day (rejected health certificate, missed timing window, airline cancellation), what is the process and who bears the rebooking cost?

A pet transport service can genuinely take significant coordination off your hands during an already complicated international move. The value is real. What it requires from you is one piece of careful upfront work: getting a clear, itemised quote and understanding exactly where the agent's responsibility starts and ends, and where yours continues. That clarity makes the whole process much less likely to produce an expensive surprise.
