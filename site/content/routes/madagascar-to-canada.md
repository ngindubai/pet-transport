  h1: "Pet Transport from Madagascar to Canada"
  overview: |
    Madagascar's isolation and rabies-affected reputation might suggest Canada treats an arriving pet with special caution. It doesn't, not for an ordinary family dog or cat. CFIA does list Madagascar as high-risk for dog rabies, current as of 13 March 2025, but that classification's teeth are reserved for commercial dog imports, banned outright since 2022. A personal pet, travelling with its owner, needs a current vaccination and a correctly built certificate. No blood test.

    Ethiopian Airlines does the practical work here. Its Antananarivo-Addis Ababa-Toronto chain is a single-carrier route all the way to Canada, a rarer thing on this corridor than it sounds.

  sections:
    - heading: "A high-risk listing that doesn't touch the family pet"
      body: |
        Madagascar's biosecurity story, its unique wildlife, its own rabies burden, might lead an owner to expect a stricter entry regime on the Canadian side. CFIA's high-risk list, current as of 13 March 2025, does include Madagascar, but the classification's real force is aimed at commercial dog shipments, stopped entirely since 28 September 2022. A dog or cat travelling with the owner named on its papers follows the ordinary process instead: vaccination, certificate, no titre, no permit.

        The detail worth double-checking is CFIA's age line. A dog under 8 months old, not accompanied by its owner, is presumed commercial, and from a high-risk country that presumption is serious. If that description could apply, confirm directly with CFIA before booking anything.

    - heading: "Ethiopian's Addis Ababa link, and why Emirates isn't quite what it looks like"
      body: |
        Ethiopian Airlines flies Antananarivo to Addis Ababa nonstop six times a week, connecting straight into its own nonstop Addis Ababa-Toronto service, the only direct passenger route between continental Africa and Canada. That makes this one of the cleaner African-origin routes on the site: one carrier, one change of aircraft.

        Emirates is the name most owners consider first, given its Dubai hub's reputation for live animal handling, but its Antananarivo-Dubai connection actually operates via a linked Seychelles stop rather than flying nonstop, worth building into any transit-time estimate even though it books as a single connection. Air France via Paris, paired with Air Canada's own nonstop transatlantic sector, is the third realistic option, and the only one that carries a small pet in the cabin.

faqs:
  - question: "Is Madagascar on Canada's high-risk list for dog rabies?"
    answer: "Yes. Madagascar appears on CFIA's list of countries at high-risk for dog rabies, last updated 13 March 2025. For a personal pet this doesn't add a titre test; the listing mainly affects commercial dog imports, banned since September 2022."
  - question: "Does my dog need a rabies titre test to enter Canada from Madagascar?"
    answer: "No. CFIA doesn't require a rabies antibody titre test for a personal pet dog, even one from a high-risk country like Madagascar. A current vaccination, recorded in English or French, is what's checked on arrival."
  - question: "Is there a direct flight from Madagascar to Canada?"
    answer: "No. Ethiopian Airlines via Addis Ababa is the only genuinely single-carrier option, connecting to its own nonstop Addis Ababa-Toronto service. Air France via Paris and Emirates via Dubai (which actually routes via Seychelles) are the alternatives."
  - question: "Why does Madagascar's high-risk listing matter if my pet doesn't need a titre test?"
    answer: "It matters most through CFIA's personal-versus-commercial distinction. A dog under 8 months old travelling without its named owner is presumed commercial, and commercial dogs from high-risk countries are barred outright. Confirm your pet's status with CFIA directly if age or accompaniment could put this in question."
  - question: "Do cats face the CFIA high-risk rules that apply to dogs?"
    answer: "No. CFIA has no federal rabies requirement for cats at all, whatever the exporting country's risk classification. A cat from Madagascar needs a health certificate for airline acceptance but none of the high-risk-list considerations dogs face."
