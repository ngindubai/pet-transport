content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from the United Kingdom to Algeria"
  overview: |
    A Pit Bull-type dog or a Staffordshire Bull Terrier can be turned away at Algiers airport even when the microchip, vaccination and certificate are all in order. Algeria treats certain breeds as a border-control issue separate from the standard paperwork, and it's a detail easy to miss until it's too late to change plans.

    Beyond breed, the route follows a familiar non-EU pattern: microchip, a rabies vaccination given three to four weeks ahead, and an Export Health Certificate rather than a passport. Where this route gets genuinely uncertain is the import authorisation question, sources disagree on whether an accompanied pet needs one, so it deserves a direct call to Algerian officials rather than a guess.

  sections:
    - heading: "Breed and hybrid restrictions decide who can travel at all"
      body: |
        Algeria doesn't publish a single definitive banned-breed list in the way some countries do, but multiple sources describe dogs considered dangerous, Pit Bull-types and Staffordshire Bull Terriers among them, as liable to be refused entry or restricted at the border. Wolf hybrids need documented proof of at least five generations of pure domestic dog ancestry. Savannah and Bengal hybrid cats are prohibited unless similarly far removed from their wild ancestors.

        None of this shows up on a standard vaccination record. Owners with a restricted or borderline breed should contact DSV and the Algerian consulate directly, in writing, before committing to flights or paying for an Export Health Certificate that a breed issue could make irrelevant.

    - heading: "The import permit question nobody answers the same way twice"
      body: |
        Ask five sources whether a pet dog or cat travelling with its owner needs a DSV import authorisation and you'll get at least two different answers. Some describe it as unnecessary for accompanied pets travelling as a family move. Others recommend applying through the Algerian consulate or DSV directly, allowing three to four weeks.

        Given that gap, the safer approach is a direct written enquiry to DSV or the consulate well ahead of travel. It costs a phone call or an email; getting it wrong costs a flight rebooking and a pet held at the airport.

    - heading: "Getting the UK paperwork right"
      body: |
        An APHA-authorised Official Veterinarian has to examine the pet and issue the Export Health Certificate; general practice vets can't sign this document. APHA's guidance notes that applications submitted more than seven working days ahead of the intended certification date are processed seven working days before that date, which is worth building into the booking timeline rather than discovering at the vet's desk.

        Algerian officials expect the certificate in French or Arabic, or in English with a certified translation attached. Sorting the translation alongside the certificate application, not after it arrives, avoids a second round of paperwork before departure.

    - heading: "Flying a pet into Algiers"
      body: |
        Air Algérie flies directly from Heathrow, Stansted and Manchester, and looks like the obvious carrier for the route. Its own policy rules out hold transport for pets on UK flights entirely; a pet booking has to go through Air Algérie Cargo as freight instead.

        British Airways, flying Heathrow to Algiers around five times a week, works through IAG Cargo and PetAir UK on the export side, the more conventional cargo route for this move. Turkish Airlines via Istanbul is worth pricing too, particularly for owners who want the flexibility of Istanbul's dedicated Pet Lounge if a direct Heathrow slot isn't available in time.
