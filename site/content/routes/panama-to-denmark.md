  sections:
    - heading: "The Titre Test Requirement for Denmark"
      body: |
        Denmark's Danish Veterinary and Food Administration (DVFA, fvst.dk) applies EU non-commercial pet movement rules. Panama's classification as an unlisted third country means that arriving pets must follow the titre test pathway.

        The sequence is fixed:

        1. Microchip (ISO 11784/11785) before vaccination.
        2. Rabies vaccination after chip. Must be an approved product.
        3. Wait at least 30 days from the vaccination date before drawing blood.
        4. FAVN blood draw. Sample sent to an EU-approved laboratory (Commission Decision 2000/258/EC).
        5. Result of 0.5 IU/ml or above. If it fails, revaccinate and restart from step 2.
        6. 90-day wait beginning from the blood draw date. Travel to Denmark cannot occur until this period is complete.
        7. DINASA-endorsed health certificate issued within 10 days of departure.

        DVFA inspectors at Copenhagen Kastrup Airport (CPH) check all of this on arrival. The health certificate must include the titre test date, result, and laboratory name.

    - heading: "Denmark vs Norway and Finland on Tapeworm"
      body: |
        One practical advantage of the Denmark route over its Nordic neighbours is the absence of an Echinococcus tapeworm treatment requirement.

        Norway requires praziquantel treatment for all dogs from non-exempt countries, 24-120 hours before arrival. Finland requires the same, 1-5 days before arrival. Both requirements add a logistical step, usually a vet visit at the hub airport during the layover.

        Denmark has no such rule. Echinococcus multilocularis is already established in Danish wildlife, so the pre-entry treatment control that applies in Norway and Finland does not apply here.

        For families choosing between Nordic destinations, this is a relevant consideration. The titre test pathway is the same for all three countries (all non-EU listed), but the Echinococcus step exists only for Norway and Finland.

    - heading: "Routing from PTY to Copenhagen"
      body: |
        Copenhagen Kastrup Airport (CPH) is Denmark's main international cargo hub and a major Scandinavian hub. All Panama-to-Denmark pet shipments require a connection at a European hub.

        Copa Airlines from PTY to AMS is the most direct path. AMS to CPH is a short sector served by KLM and SAS multiple times daily. KLM's live animal handling at Schiphol is well established, and SAS is the natural final-leg carrier for Copenhagen.

        Copa to MAD via Iberia is the alternative if AMS space is limited. From MAD, SAS Cargo or a partner carrier connects to CPH. Iberia serves the PTY to MAD transatlantic route regularly.

        Lufthansa via Frankfurt (FRA) is worth considering for larger dogs. FRA to CPH is short and served frequently. Frankfurt's animal centre handles the transatlantic sector competently.

        Book cargo space 6-8 weeks before the intended travel date. Summer bookings to Scandinavia fill up early.

    - heading: "What DVFA Checks at Copenhagen"
      body: |
        DVFA veterinary officers at CPH cargo terminal carry out border inspections for all arriving live animals. They check:

        Physical condition of the animal: general health, no obvious signs of distress or disease.

        Microchip: scanned and matched against the health certificate number.

        Rabies vaccination: current, post-microchip, at least 21 days since primary vaccination.

        FAVN titre test: result of 0.5 IU/ml or above, from an EU-approved laboratory, blood drawn 30+ days after vaccination, 90 days elapsed from the blood draw date to arrival at CPH.

        Health certificate: EU Annex IV format, DINASA-endorsed, issued within 10 days of travel.

        Collection from the cargo terminal requires your ID, airway bill, and cargo release documentation. Allow two to four hours from the aircraft's arrival time.

    - heading: "Managing the 90-Day Wait"
      body: |
        The 90-day wait is a fixed regulatory period that cannot be shortened, appealed, or paid to waive. It begins on the date of the blood draw, which you can determine precisely. Add 90 days to that date and you have the earliest possible date your pet can enter Denmark.

        Some practical advice during the waiting period:

        Get the IATA crate sorted early. Introduce your pet to the crate as its resting place well before travel. A dog or cat that is relaxed in its crate before the journey will handle the flight significantly better.

        Book cargo space. Most cargo bookings can be made two to three months in advance. Book early to secure the best routing and competitive rates. You can set the travel date based on the calculated end of the 90-day period.

        Prepare copies of all documents. The original DINASA health certificate, the original FAVN laboratory report, and the vaccination record are all originals that must travel with the pet or be available at the cargo terminal. Keep digital copies stored securely.

  - question: "We are flying to Copenhagen ourselves but our pet needs more preparation time because of the titre test. Can we send the pet ahead separately?"
    answer: "Yes. Your pet can travel as unaccompanied cargo once all the titre test steps are complete and the 90-day wait has elapsed. A freight forwarder or PetTransportGlobal manages the shipment. The DINASA health certificate is still issued within 10 days of the pet's actual travel date, regardless of when you depart. We can coordinate the unaccompanied cargo timeline so the pet arrives in Copenhagen within a few days of your own arrival."
  - question: "Is there a Danish quarantine facility if something goes wrong with the documentation at CPH?"
    answer: "Denmark does not have a standard quarantine requirement for pets arriving with compliant documentation. However, if documents are missing or incorrect, DVFA has the authority to detain the animal at a holding facility at the importer's cost while the situation is resolved. This can be expensive and distressing for both animal and owner. Thorough document preparation is the only way to avoid this outcome."
  - question: "Can I use a titre test result that was done in a country other than Panama?"
    answer: "Yes, as long as the laboratory that performed the test is on the EU-approved list under Commission Decision 2000/258/EC, and the test was performed on a blood sample taken from your pet at least 30 days after the vaccination. The country in which the blood draw occurred is less important than the laboratory's approval status and the timing of the draw relative to the vaccination."
