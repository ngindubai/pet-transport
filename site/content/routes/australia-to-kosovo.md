  overview: |
    Turkish Airlines didn't fly to Australia at all until 2024. Now it connects Melbourne, and from January 2026 flies Sydney to Istanbul daily, which closes what used to be the hardest part of this route: simply getting a pet off the continent on a carrier that also has an established programme for handling one at the other end.

    Once in Istanbul, the rest of the journey matches every other Kosovo route on this site. No rabies titre test, from Australia or anywhere else, under Kosovo's current published guidance. What Kosovo does ask for, distinct from the titre question, is Echinococcus (tapeworm) treatment recorded on the certificate, a genuine entry condition rather than a formality.

    Budget six to ten weeks. The vaccination timeline sets the floor; booking cargo space on a still-young route is the part worth starting early.

  sections:

      body: |
        Kosovo's Food and Veterinary Agency hasn't published the kind of formal, dated risk list that a neighbour like Bosnia and Herzegovina uses to sort countries into exempt and non-exempt groups. Current guidance is consistent: no titre test applies to a pet entering Kosovo, whatever the country of origin.

        That holds for Australia specifically, verified separately rather than copied across from the UK and US pages this site already publishes for the same destination. It's a genuine finding worth stating plainly, because a reader could reasonably assume a rabies-free country like Australia would face extra scrutiny going somewhere with less developed veterinary infrastructure. It doesn't, not here.

        For years, the honest answer to "how do I fly a pet from Australia to Kosovo" involved routing through a Gulf carrier to a European hub, then a separate connection to Istanbul or Vienna. Turkish Airlines flying its own aircraft into Melbourne and Sydney changes that. The airline already runs an established AVIH cargo programme and a dedicated Pet Lounge at Istanbul, built for exactly this kind of connecting journey, now reachable without a third airline in the mix.

        Kosovo's entry conditions list treatment against Echinococcus (tapeworm) for dogs and cats, documented on the veterinary certificate, as a genuine requirement separate from the rabies question. It needs to happen at the same appointment as the main pre-export health check, recorded properly, not assumed to be covered by routine Australian vaccination paperwork.
