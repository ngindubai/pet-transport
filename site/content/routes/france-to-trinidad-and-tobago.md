content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from France to Trinidad and Tobago"
  overview: |
    Only one airline flies nonstop between Paris and Miami. Air France runs the sector roughly fourteen times a week, just under ten hours in the air, and no competitor operates it without a stop. That makes it the obvious opening leg for a France-to-Trinidad move, even though Air France's own network stops well short of Piarco.

    The paperwork runs on the same lines as the UK, US and German routes already documented on this site: a rabies titre test, an Animal Production and Health Division import permit, and a Brucella canis test for any dog that hasn't been neutered. Trinidad and Tobago has operated without routine quarantine for compliant pets since April 2013, so getting the file right matters more here than waiting out a long stay once the pet lands.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Miami, and why it's still only half the journey"
      body: |
        Air France's Paris-Miami service is genuinely unique on this corridor: no other airline flies the pair nonstop, at around fourteen departures a week. Miami itself has no direct pet cargo product into Trinidad, though, so American Airlines or Caribbean Airlines picks up the final Miami-Piarco leg.

        London offers a second way in. A short connecting flight from Paris into Heathrow or Gatwick feeds into British Airways' established nonstop Gatwick-Piarco service, run through IAG Cargo, the same routing UK-origin pets already use. Either way, a French pet picks up a second carrier that a Trinidad-to-UK move wouldn't need.

    - heading: "The titre test and permit sequence"
      body: |
        APHD wants the import permit application at least one month ahead of travel, though 1.5 months is the safer buffer given typical processing volumes. Running in parallel is the titre test, FAVN or RFFIT, drawn at least one month after vaccination and sent to an approved laboratory. Order matters here: microchip first, then vaccination, then the blood draw. Get that sequence wrong and the result doesn't count.

        Intact dogs face an extra step cat owners never see: a negative Brucella canis test, ideally taken at the same lab visit as the titre. Cats and neutered dogs skip it entirely, which trips people up more than any other single requirement on this route.
