content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from the Dominican Republic to Denmark: 2026 Guide"
  overview: |
    A rabies vaccination and a blood draw, thirty days apart, then ninety more days of waiting. That is the shape of a Dominican Republic-origin pet move into Denmark, since the DR has not been assessed as an EU-equivalent country for rabies control and Denmark applies the full unlisted third country process as a result.

    None of it is negotiable, and none of it moves faster for an urgent travel date. Plan on five to seven months from the first vet appointment, with the flight itself, via Madrid or Amsterdam, adding only a day or so to the total.

  sections:
    - heading: "The FAVN titre test: why it applies here and what it costs in time"
      body: |
        Denmark, like every EU member state, splits non-EU countries into those with assessed rabies-control equivalence and those without. The Dominican Republic sits in the unassessed group, so a FAVN or ELISA titre test applies: blood drawn at least 30 days after vaccination, a result of at least 0.5 IU/ml, then a 90-day wait from that draw date before Danish border vets will clear the arrival.

        The 90 days runs from the blood draw, not the day the lab reports a result. A fast turnaround saves nothing.
    - heading: "DIGEGA's export process and the routing to Copenhagen"
      body: |
        DIGEGA, the Dominican Republic's Dirección General de Ganadería, validates and signs the export health certificate following its established protocol for dogs and cats. That certificate has to be issued within 10 days of travel, so plan the final vet visit around your confirmed cargo booking.

        Since there's no direct service from Santo Domingo to Copenhagen, Iberia's Madrid connection and KLM's Amsterdam routing are the two established options, with Iberia offering a cabin allowance for smaller pets on the transatlantic leg.
