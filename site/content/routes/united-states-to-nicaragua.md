  h1: "Pet Transport from United States to Nicaragua"
  overview: |
    A rabies vaccination with a floor and a ceiling decides the timing on this route: at least 30 days old, no more than 12 months old, on the day of entry into Nicaragua. Miss the 30-day minimum and an otherwise perfect health certificate does not fix it.

    Nicaragua does not ask for a titre test, so this route is lighter on paperwork than the EU or UK routes on this site. A US health certificate issued within 14 days of entry, the vaccination timed correctly, and a quarantine permit collected on arrival at Managua cover the core requirements.

    Two daily Miami non-stops on Avianca and American Airlines, plus a Fort Lauderdale non-stop on Avianca, make the flight itself straightforward for anyone departing from South Florida. Departing elsewhere in the US usually means connecting to one of those gateways, or routing via Copa's Panama City hub, which brings its own breed restrictions to check first.

  sections:
    - heading: "The 30-day floor most owners miss"
      body: |
        Most countries that require rabies vaccination for pet entry only set a maximum age on the shot, commonly 365 days. Nicaragua sets a minimum too: the vaccination has to be at least 30 days old by the date of entry, and no more than 12 months old. A pet vaccinated the week before departure, which would satisfy almost any "current vaccination" rule elsewhere, does not clear Nicaragua's own window.

        This makes the vaccination the actual pacing item on this route, not the flight or the health certificate. Book the vaccination appointment with the 30-day floor in mind, and the rest of the paperwork, the US health certificate within 14 days of entry and the microchip, fits comfortably around it.

        A microchip is not mandatory under Nicaragua's published general rules, but skipping it is a false economy. It is the only reliable way for an official at Augusto C. Sandino International Airport to confirm the animal in the crate matches the animal on the paperwork.

    - heading: "Getting from a US gateway to Managua"
      body: |
        Avianca and American Airlines both fly Miami to Managua non-stop, daily, and Avianca adds a second daily non-stop from Fort Lauderdale. For an owner already in South Florida, that means a single-carrier booking with no connection needed on the US side.

        Departing from anywhere else in the US usually means a domestic leg to one of those gateway cities first, then the international cargo booking, which is normal for pet shipping and not unique to Nicaragua. The alternative is Copa Airlines via its Panama City hub, useful if a US departure city has better connections to Panama than to Miami or Fort Lauderdale, but Copa only accepts pets as cargo Monday to Thursday and excludes several breeds outright, including Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, English Bulldog, and Cane Corso, regardless of what Nicaragua's own import rules say about that breed.

faqs:
  - question: "How soon before travel does my pet need its rabies vaccination for Nicaragua?"
    answer: "At least 30 days and no more than 12 months before the date of entry into Nicaragua. Both ends of that window matter: a vaccination given too recently fails the entry rule just as a vaccination given too long ago does."
  - question: "Will my pet be quarantined on arrival in Nicaragua?"
    answer: "Not physically, if the vaccination timing, microchip, and health certificate are all in order. Nicaragua issues a quarantine permit on arrival, which is paperwork completed at the port of entry rather than a stay in kennels. A pet that does not meet the entry conditions can still face quarantine, be returned to the US, or in serious cases be refused entry outright."
  - question: "Is there a non-stop flight from the US to Managua?"
    answer: "Yes, more than one. Avianca and American Airlines both fly Miami to Managua non-stop daily, and Avianca also runs a daily non-stop from Fort Lauderdale. Departing from other US cities usually means connecting through one of those gateways, or via Copa Airlines' Panama City hub."
  - question: "Are any dog breeds restricted from entering Nicaragua?"
    answer: "IPSA's published rules do not set a breed-based import restriction for personal pets. The airline carrying the animal can still refuse certain breeds under its own cargo policy. Copa Airlines, for example, excludes Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, English Bulldog, and Cane Corso from cargo regardless of Nicaragua's entry rules."
