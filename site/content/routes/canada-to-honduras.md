content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Canada to Honduras"
  overview: |
    SAG's Ministerial Agreement 331-2009 is still the rulebook Honduran border vets use to check every dog and cat entering the country, and it asks for surprisingly little: a rabies vaccination timed to a 21-day-to-12-month window, an import permit, and a standard health certificate.

    The harder step sits on the Canadian side, and it isn't really about Honduras at all. CFIA has to endorse the export certificate in person, at a booked appointment, before the pet leaves Canada, and that appointment can't happen after the fact. Get that piece right and the rest of this route moves at a fairly ordinary pace.

  sections:
    - heading: "CFIA endorsement is the step that actually needs planning"
      body: |
        A Canadian vet fills out the export certificate. An official CFIA veterinarian then endorses it, and only in person, only while the animal is still on Canadian soil. There's no retroactive fix if the appointment gets missed and the flight is already booked.

        At roughly CAD 20 to 40, the fee itself isn't the obstacle. Finding an appointment slot that lines up with the flight date is.

    - heading: "Honduras doesn't ask for much, but means what it asks"
      body: |
        No titre test. No lengthy quarantine hold for a compliant animal. Just a rabies vaccination given between 21 days and 12 months before arrival, an import permit, and a health certificate that matches the microchip if one's fitted.

        That short list can read as low-stakes. It isn't. SAG's own published rules allow for quarantine where a facility exists, return to the country of origin, or euthanasia for an animal that shows up without matching paperwork. Few requirements doesn't mean loose enforcement.

    - heading: "Routing: Avianca's direct option versus the US connection"
      body: |
        Avianca sells a Montreal-San Pedro Sula routing through its own Central American network, which means the pet never has to clear US agriculture and customs as an intermediate step. United and American both connect through Houston or Miami instead, which works, but typically requires the animal to be re-tendered as a cargo shipment at that US airport rather than carried through on the same booking. For a route where Canada and Honduras both keep things fairly simple, adding an unnecessary US customs step is often the single biggest source of delay and cost.
