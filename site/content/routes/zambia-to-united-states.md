  h1: "Pet Transport from Zambia to the United States"
  overview: |
    Six US airports are equipped to take in a dog arriving from a country on the CDC's high-risk list for rabies. Zambia is one of the roughly 100-plus countries on that list, named on the CDC's page updated 15 April 2026, and Lusaka's Kenneth Kaunda International has no nonstop long-haul route to any of them.

    That combination sets the shape of this route more than anything else does. A dog needs the microchip-before-vaccine sequence, a titre test from a CDC-approved laboratory, and arrival at one of just six designated US gateways. A cat, by contrast, clears the federal side with almost nothing beyond a general health check. Budget 12 to 16 weeks, and expect the titre test timeline to be the thing that actually decides your travel date.

  sections:
    - heading: "What Zambia's high-risk status actually triggers"
      body: |
        A dog vaccinated in Zambia can't simply fly into any US city. CDC restricts high-risk-country arrivals without a valid titre to a short list of airports with a CDC-registered animal care facility, Atlanta, JFK, LAX, Miami, Philadelphia and Washington Dulles, and a reservation at that facility has to be booked before departure. No domestic connection is permitted first.

        The titre test itself sets the pace. Blood has to be drawn at least 30 days after the rabies vaccination, with the result landing at least 28 days before the flight touches down, tested at a CDC-approved lab. Zambia has no confirmed government-approved rabies serology laboratory of its own, so that sample commonly travels overseas before results come back, which is the same courier delay this site has already flagged on the Zambia-UK route. Get the maths wrong here and either the flight slips or the dog arrives without a valid result, which converts straight into a 28-day quarantine paid for by the owner.

    - heading: "Three ways to get out of Lusaka, and where each one lands"
      body: |
        Kenya Airways is currently the only carrier flying Nairobi to New York nonstop, as flight KQ2, on a Boeing 787. Feed a Lusaka-Nairobi connection into that service and the dog lands at JFK, itself a CDC-registered facility airport, without a second domestic hop.

        Ethiopian Airlines runs a roughly daily Lusaka-Addis Ababa sector, connecting onward to Washington Dulles on flight ET501, an Airbus A350. Dulles, too, is on the CDC's six-airport list. And Emirates already flies Lusaka to Dubai nonstop as EK713/EK714, an established fact on this site's Zambia-UAE route, then runs its own nonstop Dubai services into JFK, LAX, Miami and Dulles, meaning the whole itinerary can stay on one carrier rather than switching hands mid-journey.

    - heading: "The gap between what a dog and a cat need here"
      body: |
        This is worth spelling out because it surprises a lot of owners moving more than one pet. A dog needs the full high-risk pathway: chip, vaccine, titre, facility reservation. A cat needs none of that federally. No CDC rabies vaccination requirement, no titre test, no facility booking.

        What doesn't change is Zambia's own side of the paperwork. The Department of Veterinary Services in Lusaka handles export documentation for both species identically, and most airlines still want a general veterinary health certificate regardless of what CDC asks for. A lighter federal rule for cats isn't the same as no paperwork at all, just noticeably less of it.
