content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from the United Kingdom to Montenegro"
  overview: |
    Podgorica gets around 18 direct Wizz Air flights from London every week. None of them will carry a dog or cat. That is the first thing worth knowing about this route, and it catches out more owners than the paperwork does.

    The paperwork itself is heavier than a UK owner might expect from a European neighbour. Montenegro sits on the EU's approved third-country list as a place UK pets can leave from without a titre test. The reverse does not appear to hold. Guidance specific to Montenegro describes a titre test requirement with no country exemption named anywhere, the UK included, plus a wait running to more than three calendar months after a passing result.

    That is a different shape to Albania or North Macedonia, two other EU-candidate states covered on this site, both of which extend the same exemption to UK pets that the UK extends to them. Whether that gap is a permanent feature of Montenegro's own rule or a documentation gap is genuinely hard to pin down from public sources, which is itself worth knowing before booking anything.

  sections:
    - heading: "A titre test that doesn't appear to discriminate by origin"
      body: |
        Most of the Western Balkan candidate states handle rabies serology the same way Brussels does. A pet arriving from a country on the EU's Annex II list, the same list that lets a UK pet leave for the EU without a blood test, gets the same courtesy back. North Macedonia works this way. Albania works this way.

        Montenegro, on the guidance we were able to find, does not. The requirement reads as a flat rule: FAVN or RNATT test, drawn at least 30 days after the primary rabies vaccination, then a wait of more than three calendar months from a passing result before the pet enters the country. No carve-out for the EU, the UK or the US turned up in any of the sources checked for this page.

        That inconsistency between sources is itself the honest finding here. Montenegro's own AFSVPA is the authority to check with directly rather than assuming a rule seen elsewhere in the region applies. Build the three-month wait into planning regardless, since it costs nothing to have the paperwork ready early and a great deal to find out too late that it was needed.

    - heading: "Flying out: the direct flight that isn't an option"
      body: |
        Wizz Air's London-Podgorica route is fast, cheap and frequent, and it is also a dead end for a pet owner. The airline carries no animals in the cabin or the hold on any of its routes, with the exception of certified assistance dogs.

        Lufthansa, via Frankfurt or Munich, keeps the pet on a single carrier's network for the whole trip and uses Frankfurt's dedicated Animal Lounge for the transfer. Austrian Airlines connects through Vienna instead, at a EUR 150 surcharge, though it will not carry a flat-faced dog or cat in the hold under any circumstances. Turkish Airlines' daily Istanbul-Podgorica service looks tempting on paper but excludes any UK-connected pet booking outright.

        Book whichever of Lufthansa or Austrian Airlines suits the crate size well before the three-month titre wait ends. Live animal cargo capacity on both routes is limited, and a late booking risks missing the flight the wait was built around.
