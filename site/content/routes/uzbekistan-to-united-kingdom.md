  overview: |
    Tashkent's veterinary bureaucracy changed hands in 2023, and it still trips up owners searching for the old State Committee for Veterinary and Livestock Development. That body was folded into the Ministry of Agriculture; the export certificate for a pet dog or cat now comes from the territorial veterinary service, processed through the Committee's newer electronic system, with final sign-off at the border checkpoint at Tashkent International Airport.

    None of that is what actually sets the clock on this route, though. The UK's own titre test sequence does that: Uzbekistan sits outside GB's list of rabies-listed countries, so a FAVN test and a fixed 90-day wait from the blood draw apply regardless of how quickly the Uzbek paperwork moves. Realistically this route runs 18 to 24 weeks.

  sections:
    - heading: "A veterinary authority that changed its name in 2023"
      body: |
        For years, Uzbekistan's animal export process ran through the State Committee for Veterinary and Livestock Development, and that name still shows up on older pet transport guides and forum posts. Since 1 January 2023 it's the Committee of Veterinary and Livestock Development, sitting under the Ministry of Agriculture, with a current legal basis in Cabinet of Ministers Resolution No. 148 (April 2023). The working portal is vetgov.uz.

        For someone shipping a single pet, the practical part hasn't changed much: apply to your regional (territorial) veterinary service, and moving up to two non-productive animals means you skip the extra Chief State Veterinary Inspector sign-off that commercial shipments need. The certificate itself gets finalised at the border checkpoint, which for air travel means Tashkent International Airport.

    - heading: "Why the UK titre test dominates the timeline"
      body: |
        Uzbekistan doesn't appear on GB's list of rabies-listed countries, so the standard unlisted-country sequence applies in full: microchip before vaccination, a 30-day gap before the titre blood draw, a result of at least 0.5 IU/ml, then a 90-day wait counted from the draw date itself, not the day the lab confirms the result.

        Few, if any, APHA-approved laboratories operate inside Uzbekistan, so plan for the blood sample to travel abroad for testing. That adds real time on top of the standard lab turnaround, and a failed or degraded sample means restarting the 30-day wait from scratch.
