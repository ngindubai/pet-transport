  h1: "Pet Transport from United Arab Emirates to Eswatini"
  overview: |
    Four flights a day. That's how often Emirates flies Dubai to Johannesburg since a March 2025 schedule expansion, a frequency neither the UK nor the US route on this site can match with a single daily long-haul option each. For an owner trying to line up a cargo booking with a tight vaccination or permit date, that extra scheduling room is worth more than it sounds.

    Eswatini's own rules stay light regardless of which frequency gets a pet there: a vaccination record, a microchip, and a Veterinary Import Permit from the Director of Animal Health, valid for six months once granted. No titre test features anywhere in the process.

    What doesn't change is the last leg. King Mswati III International Airport has no long-haul service of its own, so every pet, UAE-origin or otherwise, finishes the trip on an Airlink codeshare hop from Johannesburg.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why frequency matters more than it looks like it should"
      body: |
        A cargo booking has to line up with a specific certificate date, a specific vet appointment and, often, a specific import permit's validity window. A route with four daily flights gives an owner or agent far more room to adjust than a route with one flight a day, since a missed connection or a delayed certificate doesn't necessarily cost a full extra week.

        Emirates' Dubai-Johannesburg schedule sits at four flights daily as of March 2025, entirely through Emirates SkyCargo since the airline carries no pets in its passenger cabin on any route. Etihad's Abu Dhabi-Johannesburg service is the practical alternative if an owner is based nearer Abu Dhabi, running through Etihad Cargo's LiveAnimals programme on broadly similar terms.

    - heading: "The Johannesburg handoff, and what Eswatini actually checks"
      body: |
        Airlink covers the short Johannesburg-Manzini sector under its codeshare arrangement with Emirates, several times a day, into King Mswati III International Airport. The two legs sit on separate bookings, so a ground agent used to managing this specific handover at O.R. Tambo is worth the fee over trying to coordinate it independently.

        On arrival, Eswatini's Director of Animal Health checks the Veterinary Import Permit, the MOCCAE-issued health certificate and the microchip against the animal. None of that involves a blood test result; the permit, not a laboratory report, is the one document worth applying for early, since its six-month validity gives real flexibility if the travel date shifts.
