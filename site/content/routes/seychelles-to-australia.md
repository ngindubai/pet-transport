  h1: "Pet Transport from Seychelles to Australia: 2026 Guide"
  overview: |
    It's a genuine surprise to most owners: Seychelles is internationally recognised as rabies-free, yet DAFF still classifies it as a Group 3 country, the same tier that covers most of Europe and the US. That means the full titre test and quarantine sequence applies, not the shortcut a truly rabies-free classification would suggest.

    An RNATT test, a 180-day wait from lab receipt, and 10 to 30 days at the Mickleham facility near Melbourne all still apply. This is a genuinely long route: realistically 30 to 42 weeks.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why rabies-free Seychelles still faces the Group 3 pathway"
      body: |
        DAFF's Group 1 and Group 2 tiers, the ones that skip the titre test entirely or waive it under specific conditions, are reserved for a short, specific list of countries: New Zealand, Norfolk Island, and a handful of Pacific and Indian Ocean territories with formally verified rabies-free status recognised by DAFF itself. Seychelles, despite its own rabies-free reputation, sits in Group 3 instead, alongside most of Europe, the UK, the US and Canada.

        The practical effect is that a Seychelles-origin pet faces exactly the same titre test, 180-day wait and quarantine requirements as one leaving London or New York, a genuinely counterintuitive outcome worth flagging to any owner assuming the island's own rabies status would carry through to Australia's classification of it.

    - heading: "The 10-day quarantine shortcut"
      body: |
        The default 30-day Mickleham stay can shorten to 10 days, but only through a formal identity verification completed by a competent authority in Seychelles before the RNATT blood sample is drawn, at a separate appointment from the blood draw itself. This is worth arranging deliberately rather than assuming it happens automatically alongside routine vet care, since getting the order wrong risks having to repeat the RNATT and restart the 180-day wait.
