  h1: "Pet Transport from North Macedonia to the United States"
  overview: |
    North Macedonia doesn't appear on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies, last confirmed current as of the 15 April 2026 update. That puts this route in the simpler of the two tiers the CDC created when it overhauled dog import rules on 1 August 2024. No serology titre test. No CDC-registered facility restriction. No quarantine.

    What this route doesn't have is a direct flight. Skopje has no scheduled service to the United States, so every pet connects through Istanbul, Frankfurt, Vienna, or London. Build that into your planning and booking timeline; it's the main variable, not the paperwork.

    Cats have it easier still. The US federal government asks almost nothing of an incoming cat, low-risk origin or not.

  sections:
    - heading: "What low-risk classification actually removes"
      body: |
        The CDC splits every country into two categories for dog rabies risk: high-risk and everything else. North Macedonia sits in the "everything else" group. That single classification strips out the serology titre test, the 28-day wait after vaccination, and the requirement to land at one of the specific US airports with a CDC-registered animal care facility.

        A CDC Dog Import Form is still mandatory for every dog regardless of origin. It's free, completed online, and valid for six months with multiple entries if your dog stays in a same-risk-category country during that window.

        Rabies vaccination isn't legally required by the CDC for a low-risk dog, but skip it and you'll likely run into trouble with your airline, your connecting country, or simple common sense. Get it done.

    - heading: "Routing through a connection: Istanbul, Frankfurt, or Vienna"
      body: |
        Skopje International Airport has no nonstop US service, so the practical question is which connection to use. Turkish Airlines through Istanbul gives you a purpose-built live animal facility and direct onward flights to several major US cities. Lufthansa through Frankfurt or Munich is the other well-established option, with its own dedicated cargo handling for pets.

        Avoid assuming a Star Alliance codeshare through Vienna on United will work for cargo. United's general pet cargo service is currently suspended, cabin pets under 8kg aside, so a crated dog booked through Vienna needs a different final-leg carrier.

        Whichever route you choose, confirm your arrival US airport before booking. Low-risk dogs can land anywhere, but your connecting carrier's schedule will usually dictate the choice for you.

faqs:
  - question: "Does my dog need a rabies titre test to enter the United States from North Macedonia?"
    answer: "No. The titre test only applies to dogs from countries on the CDC's high-risk list, and North Macedonia isn't on it. Your dog still needs a microchip and, in practice, a current rabies vaccination, even though the CDC doesn't legally require the vaccination for low-risk-origin dogs."
  - question: "Is there a direct flight from Skopje to the United States?"
    answer: "No. There's no scheduled nonstop service between Skopje and any US city. Every pet travels via a connection, most commonly through Istanbul, Frankfurt, Munich, or London, on a carrier with an established live animal cargo programme."
  - question: "Can I book my pet on United Airlines through Vienna?"
    answer: "Not as cargo. United Airlines currently accepts only cabin pets under 8kg and has suspended its general cargo pet service. If your pet needs to travel as cargo, book with Turkish Airlines, Lufthansa, or another carrier with an active cargo pet programme instead."
  - question: "Which US airport does my pet need to arrive at?"
    answer: "Any of them. Because North Macedonia is a low-risk country, your dog can arrive at any US airport, seaport, or land border crossing. There's no requirement to land at a specific CDC-registered facility, unlike the rule for high-risk-country dogs."
  - question: "What does my cat need to enter the United States?"
    answer: "Very little at the federal level. The CDC has almost no import requirements for cats regardless of origin country. Check your arrival state's own animal import rules, since some states add their own conditions that the federal government doesn't cover."
