content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Ukraine to the United Kingdom"
  overview: |
    Ukraine's airports have had no scheduled commercial flights since Russian forces invaded in February 2022. That has not changed as this guide is written in mid-2026: EASA's restriction on Ukrainian airspace remains in force to at least the end of July 2026, and while Kyiv has formed a working group to plan a phased reopening, no airline is currently flying passengers or cargo into or out of the country. For a pet, that means the flight is the last leg of the journey, not the first.

    The realistic route runs overland first. Most owners in western Ukraine cross into Poland near Lviv, at Shehyni-Medyka or Krakovets-Korczowa, then complete the UK's titre test and documentation requirements from Polish soil before flying on to Great Britain. It's a longer process than a standard route, but it is a well-worn one: thousands of Ukrainian pets have made this same crossing since 2022.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why there's no direct flight, and what replaces it"
      body: |
        Ukraine's civilian airspace closed on 24 February 2022 and has stayed closed. EASA's Conflict Zone Information Bulletin covering Ukraine remains active, currently extended to at least 31 July 2026, and airlines including AirBaltic have said they are ready to resume flights once it lifts, but no date has been set. Most estimates put a full civilian reopening well beyond the near term.

        In practice, this means booking a flight out of Kyiv, Lviv or any other Ukrainian airport is not an option for a pet move right now. Owners drive or arrange transport to a Polish border crossing, most often near Lviv given its proximity to the frontier, then continue by road or a short domestic connection to an airport with international departures: Rzeszow in Poland sits roughly 90 minutes from the Krakovets crossing and has become a common staging point, with Krakow and Warsaw as the larger alternatives. Romania offers a similar path via its own border crossings for owners in southern or central Ukraine.

    - heading: "GB entry rules: the titre test and the 3-month wait"
      body: |
        The UK's Animal and Plant Health Agency treats Ukraine as an unlisted country, the same category that applies to most countries outside the EU and a short list of approved third countries. That means the full rabies protocol: a microchip fitted before vaccination, a titre test blood draw at least 30 days after that vaccination, a result of at least 0.5 IU/ml, and then a fixed 3-month wait counted from the day the blood was drawn, not the day the lab confirms the result. Get that date wrong and the whole wait effectively restarts.

        Dogs also need tapeworm treatment for Echinococcus multilocularis, given by a vet between 24 and 120 hours before arrival in Great Britain and recorded on the certificate. Cats don't need it. None of this can be completed inside Ukraine once the titre wait has started, since the Pet Health Certificate and tapeworm treatment window are timed to arrival, so most owners complete the final certificate step with a vet in Poland shortly before the flight.

    - heading: "The Ukraine-specific licence route, for pets who can't meet the standard timeline"
      body: |
        GOV.UK publishes separate guidance for people bringing pets from Ukraine, aimed at those arriving under the Homes for Ukraine, Ukraine Family Scheme or Ukraine Permission Extension routes, which the UK government confirmed remain open with an extended application window from April 2026. The guidance itself doesn't relax the science: a pet still needs the microchip, vaccination, titre test and 3-month wait to enter without quarantine.

        What it adds is a fallback for people who left in a hurry and couldn't complete that timeline before travelling. Those owners can apply for a GB import licence, covering up to 5 personal pets under one application, and may be offered home isolation instead of a commercial quarantine facility while the pet completes its wait in Great Britain. It is a genuine difference from how an unlisted-country pet without correct paperwork is normally handled, and worth knowing about if your circumstances mean the standard timeline simply wasn't possible.
