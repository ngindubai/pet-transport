  h1: "Pet Transport from the Bahamas to Canada"
  overview: |
    Sixty-eight flights a week connected Toronto and Nassau as of May 2026, spread across Air Canada, WestJet and Porter. That density puts this route in a different category from most Caribbean-to-Canada moves on this site, which usually connect through a US hub first. A dog or cat can fly Nassau to Pearson without ever touching American soil.

    The paperwork matches the flight's simplicity. The Bahamas doesn't appear on the Canadian Food Inspection Agency's list of countries it treats as high-risk for dog rabies, so none of the age rules, import permits or designated-airport restrictions that apply to a high-risk-country dog apply here.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why this route skips CFIA's toughest rules entirely"
      body: |
        CFIA reserves its strictest personal-pet controls, a 7-month minimum age, a mandatory import permit and entry limited to a handful of designated airports, for dogs coming from countries on its high-risk list for dog rabies. That list, last reviewed in March 2025, runs across Africa, parts of Asia, the Middle East and Eastern Europe, plus a shorter run of Central and South American and Caribbean nations. The Bahamas isn't on it.

        What's left for a Bahamian dog is close to the minimum CFIA asks of anyone: a rabies vaccination certificate, current within 3 years, written in English or French by a licensed vet. Cats need even less, since CFIA's federal rules barely touch them at all.

    - heading: "Three airlines, one short flight"
      body: |
        Air Canada carries the bulk of Nassau-Toronto traffic, with cabin space for a small pet and Air Canada Cargo handling anything larger. WestJet adds four nonstop services a week on the same route, and Porter Airlines has more recently added its own Pearson-Nassau service, though Porter's pet policy across its network stops at the cabin door.

        A roughly three-and-a-quarter-hour flight is short enough that the travel day itself rarely causes problems. The harder question on this route is choosing the right airline for a bigger dog, since only two of the three carriers actually offer a cargo option.

    - heading: "What BAHFSA still hasn't published"
      body: |
        The gap on this route sits entirely on the Bahamian side, not the Canadian one. BAHFSA's Trade Facilitation Unit runs a detailed, well-documented ePermit system for pets arriving into the Bahamas, but its guidance for pets leaving Nassau is thinner. Whether a Canada-bound certificate needs a government endorsement stamp on top of the vet's signature isn't spelled out publicly in the same way. A direct call before booking closes that gap.
