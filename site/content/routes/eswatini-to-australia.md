content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Eswatini to Australia"
  overview: |
    King Mswati III International Airport has no long-haul carrier of its own, so every pet leaving Eswatini flies to Johannesburg first, no matter where it is ultimately headed. For an Australia-bound move, that fact turns out to be useful rather than just unavoidable, because South Africa is itself a DAFF Group 3 approved country. The transit hub and the required 180-day staging country are the same place.

    That coincidence saves a detour, not the wait. Eswatini is non-approved under DAFF's rules, so the 180 consecutive days of residency still has to happen before any Australian paperwork opens, and Mickleham's quarantine sits at the end regardless of where the pet staged. Thirty-two to thirty-eight weeks is a realistic floor for this route, not a worst case.

    Compare it with the same pet's UAE route, covered separately on this site: no residency requirement there at all, just a titre test. Two different governments, two very different verdicts on the same country's rabies risk.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why South Africa is not really a choice"
      body: |
        Most non-approved-country routes on this site involve an owner deciding between two or three plausible staging countries, usually the UK, USA or Singapore, each requiring its own dedicated long-haul flight from the pet's actual starting point. Eswatini does not offer that choice in the same way. Every regional and international connection out of King Mswati III International Airport goes through Johannesburg, on Airlink or Eswatini Air, and South Africa happens to already hold DAFF Group 3 status.

        That means the 180-day clock can start on the very first flight, with no separate detour needed to reach an approved country. It is a genuine advantage this route has over routes originating in countries with better long-haul connectivity of their own.

    - heading: "The titre test, and the quarantine trade-off"
      body: |
        South Africa's Group 3 status still requires an RNATT titre test, blood drawn at least 30 days after vaccination and sent to an accredited laboratory, in practice usually Onderstepoort Veterinary Institute in Pretoria. The sample needs to arrive at the lab somewhere between 180 days and a year before the Australia-bound flight.

        The detail worth getting right early: Mickleham's quarantine period only drops to its 10-day minimum if the pet's microchip is officially verified before that RNATT blood draw, and that verification itself happens at least 180 days before export. Book the verification appointment first, deliberately, rather than treating it as a formality that can wait.

    - heading: "From Johannesburg to Melbourne"
      body: |
        Both Qantas and South African Airways fly Johannesburg to Perth direct, Qantas three times a week and South African Airways around five, giving reasonable flexibility for landing on a weekday, since Mickleham does not take weekend arrivals. Whichever pet's final Australian city, the quarantine stop is always Melbourne. There is no other DAFF-approved facility.

    - heading: "A note for flat-faced breeds"
      body: |
        Mickleham runs hottest from December to February, and DAFF issues its own heat stress warnings for brachycephalic dogs during that window. A pet finishing its 180-day South African residency around the southern summer is worth timing carefully; a May-to-September arrival is the lower-risk pick for a flat-faced breed, regardless of when the paperwork happens to be ready.
