  sections:
    - heading: "Why driving is the most practical option on this corridor"
      body: |
        Slovenia borders Italy to the east, and Ljubljana is remarkably close to the Italian northeast. From Trieste to Ljubljana is about 100km via the A1 motorway; the drive takes under 1.5 hours. From Venice, allow roughly 2 hours. From Udine, it is under an hour.

        Both Italy and Slovenia are EU Schengen members, which means no border checks, no customs stop, and no requirement to present documents at the crossing. Your EU pet passport is the document for Slovenia entry, but nobody stops you at the border to check it. In practice, the crossing is like driving between any two EU countries.

        For large dogs that would need cargo transport by air, this is particularly compelling. A large Labrador or German Shepherd that does not travel well in an aircraft cargo hold may be completely comfortable in a car for 2 hours with rest stops. The cost is a tank of fuel.

    - heading: "The air options for northern or southern Italy"
      body: |
        For owners in Rome, Florence, or southern Italy, the drive to Slovenia is less practical at 5-8 hours. The air option is more sensible.

        Austrian Airlines operates from Rome Fiumicino (FCO) to Vienna (VIE), with an onward VIE-LJU leg. This is a two-leg journey, but Austrian Airlines is a well-organised carrier that accepts cabin pets under 8kg and cargo pets on its European routes.

        An alternative for small cabin pets is Ryanair, which operates seasonal Rome Ciampino (CIA) to Ljubljana services in summer. The cabin-only restriction limits this to cats and small dogs, but for the right animal it is a direct 1.5-hour flight.
