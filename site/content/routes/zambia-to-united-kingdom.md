  h1: "Pet Transport from Zambia to the United Kingdom"
  overview: |
    No airline flies Lusaka's Kenneth Kaunda International nonstop to Heathrow. Every option adds at least one stop, most workably through Addis Ababa on Ethiopian Airlines or Nairobi on Kenya Airways, and that routing choice ends up shaping the whole move as much as the paperwork does.

    The paperwork itself follows the same pattern as most of Sub-Saharan Africa on this route: Zambia sits outside both of Great Britain's listed-country tables, so a rabies titre test and a fixed 3-month wait replace what would otherwise be a simple post-vaccination wait. The Zambian side, by contrast, runs on direct correspondence with the Department of Veterinary Services in Lusaka rather than an online export portal.

  sections:
    - heading: "Unlisted status drives the timeline"
      body: |
        Once the microchip and vaccination are in the correct order, the titre test blood draw has to happen at least 30 days later. Whatever the result, Great Britain then requires a further 3 calendar months before the pet can travel, counted from the day blood was drawn. The CDC's own high-risk list for dog rabies, updated 15 April 2026, names Zambia directly, which lines up with why GB treats it as unlisted rather than as an exception.

        Add a realistic timeline from a standing start (microchip, vaccination, 30-day wait, blood draw, lab and courier turnaround, 3-month wait, final paperwork) and 16 to 24 weeks is typical. Pets that already carry a valid, correctly-timed rabies vaccination can sometimes shave a few weeks off that.

    - heading: "Sending blood overseas for the titre test"
      body: |
        This is the detail that catches owners out. No government-approved rabies serology laboratory has been confirmed operating within Zambia, so the sample usually needs to travel, commonly to an approved facility in the UK or South Africa, before results come back. That adds transit time on both legs, not just laboratory processing.

        Plan the blood draw with that in mind. A test booked without allowing for courier delays can quietly add two to three weeks to a schedule that already runs to several months.

    - heading: "Two workable routes to Heathrow"
      body: |
        Ethiopian Airlines connects Lusaka to Addis Ababa and onward to Heathrow, with 1-2 flights daily on the Addis-London leg; small pets travel in the cabin in an approved container, larger animals as cargo, though snub-nosed breeds aren't accepted on any Ethiopian flight. Kenya Airways runs the alternative via Nairobi, feeding into KQ100, its nonstop Nairobi-Heathrow service, but only as cargo; Kenya Airways doesn't carry pets in the cabin or as checked baggage.

        British Airways has no direct Lusaka link at all. IAG Cargo picks up shipments at Heathrow from connecting gateways, and a Zambia-origin booking will usually need an IPATA- or ATA-registered agent to manage that handover. South African Airways' historical routing via Johannesburg is worth a look too, schedule permitting, feeding onto British Airways or Virgin Atlantic for the final leg.

    - heading: "What it costs, roughly"
      body: |
        Zambian vet fees, microchipping and vaccination typically run USD 60-150; no published government tariff exists, so confirm locally rather than assume a fixed number. The titre test with overseas courier adds another USD 150-300.

        The Department of Veterinary Services doesn't publish an export fee schedule either, which means budgeting has to lean on direct contact with Mulungushi House rather than a price list. The GB Pet Health Certificate runs GBP 150-300, and air cargo from Lusaka to Heathrow, including an IPATA agent's handling fee for the interline transfer, generally lands between USD 1,800 and 4,000 depending on crate size, season and routing.

Source: GOV.UK, 'Bringing your pet dog, cat or ferret to Great Britain,' 2026."

    answer: "Yes. Because Zambia is unlisted, the titre test (RNATT/FAVN) is mandatory, taken no sooner than 30 days after the rabies vaccination and showing a result of at least 0.5 IU/ml. As Zambia has no confirmed approved laboratory of its own, the sample is usually sent overseas, which adds transit time. Source: GOV.UK, 2026."
