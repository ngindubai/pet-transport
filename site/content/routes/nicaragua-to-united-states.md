content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Nicaragua to United States"
  overview: |
    Nicaragua does not appear on the CDC's list of 161 countries and political units treated as high risk for dog rabies, current as of the agency's 15 April 2026 update. That absence matters more than it sounds. It means no FAVN titre test, no CDC-approved laboratory, and no restriction to a CDC-registered animal care facility airport, the three things that turn a pet move into a five- or six-month process on plenty of other routes into the US.

    What is left is comparatively light: Nicaragua's own IPSA export paperwork, a free CDC Dog Import Form, and a flight. American Airlines flies Managua to Miami non-stop every day, and Avianca has recently added a non-stop Fort Lauderdale service too, so this is one of the few Central American routes where a single-leg option genuinely exists.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why the CDC's high-risk list is the whole story here"
      body: |
        The CDC splits the world into two groups for dog imports: high-risk and everything else. A dog arriving from a high-risk country needs a CDC Dog Import Form, a rabies vaccination certificate, a FAVN titre test result of at least 0.5 IU/mL from a CDC-registered laboratory, and it can only land at a CDC-registered animal care facility airport. Nicaragua sits outside that list.

        A dog from Nicaragua still needs the CDC Dog Import Form. Every dog does, since 1 August 2024, when the CDC simplified the rules for low-risk arrivals. What it does not need is the titre test, the laboratory step, or the airport restriction. Practically, that is the difference between a wait measured in weeks rather than months.

        Cats sit outside the CDC's dog-specific framework entirely. There is no federal microchip or vaccination requirement for a cat entering the US. That does not mean skip the paperwork, since the Nicaraguan export side and most airlines still expect a proper health certificate.

    - heading: "What IPSA asks for before departure"
      body: |
        IPSA's general requirements for a dog or cat leaving Nicaragua are shorter than they look. A health exam within 2 weeks of departure, a rabies vaccination no more than 365 days old, and parasite treatment with an approved product. There is no separately published pet-specific export fee schedule, so IPSA's Ventanilla Unica desk is worth a direct call before you book anything, particularly to confirm which vets currently hold the endorsement authority.

    - heading: "Getting from Managua to a US gateway"
      body: |
        American Airlines' daily non-stop from Managua to Miami is the most established option, and its PetEmbark cargo programme is open to any member of the public, not restricted to military bookings as some older guides suggest. Avianca's newer non-stop to Fort Lauderdale is a genuine alternative, though a route this recent can still see schedule changes in its first year, so confirm current frequency before locking in a date.

        Copa Airlines, via its Panama City hub, is the third option and the one with the tightest scheduling constraint: Copa Pets only accepts cargo bookings Monday to Thursday. If your travel plans land on a weekend, Copa's cargo hold simply is not available that day, regardless of what the passenger side of the booking shows.
