content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Guatemala to Canada"
  overview: |
    Seven months. That's the minimum age a dog needs to be, on the actual date of arrival, before Canada will let it in from Guatemala as a personal pet. It's the kind of detail that catches out owners moving with a puppy, and it exists because Guatemala sits on CFIA's list of countries at high risk for dog rabies.

    That listing sounds worse than it plays out in practice. The restriction CFIA introduced in 2022 targets commercial dog imports, rescues, sales, breeding stock, not a family's own dog travelling with them. A personal pet still needs a CFIA import permit, the microchip-before-vaccination sequence, and arrival at one of five designated airports, but it is not banned.

  sections:
    - heading: "Commercial ban, personal exemption: how the distinction actually works"
      body: |
        CFIA's 2022 measure closed the door on commercial dog imports from more than 100 high-risk countries, Guatemala among them. Read the headlines about that ban and it's easy to assume every Guatemalan dog is shut out. It isn't true for a personal pet travelling with its owner.

        What does apply is a longer list of specific conditions: the dog has to be at least 7 months old at entry, the ISO microchip has to go in before or on the same day as the rabies vaccination, and a CFIA import permit, endorsed by a government veterinarian, has to be secured ahead of travel. None of that is a ban. It is a checklist, and missing any one item is what actually causes a hold at the border.

    - heading: "Five airports, one document set"
      body: |
        A high-risk-country dog can only land at Toronto Pearson, Montreal Trudeau, Vancouver International, Calgary International or Halifax Stanfield, the five airports with the animal inspection facilities CFIA requires for this pathway. Avianca's connecting network reaches Toronto reliably; Air Canada runs a limited seasonal direct service worth checking for cargo acceptance rather than assuming.

        Cats face almost none of this. Canada's federal rules for cats stay minimal whatever the country of origin, so a family moving with both a cat and a dog should expect two very different processes running side by side.

    - heading: "Timing the microchip and vaccination in the right order"
      body: |
        Get the sequence wrong here and the whole certificate needs redoing. CFIA's rule is specific: the microchip has to be implanted before, or on the same day as, the rabies vaccination, not after. A dog vaccinated first and chipped later effectively has an undocumented vaccination as far as CFIA is concerned, and the shot has to be repeated once the chip is finally in.

        This is a one-line instruction to give your Guatemalan vet clinic before the first appointment, and it costs nothing to get right the first time. Getting it wrong costs a repeat vaccination and, depending on timing, a delayed flight.

    - heading: "Why the screwworm question is still open for this route"
      body: |
        The US requires a pre-shipment screwworm inspection certificate for dogs leaving Guatemala, a direct response to New World Screwworm's re-emergence across Central America. CFIA has introduced its own screwworm-related restrictions in 2026, but so far these have targeted livestock movements from parts of North America, not a documented companion-animal certificate for Guatemala-origin dogs.

        That gap in the public record isn't the same as confirmation there's nothing to worry about. Ask CFIA or your agent directly, close to your travel date, whether anything has changed, given how quickly this situation has moved through 2026.
