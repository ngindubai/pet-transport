  overview: |
    One flight number covers the whole journey from Dubai to Antananarivo. Emirates' EK707 makes a scheduled stop in the Seychelles on the way, but the aircraft, the crew and the cargo hold stay the same throughout, a materially different shape from the UK and US routes on this site, both of which need an actual change of carrier partway through the trip.

    That single flight runs four times a week on a Boeing 777-300ER, with 22 tonnes of weekly bellyhold cargo capacity, more than enough room for a pet shipment alongside Emirates SkyCargo's other freight on the sector.

    Madagascar's own entry rules ask for no titre test, despite the country's genuine reputation for biosecurity caution around its unique wildlife. What actually sets the calendar is a letter, not a form: an import permit request sent to Madagascar's Direction des Services Veterinaires, with no published processing time. Budget twelve to sixteen weeks, most of it waiting on that letter.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why the Seychelles stop doesn't count as a connection"
      body: |
        A technical stop and a connection sound similar but work very differently for a live animal in transit. Emirates' Dubai-Antananarivo service keeps the same flight number and aircraft through its Seychelles stop, which means the pet's crate stays loaded on one aircraft the entire way rather than being unloaded, transferred airside and reloaded onto a different carrier's plane, the way a genuine hub connection through Nairobi or Addis Ababa would require.

        Four flights a week is a real limit on scheduling flexibility, so this isn't a route to book at the last minute. Kenya Airways via Nairobi and Ethiopian Airlines via Addis Ababa both remain workable if the Emirates schedule doesn't fit a specific travel window, at the cost of a genuine carrier change partway through.

    - heading: "A letter with no deadline, not a titre test, sets the timeline"
      body: |
        Madagascar's isolation and its unique wildlife might suggest a tougher entry regime for pets than most mainland African destinations. That's not really how it plays out for a companion dog or cat: no titre test applies from any country of origin, the UAE included, and the country's genuine biosecurity caution is channelled through a separate CITES process aimed at wildlife trade, not family pets.

        What does take real time is the DSV import permit, a letter of request rather than an online form, covering the owner's and animal's details, a copy of the vaccination record, and the arrival flight information. No fixed turnaround is published, and guidance consistently describes it as taking several weeks. Send it twelve to fourteen weeks before the planned travel date and confirm the current DSV contact details directly rather than relying on an older listing.
