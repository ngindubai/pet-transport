content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Mozambique to Canada"
  overview: |
    LAM Mozambique Airlines, the country's own national carrier, doesn't fly anywhere near Canada. It handles domestic and regional African routes only, which means every pet leaving Mozambique for Canada changes to a different airline at Maputo, whatever the rest of the journey looks like.

    Ethiopian Airlines and TAP Air Portugal are the two carriers that actually get the job done, feeding into Addis Ababa or Lisbon respectively before a long-haul sector into Toronto. Mozambique also sits on CFIA's high-risk-for-dog-rabies list, current as of 13 March 2025, though for an ordinary family pet that classification changes very little: its real force is a ban on commercial dog imports, in place since 2022.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Maputo is a changeover, not a starting point for the long haul"
      body: |
        LAM Mozambique Airlines serves the domestic network and regional African destinations, nothing further. That rules it out entirely for the transatlantic leg, so every plan for this route needs a second carrier from the outset. Ethiopian Airlines' Maputo-Addis Ababa connection feeds its own nonstop Addis Ababa-Toronto service, the only direct passenger route between continental Africa and Canada. TAP Air Portugal's nonstop Maputo-Lisbon sector, at roughly 11 hours, connects to Air Canada's own nonstop Lisbon service instead.

        Either works. The choice usually comes down to schedule and crate limits, since TAP's hold weight allowance can run tighter on some sectors than Ethiopian's.

    - heading: "The high-risk listing, and what it doesn't do"
      body: |
        CFIA's list, updated 13 March 2025, groups Mozambique with several dozen other countries. For a commercial dog shipment, that listing is a hard stop, banned since 28 September 2022. For a personal pet, travelling with the owner named on its papers, the process stays ordinary: current vaccination, correctly built certificate, no serology test, no quarantine.

        The detail worth checking regardless is CFIA's 8-month age presumption. A younger dog not accompanied by its named owner risks being treated as commercial, a genuinely serious outcome from a high-risk country. Confirm directly with CFIA if that could apply.
