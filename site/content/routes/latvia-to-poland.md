  sections:
    - heading: "Riga to Warsaw: choosing your airline"
      body: |
        Two carriers are worth comparing for this route. airBaltic, based in Riga, operates direct flights to Warsaw Chopin (WAW) and accepts small pets (up to 8kg including carrier) in the cabin. LOT Polish Airlines also serves Riga to Warsaw and has a similarly pet-friendly cabin policy for small animals.

        For larger dogs travelling in the hold, Lufthansa Cargo via Frankfurt is a reliable option. The hold on commercial aircraft is pressurised and temperature-controlled; direct routes are preferable to minimise transit time for animals.

        Warsaw Chopin (WAW) is the main entry point. From there, domestic Polish connections serve Krakow, Gdansk, Wroclaw, and other cities. For destinations in eastern Poland, driving from Riga via Lithuania is a viable alternative.

    - heading: "Poland's veterinary entry rules"
      body: |
        Poland's Chief Veterinary Inspectorate (GIW) applies the standard EU framework for incoming pets. Your dog, cat, or ferret needs an ISO 11784/11785 microchip, a valid rabies vaccination with the 21-day first-vaccination wait completed, and an EU pet passport or AHC.

        The AHC must be endorsed within 10 days of crossing the Polish border. If you are driving, the clock starts at the border crossing; if flying, it starts on the date of entry via the airport.

        There is no quarantine for compliant pets. Poland does not require a titre test for EU-origin animals.

    - heading: "What to know about dogs in public in Poland"
      body: |
        Poland has no national breed ban, but the Polish Animal Protection Act and related regulations do require muzzles and leads for certain breeds in public spaces. The list includes American Pit Bull Terriers, Rottweilers, and other powerful breeds. These rules govern how you manage your dog after arrival; they are not entry conditions.

        In practice, responsible handling is expected for all large dogs in Polish cities, with or without a legal requirement. Parks, public transport, and shared residential spaces may have additional local rules.

        For cats, there are no breed restrictions and no specific public-space rules beyond standard animal welfare obligations.
