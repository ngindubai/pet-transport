content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Grenada to the United Kingdom"
  overview: |
    Seven of Grenada's Caribbean neighbours sit on the UK's Part 2 list, exempt from the rabies titre test: Antigua and Barbuda, Barbados, Jamaica, Saint Kitts and Nevis, Saint Lucia, Saint Vincent and the Grenadines, and Trinidad and Tobago. Grenada itself doesn't appear on that list, or on Part 1 either. It's an easy assumption to get wrong, that a Windward Islands nation with a low rabies risk would automatically qualify. It doesn't, not on paper.

    That single gap changes the whole shape of the move. A dog or cat leaving Grenada for the UK needs the full unlisted-country pathway: microchip before vaccine, a titre test at an APHA-approved lab, and a 90-day wait once the result comes back clean. Budget four to five months, and expect the titre clock, not the flight booking, to decide the date.

    On the transport side there's really one option. British Airways is the only carrier connecting Maurice Bishop International with the UK, and even that routing isn't a straight shot; recent schedules show a technical stop in the Eastern Caribbean before the aircraft continues to Gatwick.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Grenada's neighbours get an easier ride"
      body: |
        Look at a map of the UK's Part 2 list and the Eastern Caribbean is well represented. Barbados, Jamaica, Trinidad and Tobago, Saint Lucia, Saint Kitts and Nevis, Saint Vincent and the Grenadines, Antigua and Barbuda: all listed, all exempt from the rabies titre requirement. Grenada sits in the same regional rabies-control picture as those countries but isn't on the list itself, which means a pet leaving Grenada faces exactly the pathway an owner might assume only applies to countries with a genuinely higher rabies risk.

        This matters for timing more than anything else. A titre test result has to come back clean, and then 90 days have to pass before the flight, full stop. There's no way to compress that window by paying more or moving faster on the rest of the paperwork.

    - heading: "Getting the Grenada side right"
      body: |
        The Veterinary and Livestock Division at the Ministry of Agriculture, based at the Ministerial Complex in St George's, handles both the outbound documentation and the microchip-and-vaccination sequence that has to precede it. There's no separate export permit to apply for; the health certificate itself does that job.

        Where it gets more exacting is sequencing. The microchip has to go in before the rabies vaccine, or the UK won't recognise the vaccination at all. Get that order wrong and the entire 30-day-plus-90-day clock resets from scratch.

    - heading: "One airline, one routing"
      body: |
        British Airways flies Gatwick to Grenada as BA2159, and back as BA2158, a Boeing 777 service that typically runs ten to eleven hours including a technical stop in the Eastern Caribbean, commonly St Lucia or Tobago on recent schedules. Pets travel through IAG Cargo rather than as hold baggage; British Airways carries no live animals in the main baggage hold on any route.

        Worth noting: IAG Cargo has said pet bookings originating in the Caribbean can sometimes be arranged directly, without going through a freight forwarder first, which isn't the case on every long-haul route British Airways operates. It's worth asking about when getting a quote, since it can shave a step off the booking process.
