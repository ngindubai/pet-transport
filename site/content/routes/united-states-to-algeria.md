content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from the United States to Algeria"
  overview: |
    Algeria doesn't ask a US pet for a rabies titre test on the way in. It does, unusually for this site, insist on a pre-export deworming treatment, a step APHIS states outright for this destination and that most other countries never mention at all.

    There's no direct flight linking Algiers to any US city, so every booking runs through Paris or Istanbul. Combine that with a French-or-Arabic paperwork requirement and inconsistent guidance on which dog breeds Algeria will actually let in, and this becomes a route where the individual steps are each fairly light, but the sequencing takes real attention.

  sections:
    - heading: "The deworming step almost nobody expects"
      body: |
        Most of the destinations covered on this site ask for a microchip, a vaccination record and a certificate, full stop. Algeria's APHIS guidance adds a line most US owners don't see coming: the pet must be dewormed before export. It isn't a footnote buried in a third-party summary, it's stated directly in the federal guidance for this specific corridor.

        Build this into the vet visit that also handles the certificate signature, rather than treating it as a separate errand. A certificate missing the deworming record is the kind of small gap that causes friction at the Algerian end that has nothing to do with the pet's actual health.

    - heading: "Why there's no titre test, and when one does matter"
      body: |
        Algeria doesn't require a rabies antibody titre test from a pet arriving from the United States, or from most other countries of origin. The vaccination record, given within the 30-day-to-12-month window, does the job on its own.

        That's easy to confuse with the return journey. Algeria sits on the CDC's own high-risk list for dog rabies, which matters if this same pet is ever brought back into the US: a titre test or a 28-day quarantine at a CDC-registered facility would then apply, under an entirely separate American framework. Going out to Algeria and coming back are two different rulebooks, and it's worth knowing which one applies before assuming the outbound experience predicts the return trip.

    - heading: "Two hubs, and a breed question worth settling early"
      body: |
        No airline flies Algiers to the US directly. Air France's Paris CDG connection is one of the busiest links into North Africa from any European hub, and Turkish Airlines via Istanbul offers a genuine second option, with a dedicated Pet Lounge easing a longer layover.

        Separately, and worth checking before either flight is booked: multiple sources describe Algeria as liable to refuse entry to dogs it considers dangerous, Pit Bull-types and Staffordshire Bull Terriers among them, though no single official banned list is published in the way some countries maintain. If a restricted or borderline breed applies, a direct written check with DSV or the Algerian consulate costs little and avoids a pet held at the airport with an otherwise perfect set of documents.
