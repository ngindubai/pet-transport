  h1: "Pet Transport from United States to Eswatini"
  overview: |
    USDA endorsement takes three to five business days and costs 38 dollars. Eswatini's own entry rules ask for less than that endorsement takes to arrange: a vaccination record, a microchip, and a permit from the Director of Animal Health. No titre test, no CDC-style facility reservation, none of it.

    That's a genuinely different shape from the reverse direction of this route, where a pet leaving Eswatini for the US runs into the CDC's high-risk protocol. None of that machinery applies here; the CDC governs entry to the United States, not entry to Eswatini. What still applies, in both directions, is the flight. Nothing lands directly at King Mswati III International Airport from anywhere in the US. Johannesburg is the gateway every time.

  sections:
    - heading: "What Eswatini checks, and what it doesn't"
      body: |
        A dog or cat arriving from the US needs a current rabies vaccination, given between 30 days and 12 months before arrival, plus the standard core vaccinations, and a Veterinary Import Permit from the Director of Animal Health. That permit exists because the US sits outside the Southern African Development Community bloc, whose members get a waiver on this step.

        What Eswatini doesn't ask for, from the US or anywhere else, is a rabies titre test. That's worth flagging precisely because it cuts against the pattern owners often expect from an African destination country. The permit is the real lead-time item here, not a blood test, and it's valid for 6 months once granted, so applying early costs nothing.

    - heading: "Two departure cities, one connection everyone makes"
      body: |
        Delta flies nonstop from Atlanta to Johannesburg, a long sector at around 16 hours. United covers the same city pair from Newark. Either works as the long-haul leg; which one suits depends more on which US city is convenient for you than on any difference in cargo policy.

        From O.R. Tambo, Airlink and Eswatini Air both run the short regional hop into King Mswati III International Airport several times a day. Book the whole journey as a single interline cargo movement rather than two separate tickets, and use a cargo agent with a Johannesburg presence to manage the handover between the long-haul and regional carriers.
