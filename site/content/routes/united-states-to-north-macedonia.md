content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from the United States to North Macedonia"
  overview: |
    Skopje has never had a scheduled flight to the United States, and current airline schedules suggest that isn't changing soon. That is the genuine obstacle on this route. The regulatory side is comparatively simple.

    North Macedonia's Food and Veterinary Agency exempts arrivals from any country the EU classifies as rabies-controlled, and the United States sits on that list, the same one that keeps a North Macedonia-origin dog off the CDC's titre requirement travelling the other way. Neither direction on this city pair needs a blood test, which is not the case for the equivalent Montenegro routes documented elsewhere on this site.

    What's left is a microchip, a current rabies vaccination, the standard 21-day wait, and a USDA-endorsed health certificate, plus a connecting flight through one of three European hubs since Skopje has no nonstop of its own.

  sections:
    - heading: "The regulatory picture: no asymmetry this time"
      body: |
        It would be easy to assume every Balkan candidate state treats US pets the same way. It doesn't. North Macedonia's FVA extends the EU's rabies-controlled exemption to US arrivals in both directions, which produces a clean, symmetrical result: no titre test coming or going.

        Montenegro, covered elsewhere on this site, appears to apply its own titre requirement without the same US carve-out, even though a Montenegro-origin dog gets the CDC's low-risk treatment entering the US. That asymmetry doesn't exist here. A US owner moving a pet to North Macedonia faces the same administrative shape as the reverse trip: paperwork, not serology.

        Rabies vaccination is still required by North Macedonia regardless of the CDC's own relaxed rule for a low-risk dog returning stateside, so keep it current on both legs of a round trip rather than treating the two directions as interchangeable.

    - heading: "Routing through Istanbul, Frankfurt or Vienna, since Skopje has no nonstop"
      body: |
        The practical question on this route isn't the paperwork, it's the connection. Turkish Airlines through Istanbul gives direct onward flights to several major US cities and a purpose-built live animal lounge for the transfer. Lufthansa through Frankfurt or Munich is the other well-established choice, with its own dedicated cargo handling.

        British Airways works through Heathrow, though every pet travels as cargo via IAG Cargo and its ground agent PetAir UK rather than in the cabin. United Airlines' Vienna-Newark service looks like a fourth option but currently carries no crated pets, cabin animals under 8kg aside, since its general cargo pet programme is suspended.

        Confirm the transit country's own rules alongside North Macedonia's before booking. A layover in the EU, the UK or Turkey brings that country's own animal handling requirements into play, on top of the checks FVA carries out on arrival at Skopje.
