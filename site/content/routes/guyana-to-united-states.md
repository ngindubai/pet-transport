  h1: "Pet Transport from Guyana to the United States"
  overview: |
    American Airlines Cargo flies Georgetown to both Miami and New York-JFK nonstop, and most owners assume that settles the question of which airline to book. It doesn't, not for a dog. American Airlines Cargo won't take a new booking for a dog originating in a country on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies, and Guyana sits on that list. JetBlue also serves the JFK route nonstop, but its pet programme stops at a small carrier under the seat, nothing that fits a crate-sized dog. That leaves Caribbean Airlines' JFK service as the one scheduled option that will actually carry a dog in the hold on this route, and it happens to land at one of the handful of US airports fitted with a CDC-registered animal care facility.

    Cats have none of these problems. No CDC rabies vaccination requirement, no titre test, no airline embargo tied to Guyana's rabies status. For a dog, the timeline runs on two connected clocks instead: a titre test that has to be drawn at least 30 days after vaccination and land at least 28 days before the flight, and a carrier choice that narrows sharply once the CDC's high-risk classification is factored in.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why American Airlines isn't the answer here, and what is"
      body: |
        It's an easy mistake to make. American's own nonstop Georgetown services look like the natural choice, and for most routes on this site they would be. Guyana's CDC classification changes that. The airline's cargo division embargoes new dog bookings from any country on the CDC's high-risk rabies list, a rule that sits on top of, not instead of, the CDC's own entry requirements. Guyana has been on that list for years, so the embargo isn't a temporary blip. It's a standing policy for this specific origin.

        Caribbean Airlines doesn't apply the same restriction, and its Georgetown to JFK service runs nonstop, several times a week. JFK is also one of the airports with a CDC-registered animal care facility on site, which matters if your dog's titre result isn't back in time and the 28-day quarantine option becomes the fallback. Confirm current cargo capacity directly before booking. Availability on a single scheduled service can tighten fast.

    - heading: "The titre test clock, and cats skipping it entirely"
      body: |
        Two dates matter more than any others on this route: the day blood is drawn for the titre test, and the day your flight lands in the US. The draw has to happen at least 30 days after the rabies vaccination, and the result has to be in hand at least 28 days before arrival. Kansas State University's Rabies Laboratory, a commonly used CDC-approved option, runs the FAVN test for around USD 90 with a three to four week turnaround. The maths leaves little slack if you start late.

        Get the dates wrong, or the result doesn't arrive in time, and your dog still travels. It just does so into a mandatory 28-day quarantine at a CDC-registered facility, paid for by you, on top of everything else. A cat sidesteps all of this: no vaccination requirement, no titre test, no facility booking. GLDA's own export paperwork still applies to cats in full, and most airlines will still want their own health certificate, but the federal US side is close to a formality by comparison.
