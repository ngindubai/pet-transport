  sections:
    - heading: "Italian Pet Import Rules at a Glance"
      body: |
        Italy's animal import authority is the Ministry of Health (Ministero della Salute, salute.gov.it). For non-commercial pet movement, Italy applies EU rules directly.

        Microchip: ISO 11784/11785 transponder, implanted before the rabies vaccination. Both Milan Malpensa (MXP) and Rome Fiumicino (FCO) cargo terminals have microchip scanners and will verify the number on arrival.

        Rabies vaccination: Current and post-microchip. At least 21 days must have passed since the primary vaccination before the pet enters Italy. Boosters within the valid period are treated as continuous.

        Health certificate: EU Annex IV non-commercial format, signed by a VSD-authorised vet in Jamaica and endorsed by the Veterinary Services Division. The certificate must be issued within 10 days of travel. Italian customs and veterinary inspectors check the issue date carefully.

        No titre test is required from Jamaica. No quarantine. No import permit.

    - heading: "Airline Options and Hub Choices"
      body: |
        Italy has no direct long-haul cargo link from Jamaica, so all shipments transit through a European hub. The main hub options are London Heathrow (LHR), Amsterdam (AMS), and Frankfurt (FRA).

        British Airways World Cargo operates live animal cargo on the Jamaica to LHR route from both KIN and MBJ. From LHR, British Airways or ITA Airways (formerly Alitalia) can carry the animal to MXP or FCO. ITA Airways Cargo handles live animals and the Rome and Milan airports have dedicated import facilities.

        KLM via Amsterdam gives good coverage. KLM Cargo flies the transatlantic leg and connects into Italian cities via code-share and partner arrangements at AMS. Amsterdam's animal handling facility at Schiphol is well regarded.

        Lufthansa via Frankfurt is particularly convenient for large dogs. Frankfurt (FRA) is one of Europe's leading live animal cargo centres, and the FRA to MXP or FCO sector is served multiple times daily.

        Choosing the right hub depends partly on your pet's size, the time of year, and which carriers have available capacity on your preferred dates.

    - heading: "Milan or Rome: Which Entry Point?"
      body: |
        Most international live animal cargo from the Caribbean enters Italy at either Milan Malpensa (MXP) or Rome Fiumicino (FCO). Both airports have Italian Ministry of Health veterinary posts that carry out import inspections.

        Milan Malpensa is the busier cargo hub and tends to have more direct cargo connections from LHR, AMS, and FRA. If you are relocating to northern or central Italy, MXP is usually the more practical entry point.

        Rome Fiumicino handles cargo connections primarily via LHR and AMS. For moves to Rome, southern Italy, or Sicily, FCO may be more convenient, though your pet would need a further domestic journey if flying into MXP.

        Confirm with the cargo terminal which inspection procedures apply on your arrival day, as some airports require advance booking of veterinary inspection slots. Your freight forwarder or PetTransportGlobal can handle this.

    - heading: "Breed Restrictions in Italy"
      body: |
        Italy maintains a list of restricted dog breeds that cannot be imported or owned in certain circumstances. The list has changed over the years and is governed by Ordinance of 3 March 2009 (as subsequently updated by the Ministry of Health). Breed-specific legislation in Italy focuses primarily on dogs considered high-risk based on morphology and historical bite records.

        If your dog is a Pit Bull Terrier, Rottweiler, or is otherwise on the restricted list, you must check current Italian Ministry of Health guidance at salute.gov.it before finalising travel plans. Restrictions can include mandatory muzzling in public, microchipping (already required), and liability insurance.

        For cats and non-restricted dog breeds, no additional requirements apply beyond the standard import documentation.

    - heading: "Timeline Summary"
      body: |
        Week 1-2: Microchip implanted by your Jamaican vet (if not already done).

        Week 1-6: Rabies vaccination administered after chipping. Wait the mandatory 21 days. During this period, source your IATA crate and begin crate training.

        Week 2-6: Book cargo space to Italy via your chosen hub. Italian routes are popular and cargo space is limited, particularly during summer.

        Within 10 days of departure: VSD-authorised vet issues the EU health certificate. VSD endorses it. This is time-sensitive, plan around your departure date.

        Day of travel: Deliver to KIN or MBJ cargo terminal with all documentation. Italian Ministry of Health veterinary inspection on arrival at MXP or FCO.

        The total elapsed time from starting preparations to departure is four to eight weeks.

faqs:
  - question: "Does my Jamaican-origin pet need a titre test to enter Italy?"
    answer: "No. Italy is an EU member state, and Jamaica is classified as an EU Group 2 listed country under Implementing Regulation (EU) 2024/1130. The titre test pathway applies only to pets from unlisted countries. Your pet needs a microchip, a current rabies vaccination with 21 days elapsed since the primary dose, and a VSD-endorsed EU health certificate."
  - question: "My pet will arrive at Milan Malpensa as unaccompanied cargo. How does the collection process work?"
    answer: "Unaccompanied live animal cargo arrives at the MXP cargo terminal. You or your authorised representative must present the cargo tracking number, your ID, and collection documentation to the cargo terminal. The Italian Ministry of Health veterinary officer inspects the animal and documents before release. Allow several hours for the collection process. Your freight forwarder can guide you through the specific procedures at MXP."
  - question: "How long after arrival in Italy should I wait before taking my pet to a vet?"
    answer: "A check-up within 48-72 hours of arrival is good practice. The journey is tiring, and your vet in Italy will want to confirm the animal is settled, hydrated, and in good health after travel. There is no regulatory requirement for a post-arrival vet visit, but it is advisable for any pet that has undergone a long cargo journey."
