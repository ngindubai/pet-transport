  h1: "Pet Transport from the United States to Russia"
  overview: |
    Ask what Russia actually requires from an incoming pet and the answer is refreshingly short: a microchip, a current rabies vaccination, a few routine vaccines, and an APHIS-endorsed health certificate. No titre test. No quarantine. No import permit for the usual one or two pets. On paper, this looks like one of the easier long-haul moves on this site.

    Then you try to book the flight. No airline has flown directly between the US and Russia since early 2022, when reciprocal airspace bans followed the closure of Russian skies to Western aircraft. That situation has not changed by mid-2026, and nothing currently on the table suggests it will soon.

    So the real work on this route is not veterinary, it's logistical: two separate cargo bookings through Istanbul, Dubai or Doha, timed so the US health certificate's 5-day window still holds by the time your pet reaches Moscow.
  sections:
    - heading: "What Russia checks on arrival, and why it's lighter than you might expect"
      body: |
        Rosselkhoznadzor's requirements for an incoming pet cover the basics: an ISO microchip implanted before vaccination, a rabies vaccine given between 30 days and 12 months before arrival, the DHLP series for dogs or FVRCP for cats, and a recent parasite treatment. Up to 2 pets travelling with their owner need no separate import permit.

        The document that ties it together is the APHIS-endorsed Veterinary Health Certificate for Russia. A USDA-accredited vet has to examine the animal within 5 days of the flight, and APHIS then endorses the certificate, either on paper with an original ink signature or electronically through VEHCS. There is no titre test and no post-arrival quarantine for a pet that shows up with this paperwork in order.

        Source: USDA APHIS's country page for pet travel to Russia, aphis.usda.gov/pet-travel, checked July 2026.
    - heading: "No direct flight, and what a two-leg routing actually means for your paperwork"
      body: |
        The US and its allies closed their airspace to Russian carriers in early 2022, and Russia responded with its own ban covering US-registered aircraft. Both remain in force in mid-2026. Turkey, the UAE and Qatar sit outside both bans, which is why Istanbul, Dubai and Doha are the three routings that actually work.

        That solves the flight problem but adds a paperwork timing problem. The US health certificate's 5-day pre-travel window has to still be valid when your pet lands in Russia, not just when it departs the US. With a transit stop and a connecting live-animal booking on a separate airline, that means locking in both flight dates before the vet appointment is booked, not the other way round.

        Emirates via Dubai currently reaches the widest spread of US gateway cities for the first leg. Turkish Airlines via Istanbul and Qatar Airways via Doha are the other two realistic options; all three require two separate cargo bookings rather than one through-fare.
    - heading: "Planning the certificate timing around a routing with no through-ticket"
      body: |
        Because no single airline sells a US-to-Moscow fare, your relocation plan has to work backwards from a confirmed arrival date in Russia rather than forwards from a convenient US departure date. Book both legs first, then schedule the USDA vet exam so the 5-day certificate window still covers the actual Moscow landing, allowing for the transit connection.

        An IPATA-registered agent with recent Russia experience is the most reliable way to keep the certificate timing, the crate booking, and the transit handover aligned, particularly since Gulf-hub carriers sometimes apply seasonal live-animal embargoes that can force a late change of routing.
