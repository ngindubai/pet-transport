  h1: "Pet Transport from Ukraine to Canada"
  overview: |
    A dog that has lived in Ukraine within the past six months has to be at least seven months old to clear Canadian border control, because Ukraine sits on the CFIA's list of countries considered high-risk for dog rabies. That single age rule catches out more puppies on this route than any other single requirement.

    Cats have an easier time of it: CFIA's high-risk-country rules are built around dog rabies specifically, so a cat from Ukraine follows a simpler standard vaccination-certificate process without the age threshold or the extra scrutiny dogs face.

  sections:
    - heading: "What Ukraine's place on the CFIA list actually means"
      body: |
        CFIA maintains a list of countries it treats as high-risk for dog rabies, and Ukraine is on it. That doesn't mean a family pet can't enter Canada. It means the microchip has to go in before the vaccination, and the dog has to be at least 7 months old at the point of entry if it originated in, lived in, or passed through Ukraine within the previous 6 months.

        Commercial dogs from high-risk countries, meaning those for resale, adoption, fostering, breeding, show or research, have been banned outright from Canada since 28 September 2022. A family dog travelling with its owner isn't commercial, so this ban doesn't apply here. Worth confirming that distinction with the CFIA port office ahead of arrival regardless, since paperwork from a high-risk country tends to get a closer look.

    - heading: "No titre test, but a mandatory port inspection"
      body: |
        Unlike the CDC's US pathway or MOCCAE's UAE process, CFIA doesn't ask for a rabies titre test from a personal pet dog arriving from a high-risk country. The safeguard instead sits in the age rule and in a mandatory inspection by a CFIA veterinarian at the first Canadian port of entry, which the owner has to book at least 24 hours ahead of arrival.

        That inspection isn't a formality. Documentation gets checked against the dog's microchip, its stated age, and its vaccination history, all cross-referenced against the fact that it's coming from a high-risk country.

    - heading: "The overland leg into Poland comes first regardless"
      body: |
        None of this starts with a flight out of Kyiv or Lviv, because there isn't one. Civilian airspace has been closed since 24 February 2022 and stays closed in mid-2026. The pet crosses overland, most commonly through Shehyni-Medyka or Krakovets-Korczowa near Lviv into Poland, or a Romanian crossing further south, before reaching a working airport.

        LOT Polish Airlines then flies nonstop from Warsaw to Toronto six times a week, a genuinely simple booking once the overland leg is done. It's the one part of this route that runs on a normal schedule.

    - heading: "Costs, and the humanitarian route that doesn't cover pets"
      body: |
        Total costs typically run USD 1,700 to USD 4,200, spanning the Ukrainian vet work, the overland transfer, the border certificate, a crate, and the Warsaw-Toronto cargo fare. The CFIA port inspection itself has no published flat fee; check with the local Animal Health office for the port in question.

        Canada extended its CUAET-linked work permit measures again in March 2026, giving holders until March 2027 to apply for a further extension. That's a people-only programme. Nothing in it changes the age rule, the microchip timing, or the port inspection requirement for a Ukrainian family's dog or cat, however the owners themselves are travelling.
