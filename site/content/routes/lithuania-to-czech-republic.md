  sections:
    - heading: "Routing: Vilnius to Warsaw to Prague"
      body: |
        LOT Polish Airlines is the most straightforward option. The Vilnius (VNO) to Warsaw Chopin (WAW) leg takes under an hour, and LOT's onward service to Prague (PRG) connects well. Small pets (up to 8kg including carrier) travel in the cabin throughout the journey. Larger dogs go in the pressurised hold.

        When booking, confirm that your pet reservation covers both legs of the journey. Pet bookings sometimes need to be made separately for each segment, and a cabin space on the first leg does not automatically extend to the second.

        airBaltic via Riga is an alternative with slightly different scheduling. Lufthansa via Frankfurt is a third option, particularly useful for larger dogs needing cargo transport, as Lufthansa Cargo is well established on the Frankfurt to Prague corridor.

    - heading: "Czech SVA entry rules"
      body: |
        The Czech State Veterinary Administration follows EU standards for pets arriving from other EU member states. The requirements are: a microchip (ISO 11784/11785), a valid rabies vaccination with the 21-day first-vaccination wait completed, and an EU pet passport or AHC.

        Czech veterinary officers check documents at Prague Airport. For compliant pets, this is a routine inspection. If documentation is missing or outdated, entry may be refused or the pet held pending resolution.

        The AHC, if used instead of an EU passport, must be endorsed within 10 days of your arrival in Prague. Co-ordinate the endorsement appointment so it falls within that window.

    - heading: "After arrival: local rules in Czech cities"
      body: |
        The Czech Republic has no national breed ban. Czech law does not prohibit any breed from entering or residing in the country. However, individual municipalities have some latitude to set their own rules for dogs in public spaces.

        In Prague and other larger cities, dogs on public transport must be on a lead and wearing a muzzle. Parks and squares may have their own rules. These are domestic regulations and do not affect entry into the country.

        Cats face no breed restrictions in the Czech Republic and no specific public-space requirements beyond standard animal welfare obligations.
