  h1: "Pet Transport United Arab Emirates to Latvia"
  overview: |
    airBaltic's home base is Riga, which is a genuine advantage on this route: connecting through the carrier's own hub puts the pet on a network built specifically for onward Baltic travel. The rest of the journey, though, runs on EU rabies-control time rather than Gulf efficiency.

    Latvia treats the UAE as an Annex II country under EU rules, meaning a rabies antibody titre test and a fixed 90-day wait from the blood draw both apply, the same as they would for most countries outside the EU's short low-risk list.

  sections:
    - heading: "The 90-day wait, and why it's non-negotiable"
      body: |
        A pet needs a current rabies vaccination, a titre test drawn at least 30 days afterwards at an EU-approved laboratory, a result of at least 0.5 IU/ml, and then a further 90 days counted from the date blood was actually drawn. Latvia's Food and Veterinary Service (PVD) enforces this exactly as written; there's no discretion to shorten it for a UAE-origin pet, since the UAE sits in Annex II rather than the low-risk category that exempts UK and EU-origin pets from the test entirely.

    - heading: "MOCCAE's side of the paperwork"
      body: |
        Every pet leaving the UAE needs the standard MOCCAE export certificate, applied for online with an in-person clinic exam in Dubai or Abu Dhabi. For this specific destination, an EU-format health certificate is also required, valid for only 10 days, so it needs to be timed against the actual departure date rather than issued early.

    - heading: "Why Riga is the natural gateway"
      body: |
        airBaltic operates out of Riga as its home hub, which makes a Gulf-to-Riga connection a genuinely well-supported routing rather than an afterthought. Turkish Airlines Cargo via Istanbul remains a solid alternative, particularly for larger dogs, if airBaltic's live animal capacity is unavailable.
