content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Serbia to the United States"
  overview: |
    Belgrade's only direct flight to the United States happens to be one of the few Air Serbia routes that will actually carry a pet. The airline excludes pets entirely from its UK, UAE and Qatar services, but the Belgrade to New York JFK service, flying since June 2016, takes cats and small dogs in the cabin for a flat EUR 130 fee. That is the good news.

    The less convenient part is size. Nothing in Air Serbia's published pet policy describes a hold or cargo option on the JFK route, so a dog too big for an 8kg combined cabin limit needs a different carrier: Lufthansa Cargo via Frankfurt or Munich, or Turkish Airlines Cargo via Istanbul, both routine workarounds for Balkan pet shipments headed to the US.

    On the regulatory side, this route is genuinely easy. Serbia does not appear on the CDC's high-risk countries for dog rabies list, so a Serbian dog qualifies for the CDC's low-risk pathway: a Dog Import Form and a healthy appearance, no titre test, no quarantine. Cats need almost nothing at all at the federal border.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Serbia's CDC status makes this route simpler than it looks"
      body: |
        The CDC sorts countries into high-risk and low-risk for dog rabies, and the distinction drives almost everything about a dog's US entry. Checking the current list directly against cdc.gov confirms Serbia is not on it, and no other Balkan country appears there either. That puts a Serbian dog in the same low-risk bracket as the UK, most of the EU, Japan and Australia.

        Since the CDC's 2024 overhaul, every dog needs a CDC Dog Import Form regardless of origin, a free online form that generates a QR code for the border. For a low-risk-country dog, that plus looking healthy is essentially the whole federal requirement. There is no titre test, no CDC-registered facility restriction on which airport you land at, and no quarantine. Cats have it even easier: no federal health certificate, no vaccination proof, no form.

        None of this replaces good sense. A rabies vaccination, current and recorded, is still what Air Serbia and most vets on both ends of the trip will expect to see, and it protects the classification itself: a dog that has spent recent months somewhere on the CDC's high-risk list loses the low-risk treatment even if it currently lives in Serbia.

    - heading: "Air Serbia's JFK route, and what to do if your dog is too big for the cabin"
      body: |
        Air Serbia's Belgrade to New York service is the airline's longest route and one of its oldest transatlantic connections, running since 2016. Unlike the UK, UAE and Qatar services, it is not subject to a blanket pet exclusion. Cats and dogs up to 8kg combined weight with the carrier travel in the cabin, carrier dimensions capped at roughly 94cm total, for a EUR 130 fee, the same rate the airline charges on its China route.

        The gap is at the other end of the size range. Searching the airline's own guidance and independent airline-policy trackers turns up no mention of a hold or checked-cargo pet option specifically for the JFK sector, in contrast to some of Air Serbia's shorter regional routes where hold transport is described more generally. Anyone with a dog too large for the cabin should not assume the direct flight is an option and should instead book a cargo routing well ahead: Lufthansa Cargo through Frankfurt or Munich, or Turkish Airlines Cargo through Istanbul, both established, temperature-controlled cargo services with a track record on Balkan-to-US pet shipments.

    - heading: "Serbia's export process"
      body: |
        On the Serbian side, the Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry and Water Management's Veterinary Directorate handles pet export documentation. A Serbian vet examines the animal and prepares a general health certificate, and a government veterinary inspector endorses it, typically within 2 to 3 working days. There is no separate export permit to apply for beyond this certificate, since the US does not require an import permit and Serbia does not operate a general export licensing system for non-commercial pet moves.

        Because the US side asks so little of a Serbian pet, most of the real planning on this route sits with the airline booking rather than the paperwork. Decide early whether your pet fits Air Serbia's cabin limit, and if not, build the cargo alternative into your timeline rather than discovering the gap a few weeks before travel.
