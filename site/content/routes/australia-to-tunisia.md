content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Australia to Tunisia"
  overview: |
    An Australian export permit for a pet dog or cat is only good for 72 hours from the moment DAFF issues it. Book the flight around that fact, not the other way round: the permit doesn't exist until the final vet assessment is done, and the pet has to be airborne within three days of it being signed.

    Once that's arranged, the harder question sits on the Tunisian side. Tunisia bans Pit Bull, Rottweiler, Tosa and Mastiff-type dogs and their crosses outright, whatever an Australian vet's paperwork says about temperament or health. For every other pet, the process is comparatively light: no titre test in established DGSV practice, no quarantine with a compliant file, and a transit routing through Doha, Dubai or Istanbul since no airline flies this city pair direct.

  sections:
    - heading: "The 72-hour export permit, and why the order of operations matters"
      body: |
        DAFF doesn't issue a health certificate on request. The process starts with a Notice of Intention to Export, lodged with the regional office at least 10 working days ahead of departure, which books a final veterinary assessment. Only once that vet is satisfied the pet meets Tunisia's stated import requirements does the export permit get signed.

        From that signature, the clock runs to zero in 72 hours. Miss the window, whether from a delayed flight, a cancelled cargo booking or a paperwork query, and the permit has to be reissued from scratch. Build the flight booking around the vet appointment date, not the reverse.

    - heading: "A ban that no amount of Australian paperwork changes"
      body: |
        Tunisia's DGSV refuses entry to Pit Bull, Rottweiler, Tosa and Mastiff-type dogs and their crosses, a blanket rule applied at Tunis-Carthage regardless of where the dog is coming from. An Australian vet's assessment of temperament, training or health carries no weight against it. Check the dog's type before spending a cent on the export process, not after the DAFF appointment is booked.

        There's no appeal at the border. A dog turned away flies back to Australia at the owner's expense, on top of whatever the outbound leg already cost.

    - heading: "No direct flight, so the connection is part of the plan"
      body: |
        Qatar Airways is the standout option here, since it flies both the Australia leg and the existing Doha-Tunis sector, meaning one carrier can, in principle, cover the whole journey. Emirates is a strong alternative, running Australia to Dubai and picking up the same Dubai-Tunis nonstop that already serves Tunisian routes to the UAE. Turkish Airlines via Istanbul is the fallback when Gulf cargo space is tight.

        Whichever carrier is used, confirm live animal space on the connecting sector separately from the first leg. A through-fare on a booking site says nothing about whether the connecting flight has cargo capacity for a crate that size on that date.
