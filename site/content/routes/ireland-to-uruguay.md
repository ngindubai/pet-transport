content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Ireland to Uruguay: 2026 Guide"
  overview: |
    No titre test. That single fact makes the Ireland-to-Uruguay direction far simpler than the reverse trip, where a Uruguayan pet faces a 90-day EU titre-test wait to enter Ireland. Uruguay does not classify Ireland, or any EU member state, as high enough rabies risk to warrant a laboratory test.

    What Uruguay does ask for is an MGAP import permit, arranged before travel, and, for dogs, a praziquantel treatment against Echinococcus timed to a 72-hour-to-30-day window before arrival. Get those two pieces right and the process runs to six to nine weeks rather than months.

  sections:
    - heading: "The MGAP import permit: the step to start first"
      body: |
        Uruguay's Ministry of Livestock, Agriculture and Fisheries (MGAP) requires an import permit for every incoming companion pet, issued before you travel and specifying the conditions your paperwork must meet. This is not a formality to leave until the final week; apply for it as soon as your move to Uruguay is confirmed, since it can influence your vaccination and documentation timeline.

        Once the permit terms are known, the rest of the process is comparatively light: current rabies vaccination, a DAFM-endorsed Animal Health Certificate, and the dog-specific echinococcus treatment.
    - heading: "Why dogs need the echinococcus treatment and cats do not"
      body: |
        Uruguay's concern is hydatid tapeworm, not rabies, on this particular requirement. Dogs arriving from any origin need a praziquantel-based treatment administered between 72 hours and 30 days before arrival, documented by an Irish vet on the health certificate. Cats are not subject to this rule.

        Because the window opens 30 days out but closes just 72 hours before landing, most owners find it easiest to schedule the final Official Veterinarian appointment for a few days before departure, covering the AHC endorsement and the echinococcus treatment record in the same visit.
