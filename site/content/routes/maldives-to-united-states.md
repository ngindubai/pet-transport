  overview: |
    Federal entry rules make this one of the lighter destinations on paper: the CDC does not require a rabies titre test or vaccination proof for cats, and its current high-risk country list, updated 15 April 2026, does not name the Maldives at all. That is the easy part.

    The hard part is the routing. Nothing flies direct from Male to the United States, so every shipment goes through a Gulf or Asian hub, and those hubs sometimes stop accepting live animal cargo altogether during the hottest months. Plan the calendar around that, not just the paperwork.

  sections:
    - heading: "Almost nobody exports a dog from here"
      body: |
        We say this on every Maldives page because it changes what "the process" even means. Dogs cannot be legally imported into the Maldives, so the resident dog population an owner might want to relocate barely exists. If your situation involves a dog, it is worth a direct conversation rather than following a template built around cats.
    - heading: "What the CDC actually asks for"
      body: |
        For a cat, not much. No titre test, no mandatory rabies proof at the federal level, no CDC Dog Import Form, since that form applies to dogs. Airlines will still want a health certificate issued within about 10 days of travel, and some US states layer on their own requirements, so check the destination state as well as the federal baseline.
    - heading: "The real bottleneck: the connecting hub"
      body: |
        Doha and Dubai both handle heavy volumes of live animal cargo, and both have been known to pause acceptance when tarmac temperatures climb too high for animals to sit safely during ground handling. This is not a Maldives-specific rule, but it hits every Male departure because there is no way around a Gulf or Asian connection on this route.
    - heading: "Choosing a routing"
      body: |
        Qatar Airways via Doha and Emirates via Dubai are the established options. Singapore Airlines is worth asking about if you are travelling in the embargo-prone months, since a different hub sometimes means a different heat policy and an earlier travel window.
