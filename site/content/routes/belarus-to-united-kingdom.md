content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Belarus to the United Kingdom"
  overview: |
    No airline has flown between Minsk and any UK or EU airport since June 2021, when Brussels and London grounded Belarusian carriers after the forced landing of Ryanair Flight 4978. That ban is still in force in 2026, tightened further in December 2025, and it's the first problem any pet move out of Belarus has to solve. Everything starts on the road, not in the air.

  sections:
    - heading: "The airspace ban that shapes every option"
      body: |
        Belavia, Belarus's national carrier, lost access to EU airspace and airports within days of the Ryanair diversion in May 2021. The UK followed with its own suspension of Belavia's operating permit and a ban on British carriers overflying Belarus. Neither restriction has been lifted. If anything, the EU widened the sanctions regime again in December 2025, adding measures around unauthorised border crossings and hybrid activity after a series of incidents involving unmanned balloons entering Lithuanian and Polish airspace from Belarus.

        The upshot for pet owners is blunt: there's no scheduled flight, no interline transfer, no way to book a single ticket that starts in Minsk. Every move begins with a car.

    - heading: "Belarus's animal-health status hasn't changed"
      body: |
        Here's the detail most owners miss. Belarus still sits in Part 2 of GB's retained Annex II country list, the same veterinary risk category as the USA, Canada and Japan. That classification comes from Regulation (EU) No 577/2013, as retained in UK law, and it has nothing to do with the political sanctions above.

        Practically, that means no titre test and no 3-month wait. A current rabies vaccination, the microchip, and the GB Pet Health Certificate get a compliant pet through UK border checks, exactly as they would from a fully sanctions-free country. The hard part of this route is logistics, not paperwork.

    - heading: "Getting from Minsk to an EU airport"
      body: |
        Vilnius is the nearest practical option, about 184km and 2 hours 40 minutes by road via the M7. Warsaw is further but carries more flight capacity, including a daily British Airways service to Heathrow alongside LOT Polish Airlines' own network. Both routes cross an EU external border, and both require the Belarusian export certificate and, for road-transported animals, clearance through a designated Border Control Post.

        Whichever gateway is used, pets fly onward as manifested cargo. Budget carriers with high-frequency Vilnius or Warsaw to UK schedules generally don't carry live animals at all; the booking has to go through a cargo-handling agent, usually arranged by an IPATA-registered relocation specialist rather than direct with the airline.

    - heading: "If this is a rescue, or more than one pet"
      body: |
        GOV.UK publishes guidance specifically naming Belarus, alongside Poland, Romania and Ukraine, for commercial imports of dogs, cats and ferrets. That category covers moving 5 or more animals at once, and any pet where ownership changes hands, which includes most rescue rehomings.

        Under this route, the importer needs Approved Importer status from APHA, has to notify APHA at least 7 working days ahead via IPAFFS, and, for road-transported consignments originating in Belarus, has to clear an EU Border Control Post before arriving at Dover or Folkestone, and only within a Monday to Friday, 10am to 4pm GMT window. A single owned pet travelling with its owner doesn't need any of this, but it's worth checking which category applies before assuming the standard personal-pet route covers the move.
