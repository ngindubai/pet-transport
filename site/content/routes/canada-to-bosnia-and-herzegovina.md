content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Canada to Bosnia and Herzegovina"
  overview: |
    Bosnia and Herzegovina keeps its own two-part list of rabies-risk countries, and Canada already sits on the easier half of it. Part B of the Veterinary Office's Annex IV regulation names Canada by name, in the same low-risk company as the United States, Japan and Australia. That single classification strips out the titre test and its three-month wait, leaving just the standard 21 days after vaccination.

    What takes longer on this route is Canadian, not Bosnian. CFIA's own export process wants an official CFIA veterinarian's endorsement, booked by appointment, before a pet leaves the country at all, and that appointment system runs to its own schedule regardless of how light Bosnia's import side turns out to be.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Canada already qualifies for the lighter pathway"
      body: |
        Bosnia and Herzegovina's Veterinary Office runs its own Annex IV list rather than simply mirroring the EU's current framework. Part A covers EU and EFTA states plus a handful of microstates. Part B covers a separate group of recognised low-risk countries, and Canada appears there by name, alongside the United States, Japan and Australia (Official Gazette of Bosnia and Herzegovina No. 34/12 and 68/12).

        Both parts skip the titre test entirely. Only a pet from a country absent from Annex IV altogether faces the FAVN blood test and its three-month wait. For a Canadian pet, that means microchip, 21 days after vaccination, and an animal health certificate from an official vet, nothing close to the paperwork weight a titre-test route would add.

    - heading: "The part that actually takes planning"
      body: |
        A vet signing a certificate is routine. Getting an official CFIA veterinarian to endorse it runs through a district office appointment system rather than a walk-in service, typically 2 to 5 business days, longer in peak season. CFIA is explicit that it cannot endorse a certificate for a pet that has already left Canada, so this step has to happen before departure, not tidied up afterwards.

        CFIA also doesn't publish a certificate template specific to Bosnia and Herzegovina, a parallel to the same gap USDA APHIS has confirmed for its own American exporters heading to the same country. A CFIA-accredited vet builds a general international certificate to match what Bosnia's Part B rules actually ask for instead of relying on a ready-made bilateral form.

    - heading: "Getting to Sarajevo from three different Canadian gateways"
      body: |
        No airline flies Toronto directly to Sarajevo. Turkish Airlines via Istanbul runs a genuinely frequent Toronto-Istanbul service, 9 times a week, with Turkish Cargo's dedicated Pet Lounge easing the connection onward. Lufthansa via Frankfurt is a long-established alternative, and Frankfurt, not Munich, is the group's cargo-pet-capable hub.

        Austrian Airlines via Vienna is workable too, with a caveat worth flagging: Austrian's own Canadian network has narrowed, so the transatlantic leg of a Vienna-routed booking often ends up flown by a Star Alliance partner. Confirm the operating carrier before committing, since pet acceptance policy isn't guaranteed to be identical between the two.
