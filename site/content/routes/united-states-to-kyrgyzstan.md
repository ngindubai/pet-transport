  h1: "Pet Transport from the United States to Kyrgyzstan"
  overview: |
    Kyrgyzstan sits on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies, a fact that shapes everything about a dog travelling the other way, into the US. None of it applies here. A pet flying from the United States into Kyrgyzstan needs no titre test and no quarantine reservation, whatever the CDC's own rules say about the reverse trip.

    What Kyrgyzstan does ask for is precision on timing. The health certificate has to be issued within 5 days of travel, a noticeably tighter window than USDA's usual process allows for at many other destinations. Get the flight booked first, then work the vet appointment and APHIS endorsement backwards from that date.

    Budget six to nine weeks in total. The medical requirements themselves are light; the real planning challenge is Turkish Airlines being the only realistic long-haul carrier and the certificate's short shelf life.
  sections:
    - heading: "The species-neutral, titre-free rule that makes this route lighter than it looks"
      body: |
        A dog entering the United States from Kyrgyzstan faces the CDC's full high-risk protocol: a titre test, or a 28-day quarantine at a CDC-registered facility, plus a government-endorsed certification form. A dog leaving the United States for Kyrgyzstan faces none of that. Kyrgyzstan's own import rules do not require a rabies titre test from a pet arriving from any country, the US included, and the requirement applies the same way to both dogs and cats.

        What remains is a current rabies vaccination, given between 21 days and 11 months before entry, and a government-endorsed health certificate. Microchipping is recommended rather than mandated, though fitting one before vaccination keeps the paperwork trail consistent.

        Source: State Inspectorate for Veterinary and Phytosanitary Safety of the Kyrgyz Republic, and USDA APHIS's own published guidance for the US-Kyrgyzstan pet travel corridor.
    - heading: "The regional certificate and the five-day clock"
      body: |
        USDA APHIS uses a shared health certificate form for this part of Central Asia, covering Armenia, Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan alike. A USDA-accredited vet completes it, then submits it through the Veterinary Export Health Certification System (VEHCS) for the USDA APHIS State Veterinarian's endorsement, typically taking 3 to 5 business days.

        Kyrgyzstan then only accepts a certificate issued within 5 days of travel. That's a materially shorter window than the 10-day rule common on many other routes, so the sequencing matters: confirm the flight, then count backwards to the vet appointment, rather than getting the certificate done early and hoping the departure date lines up.
    - heading: "Why Turkish Airlines carries almost this entire route"
      body: |
        Air Astana, the obvious alternative carrier on the Kyrgyzstan corridor for UK-bound pets, has no scheduled US service. That leaves Turkish Airlines' network as the only realistic long-haul option: fly from a US gateway city (New York JFK, Washington Dulles, Atlanta or Chicago among them) to Istanbul, then on to Manas International Airport in Bishkek.

        If your nearest airport isn't already a Turkish Airlines departure point, American or United can usually cover the domestic connection to one of those gateways, though neither carries the pet on the international sector itself.
