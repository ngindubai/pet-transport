content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Guyana to the United Arab Emirates"
  overview: |
    There's no such thing as a single-airline booking from Georgetown to Dubai or Abu Dhabi. Every pet on this route changes carriers at least once, and for a dog the choice of connection matters more than usual because of a US rabies rule that has nothing to do with the UAE at all. Guyana's absence from MOCCAE's roughly 50-country low-risk list adds a rabies titre test to the critical path before the import permit can even be requested.

    The one piece of good news: unlike the EU's fixed 90-day wait, the UAE doesn't make anyone count down a set number of days once that titre test passes. Clear the test, get the permit, and the clock stops running.

  sections:
    - heading: "The titre test, without a fixed wait afterward"
      body: |
        MOCCAE splits every country of origin into low-risk or, by default, higher-risk. Guyana falls into the second group, so a titre test at an accredited laboratory is required: blood drawn after vaccination, a result of at least 0.5 IU/ml. What's different from the EU pathway many owners compare this to is the missing mandatory wait once the result lands. There's no 90-day countdown. The pet can travel as soon as the test passes and the import permit, valid for 90 days from issue, is confirmed.

        That's most of why this route can move faster than a comparable EU-bound one. The laboratory turnaround, not a fixed regulatory wait, is what actually decides the pace.

    - heading: "Why every booking runs through London, and why that means two airports"
      body: |
        British Airways' BA2159 is the only practical first leg: Georgetown to Gatwick via a technical stop in St Lucia, twice weekly, same aircraft throughout. From there, Emirates SkyCargo or Etihad Cargo picks up the run to Dubai or Abu Dhabi, both with strong live-animal handling reputations at their respective hubs.

        The catch most owners don't see coming: Emirates and Etihad fly from Heathrow, not Gatwick. British Airways' Guyana service lands at Gatwick. The two airports sit roughly 45 miles apart with no direct rail link, so the pet, and the paperwork, has to physically transfer between them. Good agents build this in as a distinct handling step with its own timing, not just a line item on an invoice.

    - heading: "The CDC rule that quietly shapes this route"
      body: |
        It's easy to assume the shortest path to Dubai runs through a US hub like Miami, given how much of the Caribbean and South America connects that way. For a dog leaving Guyana, it doesn't. Guyana sits on the CDC's list of high-risk countries for dog rabies, and American Airlines Cargo won't accept a new dog booking originating there. That's a rule about dogs entering the United States, with no bearing on UAE entry requirements, but it still rules out an American Airlines connection for a dog on this route.

        Cats aren't covered by that CDC classification, so a Miami or JFK-routed connection stays technically open for a cat if the London routing doesn't fit the travel window. For dogs, London is the practical answer, not a workaround.
