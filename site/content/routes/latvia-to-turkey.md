  h1: "Pet Transport from Latvia to Turkey"
  overview: |
    Riga sits at the centre of the Baltic air network, well connected in almost every direction. Istanbul is no exception in terms of flight availability. The paperwork is the part that changes once the destination stops being an EU member state.

    Latvia's PVD has to issue a standalone export certificate for this route, separate from the EU pet passport that covers the rest of Latvia's corridors in this build, and Turkey's titre test applies in full.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why the passport that works everywhere else doesn't work here"
      body: |
        An EU pet passport, once issued, moves a pet freely between member states with no titre test and no separate paperwork. Turkey isn't a member state, and doesn't recognise the passport for entry purposes. PVD, Latvia's Food and Veterinary Service, issues a distinct export health certificate for this route instead, built to Turkey's own model rather than the pet passport format. No separate export permit sits on top of that certificate.

        It's a genuinely different process from Latvia's EU-bound routes, and the most common mistake is assuming the passport already in the pet's file will do the job here too.

    - heading: "The titre test and Riga's flight options"
      body: |
        The FAVN test requires blood drawn at least 30 days after the rabies vaccination, a result of at least 0.5 IU/ml, and a 90-day wait from that draw date before travel. A booster given after the test resets its own 21-day wait, worth checking against your travel date if a revaccination is due.

        Turkish Airlines runs Riga-Istanbul direct, and airBaltic's own network from its Riga base offers a workable connecting alternative if direct cargo space is tight.
