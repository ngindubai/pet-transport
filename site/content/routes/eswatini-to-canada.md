content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Eswatini to Canada"
  overview: |
    On the US route, a dog from Eswatini has a genuine shortcut: Delta flies Johannesburg to Atlanta nonstop, landing directly at one of the six CDC-facility airports. Canada offers nothing like it. No carrier flies Johannesburg to Toronto or Montreal without a stop, so every Eswatini-to-Canada pet move runs through three legs, not two: the short regional hop from King Mswati III International Airport to Johannesburg, then a long-haul sector via Amsterdam, Frankfurt or Munich, then on to Canada.

    The paperwork side is lighter than the routing suggests. Eswatini sits on CFIA's high-risk list for dog rabies, the same list that triggered a 2022 ban on commercial dog imports into Canada. That ban doesn't reach a personal pet travelling with its owner, and CFIA hasn't published a titre test or import permit requirement for this category, despite the classification. The Department of Veterinary and Livestock Services still handles the export side the same way it does for the UK, US or Australian routes.
  sections:
    - heading: "Why Johannesburg is a stop, not a solution, on this route"
      body: |
        Every Eswatini pet move starts with Airlink or Eswatini Air covering the short SHO-JNB hop, since King Mswati III International Airport has no long-haul service of its own. On the US route, that Johannesburg connection leads straight to Delta's Atlanta nonstop, which happens to land at a CDC-registered facility airport. No equivalent nonstop exists to Canada. KLM's routing through Amsterdam and Lufthansa's through Frankfurt or Munich are the realistic options, each adding a full long-haul sector and a second connection before the pet reaches Toronto or Montreal.

        Book the whole thing as one interline cargo movement with a single ground agent managing the Johannesburg handover. Treating the three legs as separate bookings is the most common way this route goes wrong.

    - heading: "What Eswatini's high-risk listing actually changes"
      body: |
        CFIA's high-risk classification, confirmed for Eswatini on the list dated 13 March 2025, is real, but its practical effect on this route is narrower than the label implies. Since 28 September 2022, CFIA has refused import permits for commercial dogs from every high-risk country. A family pet flying with its owner isn't in that category, and CFIA's own notice only promised to consider tighter personal-pet rules later, not that it had introduced them.

        In practice, that means the rabies vaccination certificate and the Department of Veterinary and Livestock Services' export endorsement do most of the work here, the same as they would for a low-risk origin country. Confirm the position hasn't shifted before you commit to flight bookings, since this is one area CFIA has signalled it may revisit.
