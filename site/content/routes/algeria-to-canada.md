content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Algeria to Canada"
  overview: |
    CFIA draws the line between a personal pet and a commercial import at eight months old, and for a dog leaving a high-risk country like Algeria, that line does more work than almost anything else on this page. Algeria has sat on CFIA's high-risk-for-dog-rabies list since well before its most recent update on 13 March 2025, and commercial dogs from listed countries have been barred outright since September 2022.

    A personal pet, old enough and genuinely travelling with its owner, sits outside that ban entirely. What's left is a lighter process than Algeria's own US route: no titre test, no CDC-style foreign vaccination form, just a current rabies vaccination and a certificate in English or French.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why the high-risk listing matters less than it sounds"
      body: |
        CFIA's high-risk list groups Algeria with most of North Africa and dozens of other countries. It's tempting to assume that listing triggers the same titre-test-or-quarantine choice the CDC applies to the same dog entering the United States. Canada's rule works differently.

        The listing's practical force falls on commercial dog imports, stopped entirely from high-risk countries since 28 September 2022. A personal pet, one or two animals travelling with the owner who isn't handing them to someone else on arrival, follows the lighter standard process instead: vaccination, certificate, no test, no quarantine.

    - heading: "The age line that decides which rule applies"
      body: |
        CFIA presumes a dog under 8 months old is a commercial import if it isn't travelling with its named owner, a distinction that matters enormously here because a presumed-commercial dog from a high-risk country faces the outright ban, not a lighter personal-pet path. A dog old enough, and clearly accompanied by the owner named on its papers, avoids that presumption.

        This detail doesn't appear on every high-risk-country page this site has built, and it's worth taking seriously precisely because getting the classification wrong has real consequences here, not just extra paperwork. Confirm directly with CFIA if your pet's age or travel arrangements sit anywhere near that line.

    - heading: "Building the certificate for CFIA, not the CDC"
      body: |
        A DSV-registered vet who has already handled Algeria's US-bound exports knows the CDC's Certification of Foreign Rabies Vaccination and Microchip form well, including its English-only requirement and the DSV endorsement workaround that entails. Canada asks for none of that. CFIA wants a standard certificate in English or French, not a specific foreign form, which removes a translation headache that trips up the US route.

        Flag the Canadian destination clearly when booking the vet appointment, so DSV builds the certificate to CFIA's language requirement rather than reusing a template drafted for a different country's rules.

    - heading: "Getting to Canada without a direct flight"
      body: |
        Algiers has no nonstop service to Canada. Turkish Airlines via Istanbul, the same hub already established on Algeria's other routes, connects onward to Toronto or Montreal. Air France's busy Algiers-Paris sector, paired with its own Paris-Montreal service or Air Canada's transatlantic joint-venture booking on the same route, is the workable alternative.
