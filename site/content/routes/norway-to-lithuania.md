  sections:
    - heading: "Norway outside the EU: what it means for your pet's paperwork"
      body: |
        Norway is a member of the EEA (European Economic Area) and Schengen Area, but not the EU. For pet travel purposes, this places Norway in the category of a listed third country under EU Regulation 576/2013. Norwegian pets are treated with the same low-risk profile as EU pets in terms of titre test and quarantine requirements, but they carry an official health certificate rather than an EU pet passport.

        The certificate must be issued by a veterinarian authorised as an official vet by Mattilsynet (the Norwegian Food Safety Authority). Not every vet in Norway holds this official status, so check before booking the appointment. The certificate covers the same information as the EU pet passport: microchip number, vaccination history, vet details.

        Lithuania's VMVT accepts this certificate and processes the arrival in the same way as an EU pet passport. Spot checks at Vilnius Airport are occasional and straightforward for a compliant arrival.

    - heading: "Clarifying Norway's tapeworm rule before you travel"
      body: |
        Norway's Echinococcus (tapeworm) treatment requirement is one of the most commonly misunderstood rules for Norwegian pet owners. The rule is: dogs must receive tapeworm treatment using praziquantel in the 24 to 120 hours before arriving in Norway.

        It applies to dogs arriving IN Norway from certain countries, not dogs leaving Norway. If you are moving your dog from Oslo to Vilnius, no tapeworm treatment is required. Lithuania does not have an Echinococcus entry requirement for pets from listed countries.

        This is worth being clear on because the rule is strict and well-enforced for Norway arrivals. But it has no bearing on the Norway to Lithuania direction.
