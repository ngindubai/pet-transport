content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Suriname to the United Kingdom"
  overview: |
    Surinam Airways flies a Boeing 777 nonstop from Paramaribo to Amsterdam, several times a week. That single fact sets Suriname apart from Bolivia, Venezuela and most of the rest of South America on this site: there's no three-leg scramble to get a pet off the continent, just one long-haul sector and a short European connection into London.

    The regulatory side doesn't get the same shortcut. Suriname sits outside the UK's Part 1 and Part 2 listed-country groups, the unlisted category shared by nearly every other country on the continent. GOV.UK's own rabies risk map for terrestrial animals marks Suriname as high risk, consistent with that unlisted status. In practice that means a titre test, then a fixed three-month wait counted from the day blood is drawn, not the day the lab emails back a result. Budget four to five months from a standing start, even with the flight itself being the easy part of this route.

  sections:
    - heading: "One nonstop flight, unusual for this part of the world"
      body: |
        Johan Adolf Pengel International Airport (PBM), just outside Paramaribo, is Suriname's only international gateway, and Surinam Airways' own Amsterdam service is currently the sole nonstop link to Europe. No connection through Bogota, no transfer via Panama or Miami first: one long-haul sector, then a short hop from Amsterdam Schiphol into London on KLM or British Airways.

        That doesn't remove the planning work, it just concentrates it. Confirm cargo hold space on the Surinam Airways sector as early as you can, since there's no second carrier to fall back on if it's full or disrupted on your travel dates. Brachycephalic breeds face restrictions on the airline's own published policy, so a flat-faced dog or cat needs that confirmed well before the paperwork is even finished.

    - heading: "The three-month wait, and why the blood draw date is the one that counts"
      body: |
        The 90-day clock starts the day your vet draws blood for the titre test, not the day the laboratory confirms the pass. Get that date wrong in your planning and you'll miss your intended travel window by weeks, not days.

        Work backwards from a target date. Blood needs drawing at least three months and thirty days before you want to travel, and the rabies vaccination behind it needs at least 30 days of its own lead time before that blood draw. Four to five months isn't a padding figure here, it's close to the practical floor.

        One thing worth checking before assuming you're starting from zero: if your pet already holds a passing titre result from an earlier international move, and the rabies vaccination behind it hasn't lapsed, that result can still count. Ask your vet to check the dates rather than assuming a fresh test is needed.
