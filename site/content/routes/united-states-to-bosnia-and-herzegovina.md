  h1: "Pet Transport from the United States to Bosnia and Herzegovina"
  overview: |
    Search the USDA's own country-by-country pet travel pages for Bosnia and Herzegovina and you'll find a blank: APHIS says it hasn't been officially told what the country requires. That's not the same as no requirements existing. Bosnia and Herzegovina's Veterinary Office publishes its own list, and the United States sits in Part B of it, the same group as Canada, Japan and Australia.

    Part B status means no titre test and no waiting period beyond the standard 21 days after rabies vaccination. What it does mean is doing some of the verification work yourself, since the usual USDA shortcut doesn't cover this particular country.

    Flight-wise, there's no nonstop Sarajevo to US service, so plan on a connection through Istanbul or Frankfurt.
  sections:
    - heading: "Why USDA APHIS goes quiet on this one"
      body: |
        APHIS builds its country-specific pet travel pages from information foreign governments actively supply. Plenty of countries, including Bosnia and Herzegovina, haven't formally submitted that information to APHIS, so the US government page for this destination simply says the requirements aren't listed.

        That gap doesn't leave owners in the dark, though. Bosnia's own Veterinary Office publishes the rules directly: a two-part Annex IV list setting out which countries need a titre test and which don't. The United States is named in Part B (Official Gazette of Bosnia and Herzegovina No. 34/12 and 68/12), which puts it on the no-titre-test track alongside Canada and Australia.

    - heading: "What Part B actually asks for"
      body: |
        Part B's requirements are lighter than Part A's: microchip, a 21-day wait after rabies vaccination, and an animal health certificate from an official veterinarian, rather than the EU-passport-style document Part A countries are asked for. A standard USDA-endorsed international health certificate meets this in practice.

        Since APHIS itself hasn't confirmed the details, a direct check with the Veterinary Office of Bosnia and Herzegovina, or with a pet transport agent who has recently moved a pet on this route, is worth the half hour it takes. Rules that aren't published to USDA can still change without much notice.

    - heading: "Routing through Istanbul or Frankfurt"
      body: |
        No airline flies Sarajevo to the US directly. Turkish Airlines via Istanbul is the strongest option, with Turkish Cargo's dedicated Pet Lounge easing the connection and a wide US network on the other side. Lufthansa via Frankfurt is a solid alternative, and Frankfurt, not Munich, is the Lufthansa Group hub that actually handles a connecting cargo pet.

        Austrian Airlines via Vienna is workable too, with one caveat: Austrian's own transatlantic network has shrunk in recent years, so the US leg of a Vienna routing often ends up flown by a Star Alliance partner rather than Austrian itself. Confirm the operating carrier before booking if you go this way.
