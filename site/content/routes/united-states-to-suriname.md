content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from United States to Suriname"
  overview: |
    Type Suriname into APHIS's pet travel country list and nothing comes up. Confirmed directly against the site's own dropdown menu, Suriname simply isn't among the destinations with a dedicated APHIS export page, the way Venezuela, Colombia or most of the rest of South America are. That doesn't stop the move happening, it just means the USDA-accredited vet completing your export certificate has to research Suriname's current entry rules directly rather than working from a pre-built checklist.

    The Suriname side, once you get there, is one of the lighter imports this site tracks. No titre test from any origin, including the US. A current rabies vaccination and a vet-endorsed health certificate cover most of it. The trade-off is a stricter penalty than usual if something's missing: Suriname's guidance describes quarantine, return, or euthanasia for a non-compliant pet, not a temporary holding period.

  sections:
    - heading: "Working without a published checklist"
      body: |
        APHIS's standard advice for any destination is to contact a USDA-accredited vet as soon as travel is decided, and let that vet confirm the destination's current requirements before the International Health Certificate is written up. For most South American countries, that vet has an APHIS-published country page to check against. For Suriname, they don't.

        In practice this means the certificate gets built from Suriname's own published import guidance, which itself isn't especially detailed in English, cross-checked with whatever the vet can confirm directly. It's a more manual process than routes with an established APHIS page, though not a materially slower one, since Suriname's entry requirements themselves are light once confirmed.

    - heading: "A light import, with a hard edge if it goes wrong"
      body: |
        A dog or cat vaccinated in the US clears Suriname's rabies rule easily: any date between 21 days and 12 months before arrival satisfies it, no titre test needed. The health certificate has to be completed within 7 days of entry, which is the tightest deadline in the process, and it needs endorsement from a government veterinarian, which for US purposes means the USDA-accredited vet's APHIS-endorsed certificate.

        Where this route asks for more care than the paperwork alone suggests is the consequence of getting it wrong. Suriname's own guidance doesn't describe a fixed penalty-quarantine period the way, say, the UK does. It describes quarantine, return to the US, or euthanasia, at the owner's expense. Advance notice to Veterinary Services before the flight lands, and a certificate that's actually inside its 7-day window on arrival, are what keep this route in the "light" category rather than turning into a genuine problem at the airport.
