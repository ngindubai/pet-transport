content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Bolivia to the United States"
  overview: |
    A dog leaving Bolivia for the US faces a materially harder path than a cat does. Bolivia sits on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies, last confirmed 15 April 2026, which triggers a titer test, a mandatory reservation at one of just six CDC-registered animal care facilities nationwide, and an entry restricted to the airport where that facility sits. A cat, by contrast, faces no federal rabies paperwork at all.

    The flight itself is the one part of this route with a genuinely direct option. Boliviana de Aviación runs a nonstop service from Santa Cruz Viru Viru straight into Miami, one of the six approved facility airports, which removes a layer of connection risk that most South American routes to the US do not have.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why dogs and cats are treated so differently"
      body: |
        The CDC's rules since the 2024 overhaul are built entirely around dog rabies risk. Cats simply are not covered by that framework, so a cat travelling from Bolivia to the US needs a general health certificate and normal airline paperwork, nothing more at the federal level. Some US states ask for proof of rabies vaccination once a cat is resident, so check the destination state separately, but that is a state matter, not a border one.

        Dogs carry the full weight of the high-risk-country rules: age at least 12 weeks for the first vaccination, that vaccination given at least 28 days before entry, a titer drawn at least 30 days after vaccination and at least 28 days before entry, and a completed CDC Dog Import Form producing a single-entry receipt. Miss any one element and the dog either gets turned back at the facility or, more commonly, ends up in the 28-day quarantine track instead of walking straight through.

    - heading: "One direct flight, six approved airports"
      body: |
        BoA's Santa Cruz to Miami service is currently the only nonstop link between Bolivia and the US, and it happens to land at Miami, one of the six US airports carrying a CDC-registered animal care facility (the others are JFK, Atlanta, Los Angeles, Philadelphia and Washington Dulles). For a dog, that alignment matters: book anywhere else on a connecting itinerary and you have to make sure the connection actually terminates at one of those six specific airports, not just wherever is cheapest or fastest.

        Copa Airlines through Panama City and Avianca through Bogotá both serve Santa Cruz and connect onward into the US, useful if Miami is not your final destination, but each adds a second carrier's pet policy to check and a second leg where a live animal transfer needs to go right. For most owners moving to the US East Coast, the BoA direct service into Miami is the simpler choice precisely because it removes that second leg.
