content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from the United Arab Emirates to Macau"
  overview: |
    Two separate 30-day clocks govern this route, and they don't start ticking at the same time. The UAE's own MOCCAE export permit is valid for 30 days from issue. Macau's IAM import licence for live animals, PS-1092, is valid for exactly the same span. Apply for either one too early, against a flight date that later shifts, and it can expire before the paperwork on the other side is even finished.

    Everything underneath that timing problem is familiar from Macau's other unlisted-origin routes. The UAE isn't classed as rabies-free, so the titre test applies in full: blood drawn at least 30 days after vaccination, then roughly three months' wait before the pet can land in Macau. And there's no direct flight either. Every pet clears Hong Kong first, currently via Emirates SkyCargo or Etihad Airways rather than Cathay Pacific, whose Dubai route remains grounded.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why the UAE doesn't get a shortcut on the titre test"
      body: |
        IAM's default position treats the titre test as mandatory unless a pet arrives from a country with no rabies cases in the past two years. Published summaries of that guidance go further: even countries widely treated as rabies-free elsewhere, the UK and Australia included, still face the test under Macau's rules. That's a stricter bar than most owners expect.

        The UAE has an active rabies control programme but isn't classed as rabies-free by any standard comparable to what IAM applies elsewhere. Nothing published treats the UAE as a special case, better or worse than the UK or Australia. Given that, the titre test should be budgeted for as a certainty on this route, not a possibility to check late.

    - heading: "Two permits, two 30-day windows, one shared deadline"
      body: |
        MOCCAE's export permit and IAM's PS-1092 import licence both run out 30 days after they're issued, a coincidence that makes sequencing the whole point of planning this route well. Book the flight first. Then apply for both permits against that confirmed date, close enough together that neither one is sitting unused while the other is still being processed.

        Get this backward, applying for either permit before a flight is locked in, and the likely outcome is a lapsed document and a second application, not a missed flight. It's an administrative delay rather than a legal problem, but it still costs weeks on a route that already runs four to six months end to end.

    - heading: "Hong Kong, not Dubai, is where this route's carrier problem lives"
      body: |
        Macau International Airport has no long-haul cargo capacity, so the practical routing is Dubai or Abu Dhabi to Hong Kong, then a road or ferry transfer across the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macau Bridge. Emirates SkyCargo covers the Dubai leg on its own aircraft, including a newly converted Boeing 777-300ERSF freighter with extra volume. Etihad Airways serves Abu Dhabi to Hong Kong five times a week, useful if the pet's registration sits with ADAFSA rather than Dubai Municipality.

        Cathay Pacific, the carrier that would otherwise be the obvious third option, isn't currently flying Dubai to Hong Kong at all. It suspended the route in February 2026 after Middle East airspace disruption, and passenger service isn't due back until 1 September 2026, with no announced date for a dedicated Dubai freighter restart. Check its status again nearer your travel date. It may open back up within the life of this guide, but don't plan around it yet.
