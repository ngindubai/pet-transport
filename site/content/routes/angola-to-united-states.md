content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Angola to United States"
  overview: |
    Two things make Angola to USA one of the more demanding routes on this site. Angola sits on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies. And there is currently no direct flight, or even a single-carrier route, between the two countries.

    Dogs need either a valid titre test or a 28-day stay at a CDC-registered facility on landing. TAAG Angola Airlines has talked publicly about a future Luanda to Houston service, but it needs Angola to first get an FAA Category 1 safety rating, and nothing is flying yet. Every pet moving this route today goes indirectly, through Europe or Ethiopia.

    Cats have it much easier. The federal rules below apply to dogs; a cat travelling this route faces almost none of it.
  sections:
    - heading: "The titre test versus the facility stay"
      body: |
        Because Angola is CDC high-risk, your dog needs a valid rabies serology titre, drawn at least 30 days after vaccination and at least 28 days before arriving in the US, or it goes straight into a 28-day quarantine at a CDC-registered animal care facility on landing.

        Most owners choose the titre route. It costs more up front in lab and shipping fees but avoids a month-long facility stay, the welfare impact of it, and the constraint that comes with the facility route: your flight has to land at one of the specific airports where a registered facility exists. Plan the titre test timeline first; everything else on this page fits around it.

    - heading: "Why there's no direct flight, and what that means for booking"
      body: |
        TAAG Angola Airlines does not currently fly to the United States. The airline has said it wants to launch a Luanda to Houston route, reportedly targeting 2027, but that is contingent on Angola receiving an FAA Category 1 aviation safety rating, which has not yet happened. There is no confirmed launch date, so build your plan around the routes that exist today, not the one that might exist later.

        In practice, that means routing through a European or African hub with an onward US service: Lufthansa via Frankfurt, TAP Air Portugal via Lisbon, or Ethiopian Airlines via Addis Ababa, which has long operated to Washington Dulles. Each adds a transit leg and a second airline's rules to check. A pet transport agent who has actually booked this corridor before is worth the fee here, because there is little public track record to go on.

  - question: "Is Angola classed as high-risk or low-risk for pets entering the USA?"
    answer: "High-risk, for dogs. Angola is on the CDC's high-risk countries for dog rabies list (checked 17 July 2026, page last updated by the CDC on 15 April 2026), which triggers the titre-test-or-quarantine rule. Cats face no equivalent classification and no federal requirements at all."
  - question: "Is there a direct flight from Angola to the USA?"
    answer: "Not currently. TAAG Angola Airlines has discussed launching a Luanda to Houston service, targeted for around 2027, but it depends on Angola gaining an FAA Category 1 safety rating first, and no launch date is confirmed. Every pet travelling this route today goes via a connecting hub such as Frankfurt, Lisbon, or Addis Ababa."
  - question: "What happens if my dog doesn't have a titre test?"
    answer: "It goes to a CDC-registered animal care facility for 28 days on arrival, entirely at your expense, and the flight must land at an airport where one of these facilities is based. The facility may administer a US-licensed rabies vaccine and run its own titre test during the stay, with the possibility of early release if the requirements are met before the 28 days are up."
  - question: "Does my cat face the same high-risk process as a dog?"
    answer: "No. The CDC's high-risk classification only applies to dogs. A cat travelling from Angola to the US has no federal titre test, no quarantine, and no CDC form to complete. It still needs the standard export paperwork on the Angola side and airline cargo documentation, but the US border process for a healthy cat is minimal."
  - question: "How long should I allow for this route?"
    answer: "16 to 24 weeks is a realistic range if you're doing the titre test. That figure covers the mandatory 30-day-then-28-day waiting periods around the blood draw, the ISV certificate process, and the reality that cargo bookings on this corridor take longer to confirm than on a well-travelled route. Add time if you're weighing up the facility route instead, since that decision has its own knock-on effects on which airport you can fly into."
