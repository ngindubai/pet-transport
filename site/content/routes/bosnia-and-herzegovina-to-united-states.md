  h1: "Pet Transport from Bosnia and Herzegovina to United States"
  overview: |
    Not every route on this site is complicated. This one mostly isn't.

    Bosnia and Herzegovina does not appear on the CDC's high-risk countries for dog rabies list. That means a dog travelling from Sarajevo to the US needs nothing more than a microchip, a completed CDC Dog Import Form, and a general veterinary health certificate for the airline. No titre test. No quarantine. Rabies vaccination is recommended, not mandatory. A cat needs even less: essentially no federal paperwork at all.

    The main planning task is the flight, not the regulations. There's no nonstop Sarajevo to US service, so expect to connect via Istanbul, Frankfurt, or Vienna. Six to ten weeks is a comfortable timeline for most pets.
  sections:
    - heading: "What low-risk status actually removes"
      body: |
        The CDC splits dog imports into two tracks based on where the dog has spent the last six months: high-risk or low-risk. Bosnia and Herzegovina sits in the low-risk group. That single fact removes the titre test, the 28-day facility quarantine option, and the Foreign Vaccination certification form that high-risk-origin dogs need.

        What's left is the CDC Dog Import Form, a free online form valid for six months, plus a microchip and, in practice, a current rabies vaccination for the airline's sake even though the CDC itself doesn't demand one for a low-risk-origin dog. Cats have no equivalent federal process whatsoever, high-risk or low-risk.

    - heading: "Routing through Istanbul, Frankfurt, or Vienna"
      body: |
        No airline flies Sarajevo to the US directly. Turkish Airlines via Istanbul is a strong option, given Istanbul's scale as a hub and Turkish's wide US network including New York and Washington. Lufthansa via Frankfurt works well too, and Frankfurt is the group's cargo-pet-friendly hub, unlike Munich.

        Austrian Airlines via Vienna is worth a mention with a caveat: Austrian's own long-haul network to the US has shrunk over recent years, so the transatlantic leg of a Vienna-routed booking often continues on a Star Alliance partner rather than an Austrian-operated aircraft. Confirm the actual operating carrier for that leg before you commit, since pet policy can differ between the airline you booked and the one that flies the long leg.
faqs:
  - question: "Does my dog need a rabies titre test to enter the US from Bosnia and Herzegovina?"
    answer: "No. Bosnia and Herzegovina is not on the CDC's high-risk countries for dog rabies list, so your dog enters under the low-risk pathway: microchip, CDC Dog Import Form, and in practice a general vet health certificate for the airline. No titre test, no quarantine."
  - question: "Is rabies vaccination required for my dog?"
    answer: "Not by the CDC. For a dog coming from a low-risk country like Bosnia and Herzegovina, rabies vaccination is strongly recommended but not federally mandated. Almost every airline will still want proof of one, though, so don't skip it purely because the CDC rule doesn't require it."
  - question: "What does my cat need to enter the US?"
    answer: "Almost nothing at the federal level. Cats have no CDC or USDA APHIS import requirements for entry into the US. You'll still need airline paperwork and a general health certificate for the flight, and it's worth checking your destination US state for any state-level rules, but the federal process for a cat is close to nonexistent."
  - question: "Is there a direct flight from Sarajevo to the United States?"
    answer: "No. You'll connect through a European hub, most practically Istanbul with Turkish Airlines or Frankfurt with Lufthansa, both of which carry pets on the connecting leg. Vienna with Austrian Airlines is an option too, though the transatlantic leg on that routing often ends up operated by a Star Alliance partner rather than Austrian itself, so confirm the operating carrier before booking."
  - question: "How long should I allow for this move?"
    answer: "Six to ten weeks is realistic for most pets. There's no mandatory waiting period behind that figure, unlike high-risk-origin routes; it's mostly the time needed to book a pet-carrying connecting flight, get the airline's health certificate sorted close to travel, and complete the CDC Dog Import Form."
