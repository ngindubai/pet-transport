  sections:
    - heading: "USA vs UK: how the DAFF classification affects you"
      body: |
        Australia classifies origin countries into groups (Group 1 through Group 5) based on their rabies and disease risk profile. The United States is Group 4, while the UK is Group 3. In practice, the difference for pet owners is minimal: both groups require a titre test, a 180-day waiting period, a DAFF import permit, and a 10-day quarantine.

        The Group 4 classification means the import permit application process may require slightly more supporting documentation, and DAFF may take longer to process permits from Group 4 countries. Apply early: 6-8 weeks before travel is the minimum, and earlier is better.

        Cats and dogs from the USA face the same requirements. There is no federal US regulation on pet exports beyond the USDA-endorsed health certificate, so all the documentation complexity sits on the Australian side of the process.

    - heading: "The USDA APHIS health certificate and why it takes longer than you expect"
      body: |
        The health certificate for a USA to Australia pet move must be in DAFF-approved format, completed by a USDA-accredited veterinarian (not just any vet), and endorsed by USDA APHIS Veterinary Services.

        The USDA endorsement step takes 2-10 business days depending on how you submit. Electronic submission through the Veterinary Export Health Certification System (VEHCS) is faster; mail submission is slower. Given that the health certificate must be issued within 10 days of travel (the examination must fall within 10 days of the departure date), timing the USDA endorsement is important.

        Most owners use the following approach: vet examination on day 1 or 2 before travel, immediate electronic submission to USDA APHIS, with enough time buffer for endorsement to come back before departure. Discuss the process with your USDA-accredited vet before confirming your travel date.

    - heading: "The Mickleham quarantine facility"
      body: |
        All animals arriving in Australia complete quarantine at the Post Entry Quarantine facility in Mickleham, Victoria, approximately 30km north of Melbourne. There is currently no alternative facility; all pets entering Australia arrive in Melbourne regardless of their Australian destination.

        The 10-day stay is the minimum. Animals are held individually in climate-controlled kennels or cat units. The facility operates 365 days a year. Owners cannot visit during quarantine, but DAFF provides updates.

        Book your quarantine space as soon as your titre test result is confirmed satisfactory. Do not wait until you are finalising travel plans. Space fills up significantly during peak relocation periods, and arriving without a confirmed booking creates delays and costs.

faqs:
  - question: "Can my pet fly directly from the US to any Australian city or must it land in Melbourne?"
    answer: "All pets arriving in Australia must go through the quarantine facility at Mickleham, which is near Melbourne. There is no approved facility in any other Australian city. Your pet will fly to Melbourne (MEL or Tullamarine), complete the 10-day quarantine, and then you can arrange domestic transport to your final destination. If you are moving to Perth or Brisbane, factor in the additional domestic leg from Melbourne."
  - question: "My dog was vaccinated in the US with a US-licensed vaccine. Does this count for the Australian titre test?"
    answer: "Yes, provided the vaccination was properly administered, recorded, and the timing sequence is correct (microchip before vaccination, then at least 30 days before the titre test blood draw). The DAFF-approved laboratory will evaluate the titre test result regardless of where the vaccination was given. What matters is the titre result (0.5 IU/ml or above) and the timing documentation."
  - question: "I missed the 180-day mark because of a schedule change. Can I apply for an extension?"
    answer: "There are no extensions to the 180-day waiting period. If your travel date moves and you are within the 180-day window from your titre test, you must wait until the 180 days have passed. If your titre test result expires (titre tests are valid indefinitely if vaccination cover is maintained, but if vaccination lapses, a new test may be required), the timeline can restart. Keep your pet's rabies vaccination current throughout the waiting period."
  - question: "Do I need a US-side pet transport agent for this route?"
    answer: "It is strongly recommended. The combination of USDA-accredited vet, USDA APHIS endorsement, DAFF-format certificate, BICON permit application, airline cargo booking, and quarantine facility reservation is technically manageable alone but operationally complex. A single document error at any point can delay the move significantly. US-based agents who specialise in the Australian route typically charge $500 to $2,000 and coordinate all steps on your behalf."
  - question: "Are there any breeds Australia will not accept from the United States?"
    answer: "Australia bans the import of Pit Bull Terriers, Japanese Tosa, Dogo Argentino, Fila Brasileiro, and perros de presa Canario. These are absolute bans with no exemptions. A dog that substantially conforms to the type characteristics of a banned breed may also be refused, even without a formal breed certificate. Additional state-level breed restrictions may apply once the dog is in Australia."
  - question: "What is the cost difference between using an agent and doing it myself?"
    answer: "Agent fees for the US to Australia route typically run $500 to $2,000 on the US side, plus AUD 2,000 to AUD 4,000 for the Australian quarantine handling agent (usually required). The saving from not using an agent is rarely worth the risk on this particular route. Documentation errors that require the animal to be returned or held cost significantly more than the agent fee. Most experienced relocators on this corridor use an agent for both the US side and the Australian arrival process."
