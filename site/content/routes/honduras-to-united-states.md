content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Honduras to the United States"
  overview: |
    A current rabies certificate is not enough for a dog leaving Honduras for the US. Honduras sits on the CDC's high-risk countries list for dog rabies, last updated 15 April 2026, which pulls in a serology titre test, a government-endorsed Certification form, and a mandatory arrival at one of just six US airports with a CDC-registered animal care facility.

    Cats sidestep almost all of it. The CDC's high-risk-country requirements apply to dogs only, so a cat travels on a standard health certificate and the routine SENASA export paperwork, with nothing like the same lead time.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why the arrival airport matters more than the airline"
      body: |
        Three US carriers connect Honduras directly: American Airlines to Miami, Delta to Atlanta, and United to Houston. All three are real, year-round, bookable routes. Only two of them work for a dog.

        Miami and Atlanta both host a CDC-registered animal care facility. Houston does not. The CDC's rule is specific: a foreign-vaccinated dog from a high-risk country must land at the airport where the registered facility physically sits, not simply somewhere in the US with a connecting flight onward. Booking United because the fare looked better, without checking this first, is a genuine and common mistake on this route.

        For a cat, none of this applies. Houston, Miami or Atlanta all work equally well, since cats carry no CDC entry requirement at all.

    - heading: "The titre test clock, and why 28 days cuts both ways"
      body: |
        Two separate minimum gaps govern the timing here. The blood draw for the serology titre test cannot happen before 30 days have passed since the rabies vaccination. Separately, that same blood draw has to land at least 28 days before the dog actually enters the US.

        Add a realistic laboratory turnaround, usually three to five weeks once the sample reaches a CDC-approved lab, and the two windows barely overlap on paper. In practice, most owners are looking at ten to twelve weeks from vaccination to a workable travel date, not the eight-week theoretical minimum you'd get from stacking the bare rules alone.

    - heading: "SENASA's role sits alongside the CDC process, not inside it"
      body: |
        SENASA's own export certificate, the Certificado Zoosanitario de Exportación, is quick: applied for online through the SECEH platform for a flat Lps 450, usually approved within minutes. That speed can create false confidence. It confirms the animal is healthy and legally leaving Honduras. It says nothing about whether the CDC's titre test and Certification form requirements have been met on the US side.

        SENASA also has to endorse the separate Certification of Foreign Rabies Vaccination and Microchip form that the CDC requires, in the same role the Dominican Republic's DIGEGA plays for that country's high-risk dogs. Build time for this second SENASA interaction into the schedule; it is not covered by the routine export certificate application.

faqs:
  - question: "Is Honduras on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies?"
    answer: "Yes. Honduras appears on the CDC's high-risk countries list for dog rabies, current as of the list dated 15 April 2026. That triggers a mandatory rabies serology titre test, a Certification of Foreign Rabies Vaccination and Microchip form endorsed by SENASA, and arrival at a CDC-registered facility airport for any dog. Source: CDC, cdc.gov/importation/dogs/high-risk-countries.html."
  - question: "Which US airports can accept a dog arriving from Honduras?"
    answer: "Only the six airports with a CDC-registered animal care facility: Atlanta (ATL), Los Angeles (LAX), Miami (MIA), New York JFK, Philadelphia (PHL) and Washington Dulles (IAD). American Airlines flies both San Pedro Sula and Palmerola into Miami, and Delta flies San Pedro Sula into Atlanta, so both carriers work. United's Houston service does not, since Houston has no CDC-registered facility."
  - question: "Does a cat need the same CDC paperwork as a dog?"
    answer: "No. The CDC's high-risk-country requirements, including the titre test and the facility-airport rule, apply to dogs only. A cat travels from Honduras on a standard SENASA export certificate and health documentation, with a much shorter lead time."
  - question: "How long does the whole process take?"
    answer: "Budget 10 to 14 weeks for a dog from a standing start. That covers the 30-day gap before the titre blood draw, laboratory turnaround of 3 to 5 weeks, the 28-day minimum before entry that the result has to clear, and time for SENASA's Certification form endorsement and the CDC facility reservation. A cat can move considerably faster, closer to the standard SENASA export timeline of a few weeks."
