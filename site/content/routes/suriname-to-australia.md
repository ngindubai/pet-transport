content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Suriname to Australia"
  overview: |
    Suriname has no DAFF group of its own, not Group 1, not Group 6, nothing. A pet leaving Paramaribo for Australia has to live somewhere else first, for at least 180 unbroken days, before the Australian import process even begins. That's the fixed reality of this route, and it doesn't shift with better paperwork or an earlier booking.

    What makes Suriname's case a little more workable than some other non-approved countries this site covers is that the two realistic staging choices, the United Kingdom and the United States, are both routes already documented here in full. The UK is DAFF Group 3, which means a titre test for the Australia leg but no further wait once the 180 days are up. The US is Group 4, which adds its own extra 180-day wait counted from the titre blood draw. Budget 34 to 48 weeks depending on which one you pick, and read the relevant staging-country route on this site before you commit to either.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why there's no direct route, and no way around the 180 days"
      body: |
        DAFF splits countries into approved groups, numbered 1 through 6 by rising rabies risk, and everyone else. Suriname sits in the everyone-else category, the same as most of the rest of South America. There's no flight combination or paperwork shortcut that gets a Suriname-origin pet straight into Australia; DAFF requires 180 consecutive days of residency in an approved country first, checked against veterinary and travel records for the entire period.

        This has nothing to do with Suriname's own light import rules, its lack of a titre test requirement, or anything else about how Suriname treats arriving pets. It's purely about how DAFF classifies Suriname as an origin for pets coming into Australia, and that classification is unrelated to how easy or hard Suriname makes things for pets arriving there.

    - heading: "Choosing between the UK and the US, using what's already on this site"
      body: |
        Both staging options have a full route already written up here: Suriname to the United Kingdom, and Suriname to the United States. Reading either before deciding is worth doing, because the staging country's own entry process runs on its own separate timeline from the 180-day DAFF clock, not folded into it.

        The UK route means clearing the UK's unlisted-country pathway first, a titre test and a fixed three-month wait, before the 180-day DAFF residency clock even starts ticking usefully. Once it's done, Group 3 status means no extra DAFF wait after the RNATT test. The US route means clearing the CDC's high-risk-country pathway on entry, then, once staged, Group 4 status adds its own further 180-day wait from the RNATT blood draw. On paper the UK is the faster combination. In practice, existing ties, a job, family, a lease already in place, often decide it for people rather than the raw timeline.

    - heading: "Mickleham, and the last mile"
      body: |
        Whichever staging country the 180 days are spent in, the Australian end is identical: a mandatory minimum 10-day stay at Mickleham Post Entry Quarantine Facility in Melbourne, the country's only approved facility for imported cats and dogs, and the only Australian airport that accepts pet imports at all. Book the slot once the staging country's own export certificate date is confirmed, and plan the final flight to land on a weekday, since Mickleham doesn't take weekend arrivals.
