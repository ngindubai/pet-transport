  h1: "Pet Transport from Canada to Kosovo"
  overview: |
    No form exists. That's the practical starting point for this route: Kosovo has never negotiated a bilateral export certificate with Canada, so instead of a Kosovo-specific document, the CFIA issues its own Canadian International Health Certificate and asks the accredited vet to confirm it still meets Kosovo's stated conditions.

    Those conditions are lighter than they might sound. No titre test, no import permit, current guidance says, regardless of where the pet started its journey. The one hard rule worth flagging on its own is Echinococcus treatment for dogs, a tapeworm requirement that has to be recorded on the certificate rather than assumed.

    Five to eight weeks covers most pets, once a connecting flight through Istanbul, Vienna or Zurich is booked and the CFIA appointment is confirmed.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Kosovo uses a Canadian form instead of its own"
      body: |
        CFIA export certification generally follows a negotiated template agreed between Canada and the destination country, something the UK, US, EU member states and Australia all have in place. Kosovo doesn't, a gap that mirrors the same absence found when a Canadian pet heads to Bosnia and Herzegovina. Rather than leaving the process undefined, CFIA substitutes its own Canadian International Health Certificate, provided that document, once completed, still satisfies whatever Kosovo's Food and Veterinary Agency actually asks for.

        That substitution is routine for CFIA, not a workaround invented for this route specifically, but it does mean double-checking the certificate's content against Kosovo's stated conditions before the appointment, rather than trusting a template built for a different country.

    - heading: "Tapeworm treatment: the one step that isn't optional"
      body: |
        Kosovo doesn't ask for a rabies titre test from any origin country, a genuine break from the stricter unlisted-country protocols this site documents elsewhere. What it does ask for, specifically for dogs, is Echinococcus treatment, recorded on the health certificate as a distinct line rather than folded into the general vaccination record.

        Time it at the same vet visit that produces the export certificate, and confirm the vet has actually written the treatment down. A dog that received the treatment but has no documented record of it hasn't met the condition as far as Kosovo's officials are concerned.

    - heading: "Getting from a Canadian gateway to Pristina"
      body: |
        Nothing flies Toronto or Montreal to Pristina directly. Turkish Airlines' nonstop Toronto-Istanbul service, six times a week, connects to Turkish's own Istanbul-Pristina route, keeping the pet with a single carrier's cargo network bar the change of aircraft. Austrian Airlines runs a similar shape of journey through Vienna, also served nonstop from Toronto.

        Swiss International Air Lines covers the Zurich leg for an owner travelling with a small pet in the cabin, but its cargo division doesn't currently deliver unaccompanied pets to Kosovo, so a larger dog needs Turkish or Austrian instead.
