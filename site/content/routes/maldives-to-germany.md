content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Maldives to Germany: 2026 Guide"
  overview: |
    Condor now flies Frankfurt to Male four times a week through the summer season, a fact that surprises most people planning this move and helps precisely none of them. The aircraft carries holidaymakers, not manifest cargo, and its capacity for a live animal in the hold is neither advertised nor reliable. The actual move still runs through a cargo agent and, in most cases, a Gulf hub.

    What doesn't change with the new flight is the paperwork. Germany treats the Maldives as an EU-unlisted country, so a cat needs the full sequence: microchip, rabies jab, an FAVN titre test at an EU-approved lab, and a 90-day wait counted from the day blood was drawn, not the day the result lands in an inbox.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why this is a cat move, and why that's not the same as a dog ban"
      body: |
        Nothing in Maldivian law stops a resident dog from leaving the country. What stops most owners from even asking the question is that dogs cannot legally be brought into the Maldives to begin with, under the Maldives Customs Service's list of prohibited imports. Very few end up living there as a result, which is why almost every genuine enquiry on this route concerns a cat, usually belonging to a resort worker or an expat wrapping up a posting.
    - heading: "Getting a health certificate with no government office to sign it"
      body: |
        There's no export permit to apply for and no ministry counter-signature built into the Maldivian side. A private vet in Male writes the health certificate, and most airlines and agents accept it as issued. The harder part is finding cargo capacity, since Velana International moves far more resort supplies than live pets.
    - heading: "The FAVN titre test and the 90-day trap"
      body: |
        Germany applies Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2026/131 to arrivals from countries outside Implementing Regulation (EU) 2026/636's approved list, and the Maldives sits outside it. Blood drawn at least 30 days post-vaccination has to clear 0.5 IU/ml at an EU-approved lab, and only then does a 90-day clock start, counted from the draw itself. Miss that detail and a flight gets booked before the cat is legally allowed to travel.
    - heading: "Condor, and the routing that actually works"
      body: |
        If Condor's cargo desk confirms live animal capacity for your dates, it would in theory be the shortest path from Male straight into Frankfurt. Until that's confirmed in writing, plan around the established pattern instead: Qatar Airways via Doha, with its dedicated Hamad International animal facility, or a Lufthansa connection landing at Frankfurt's own Animal Lounge.

  - question: "Can Condor's new Frankfurt-Male flight carry my cat?"
    answer: "Possibly, but don't assume it. Condor's service is built for passenger holiday traffic, and its live animal cargo capacity on this specific route is limited and unconfirmed. Check directly with Condor Cargo before ruling out the standard Doha or Dubai cargo routing."
  - question: "Can my dog leave the Maldives to move to Germany?"
    answer: "Legally, yes. There is no export restriction on dogs leaving the Maldives, only an import restriction on dogs entering it. In practice almost nobody has a dog living there because of that import ban, which is why this guide, like the rest of ours, is written around a cat."
  - question: "Why does the 90-day wait matter more than the vaccination date?"
    answer: "Because it's the step people miscalculate. The 90 days run from the date blood is drawn for the titre test, not from vaccination and not from the day the lab emails a result. Get that date wrong and a booked flight can arrive before the pet is legally allowed to."
  - question: "Is there quarantine on arrival in Germany?"
    answer: "No, for a cat that completes every step correctly and holds a valid EU-format certificate. Incomplete documentation can mean the animal is held, quarantined at the owner's cost, or refused entry."
