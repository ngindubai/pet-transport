        Athens has no long-haul service of its own to Seoul, and Aegean Airlines, the national carrier, flies no widebody aircraft capable of reaching East Asia. That rules out any Athens-based direct claim.

        Turkish Airlines changes the calculation. Istanbul sits roughly two hours from Athens by air, and from there Turkish Airlines runs a nonstop service to Incheon at around 10 hours, one of the more direct East Asia connections available from this part of Europe. Turkish Cargo operates a dedicated live-animal handling unit at Istanbul with veterinary staff on site around the clock, which matters when your pet is transiting rather than starting its journey there.

        The alternative, routing via Frankfurt or Amsterdam to connect with Korean Air, adds a longer feeder leg and a longer overall transit time.     - heading: "The Greek certificate: official vet, Ministry endorsement, ten-day clock"
      body: |
        South Korea does not accept the EU pet passport for this route. Instead, you need a Korean QIAS-format certificate, issued by a Greek official veterinarian and endorsed by the Directorate General of Veterinary Services at the Ministry of Rural Development and Food. This is a different document from the one you'd use to move a pet within the EU, and mixing the two up is the single most common error on this corridor.

        The certificate is valid for 10 days from issue, so timing the appointment close to your travel date matters more than getting it done early. Official vets who handle third-country export certification are concentrated in Athens and Thessaloniki; if you're moving from one of the islands, build in extra time to reach the mainland for this step.

        On arrival, QIAS holds EU-origin pets for inspection, typically 1 to 3 days, shorter than the quarantine applied to pets from rabies-endemic non-EU countries but not something you can plan around avoiding entirely.
