content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from the Bahamas to the United States"
  overview: |
    A dog leaving Nassau for Florida needs one document from the US government, and it's free. The CDC doesn't classify the Bahamas as high-risk for dog rabies, so the titre test, the quarantine choice and the CDC-registered facility reservation that complicate so many other routes on this site simply don't apply here.

    That's a sharp contrast with the reverse direction. A UK-bound Bahamian pet has to clear a rabies titre test and a 3-month wait; heading to the US instead, the same dog just needs a form that costs nothing to file. A cat needs even less.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why this route carries almost no federal paperwork"
      body: |
        The CDC's dog import framework hinges entirely on where a dog has spent the last 6 months. Countries on its high-risk list trigger a titre test or a quarantine stay; everywhere else, including the Bahamas, a dog needs only a free CDC Dog Import Form, filed online before travel.

        Cats face an even shorter list. USDA APHIS has no federal animal-health requirement for cats entering the US at all, and the CDC's own guidance for cats is minimal. A cat leaving the Bahamas needs Bahamian export paperwork and nothing further at the American border.

    - heading: "The flight is the easy part, mostly"
      body: |
        Nassau sits close enough to Florida that American Airlines runs a genuinely frequent schedule into Miami, with small pets travelling in the cabin and larger dogs moving through American PetEmbark's cargo service. Delta and JetBlue both serve Nassau too, but carry pets in the cabin only on every route they fly, with no cargo alternative for a bigger animal.

        Bahamasair, the national carrier, also flies into Florida, though its own pet cargo policy for this specific route isn't published with the same detail as the major US carriers. Confirm directly before assuming a booking will accept a pet.

    - heading: "What Florida might still ask for"
      body: |
        The CDC's light federal requirements don't override whatever the destination US state expects. Florida, the typical port of entry from Nassau, commonly asks for a certificate of veterinary inspection issued by an accredited vet, a state-level rule that sits entirely outside CDC's own framework.

        It's a smaller hurdle than anything on the high-risk-country routes elsewhere on this site, but skipping it on the assumption that the CDC form covers everything is a genuine way this otherwise simple route trips up.

    answer: "No. The Bahamas is not on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies, so the titre-test-or-quarantine choice that applies to high-risk-country dogs doesn't apply here. A current rabies vaccination is recommended but not federally required."
  - question: "Is the CDC Dog Import Form free?"
    answer: "Yes. It costs nothing and is completed online before travel. It's still mandatory for every dog, even from a low-risk country like the Bahamas, and there is no fee-based way to skip it."

    answer: "Almost nothing at the federal level. USDA APHIS has no animal-health import requirement for cats, and the CDC's guidance is minimal. A cat needs the standard Bahamian export health certificate and whatever the booked airline requires for boarding."

    answer: "Yes, through American Airlines' PetEmbark cargo service, currently the clearest option on this route for a dog too large for a cabin booking. Delta and JetBlue both fly Nassau to the US but carry pets in the cabin only, with no cargo alternative."

    answer: "Yes. Florida, the usual port of entry from Nassau, can require its own certificate of veterinary inspection from an accredited vet. This sits outside the CDC's federal framework, so it's worth confirming with Florida's department of agriculture rather than assuming the CDC form is the only document needed."
