content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Georgia to Singapore"
  overview: |
    Georgia has a reputation, on this site and elsewhere, as one of the more manageable Caucasus origins. Its own NFA export process is genuinely simple, and British Airways flies direct from Tbilisi to London without any of the drama Armenia or Azerbaijan routes involve. None of that reputation survives contact with Singapore's AVS.

    Georgia isn't named on Schedule I or Schedule II, so it defaults to the full Schedule III sequence: titre test, 90-day wait, 30 days of quarantine on arrival. Confirmed against avs.nparks.gov.sg, fetched 21 July 2026. Budget close to five months, regardless of how straightforward the Georgian side of the paperwork turns out to be.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Georgia's easy reputation doesn't carry over"
      body: |
        Georgia's National Food Agency runs a lighter export process than its Caucasus neighbours, with no separate export permit and a comparatively fast certificate turnaround. That reputation, earned on routes into the UK and elsewhere, means nothing to AVS Singapore, which classifies by its own criteria and has never named Georgia on either favourable schedule.

        The result is a route where the origin-side paperwork is genuinely quick, but the destination-side requirements, titre test, 90-day wait, 30-day AQC quarantine, dominate the timeline regardless.

    - heading: "The lab problem, again"
      body: |
        Georgian vets routinely ship UK-bound titre samples to labs in Turkey or Germany, since neither is far and both accept international submissions. For Singapore, only the Germany route survives. Turkey doesn't appear on AVS's Schedule I or II list, so a Turkish lab result won't satisfy AVS's requirement, however convenient the shipping logistics.

        Confirm your chosen laboratory's status against the current Schedule II list before drawing blood. Getting this wrong means repeating the test and restarting a 28-day and then 90-day clock.

    - heading: "Tbilisi to Changi: two workable single-connection routes"
      body: |
        Turkish Airlines has strong frequency out of Tbilisi and connects into the Istanbul-Singapore nonstop sector that Turkish Airlines and Singapore Airlines both fly roughly 17 times a week combined. That makes Istanbul the natural hub choice.

        Qatar Airways via Doha is the established alternative, with a solid reputation on Caucasus-to-Asia cargo routes generally.
