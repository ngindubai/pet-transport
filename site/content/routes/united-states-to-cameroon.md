  h1: "Pet Transport from United States to Cameroon"
  overview: |
    An electronic signature isn't enough for a pet leaving the US for Cameroon. The health certificate needs a wet ink endorsement from a USDA APHIS office, embossed on the physical paper, which means the document itself has to travel there and back before your dog or cat does. It's a small procedural detail with a real effect on the calendar.

    Everything downstream of that is more straightforward. No titre test applies, and there's no formal Cameroonian import permit for a companion pet. What there is, instead, is a 30-day rabies vaccination window, a breed restriction on wolf hybrids and two cat breeds, and a routing that always transits through Addis Ababa, Paris, Brussels or Istanbul, since no flight runs direct.

    MINEPIA, Cameroon's livestock ministry, checks documents and the animal itself at Douala on arrival. Get the ink endorsement moving early, since it's the one step in this process that runs on paper rather than email.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why ink beats a PDF on this particular route"
      body: |
        Most USDA health certificates these days move electronically. Cameroon is one of the destinations where that shortcut doesn't apply: the certificate needs an original ink signature and an embossed seal from a USDA APHIS office, not a scanned or digitally signed copy.

        In practice, that means the physical certificate has to be mailed or couriered to an APHIS office, endorsed, and mailed back, before it's valid for travel. Where a 3-to-5 business day electronic turnaround is standard on many other routes, this one runs on postal time as well as processing time. Start the certificate process earlier than you would for a more typical destination, and use a courier service you can track.

    - heading: "No official word from Cameroon beyond the certificate form"
      body: |
        USDA APHIS is candid about the limits of what it can confirm: Cameroon hasn't given the agency a full country-specific import protocol, only the certificate form itself. That doesn't mean there's nothing else to check; it means the US side of the paperwork can't fully answer questions about Cameroon's own entry conditions.

        A relocation agent with recent Douala experience, or direct contact with MINEPIA, fills that gap. Don't treat a completed USDA certificate as proof the Cameroon side is settled; it's one part of two separate government processes.

    - heading: "Routing through Addis Ababa, Paris, Brussels or Istanbul"
      body: |
        Ethiopian Airlines' connection from Washington Dulles through Addis Ababa to Douala is a strong option for pet cargo, given Ethiopian Cargo's established live animal handling at its Addis hub. Air France via Paris, Brussels Airlines via Brussels, and Turkish Airlines via Istanbul round out the realistic choices, each adding its own transit conditions worth checking before booking.

        Whichever carrier you use, confirm live animal acceptance on both flight segments in writing. A routing that looks fine on a flight search tool doesn't always mean the airline's cargo division has confirmed space for an animal on that specific date.
