  overview: |
    A cat leaving Mahe for Heathrow does not face a single day in quarantine, provided the blood sample behind its titre test is already three months old by the time it lands. That single number, ninety days from a vet's needle to UK arrival, sets the shape of this entire route, more than the flight itself does.

    Seychelles' own rabies-free status counts for nothing on the UK side. APHA treats the islands as an unlisted third country, the same bracket as far larger, higher-risk nations, so the paperwork runs on UK time, not Seychelles reputation.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why rabies-free Seychelles still needs the full titre process"
      body: |
        It seems backwards at first. Seychelles keeps rabies out through strict controls on animals coming in, including mandatory quarantine for every arrival regardless of origin. But the UK's pet travel rules classify countries by whether they are formally listed under the scheme, not by their actual disease status, and Seychelles isn't on that list. A titre test taken too early, before the 30-day mark after vaccination, invalidates the whole timeline and forces a repeat.

    - heading: "The Abu Dhabi and Dubai routings"
      body: |
        Air Seychelles carries small pets in the hold as accompanied baggage, useful if an owner is flying with the animal, but it isn't set up to manifest an unaccompanied relocation shipment on its own network. Etihad Cargo via Abu Dhabi and Emirates SkyCargo via Dubai are the two hubs actually built for this: both run dedicated live-animal facilities and both serve Mahe on a regular schedule, which matters when a crate needs a genuine rest stop mid-journey rather than a rushed transfer.

    - heading: "Commercial versus non-commercial: the Balai trap"
      body: |
        If the owner isn't travelling within five days of the pet, GB customs treats the move as a commercial import under the Balai Directive. That means a different certificate, a mandatory Border Control Post entry point, and pre-notification through the UK's import system. Most relocation companies plan for this from day one rather than discovering it at the airport.
