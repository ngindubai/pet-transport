  h1: "Pet Transport from the USA to Paraguay"
  overview: |
    SENACSA will not admit certain breeds into Paraguay at all. American Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Cane Corso, and several mastiff types are on that list, and no amount of extra paperwork changes it. That single fact makes breed the first thing to check on this route, well before flights or vet appointments.

    For breeds that are permitted, the process is comparatively light. No titre test applies on either side of this move; Paraguay only asks for one on shipments continuing to the EU. What replaces it is a USDA-accredited health certificate on the US side and SENACSA's own CVI and clinical inspection on arrival, plus a hard cap of 5 pets per non-commercial shipment.

    Getting from a US city to Asuncion means at least one connection. Copa Airlines, through Panama City, keeps the whole journey on a single carrier's cargo network, which most agents book ahead of the Sao Paulo alternative.

  sections:
    - heading: "The breed ban comes first, not last"
      body: |
        Most pet transport routes treat breed restrictions as a footnote next to vaccination schedules and flight bookings. On this route it has to come first. SENACSA's banned list, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, American Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, American Bully, Fila Brasileiro, Dogo Argentino, Tibetan Mastiff, Neapolitan Mastiff, Dogue de Bordeaux, Boerboel, Bullmastiff and Cane Corso, applies at the border regardless of how complete the rest of the paperwork is.

        A mixed-breed dog with any visible ancestry from these types can also draw scrutiny at inspection, so owners of a dog that resembles a banned breed, even without papers confirming it, should expect questions and should have a plan for how to answer them before travel, not at the airport.

        Airlines add a second, separate layer. Copa refuses several breeds as cargo on its own terms, some of which overlap with SENACSA's list and some of which do not. Clearing the Paraguayan import rules does not guarantee a carrier will fly the dog at all.

    - heading: "Why the paperwork is lighter than a European move"
      body: |
        No titre test, no three-month wait: those two facts alone put this route well ahead of a US-to-UK or US-to-EU move in terms of timeline. SENACSA's requirements settle into three items, a current rabies vaccination, the core vaccine panel, and a CVI built from a US-issued health certificate.

        The catch is that APHIS itself says Paraguay has not formally told the US government what it requires, which is unusual for an established pet transport corridor. The practical answer, confirmed by SENACSA's own published guidance, is to get a full USDA-accredited certificate endorsed through VEHCS anyway. It costs a little more and takes a little longer than skipping the endorsement, but it is the document SENACSA checks hardest against the microchip and vaccination record on arrival, and there is no upside to arriving with a lighter file than the destination authority expects.
