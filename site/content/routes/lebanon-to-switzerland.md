  sections:
    - heading: "FSVO advance permission: the step that catches people out"
      body: |
        Most EU destinations on this route only need the standard unlisted-country paperwork completed correctly and shown at the border. Switzerland is different. Before your pet can travel at all, you need explicit written permission from the FSVO, the Swiss federal food safety and veterinary office. Apply at least three weeks before the date you plan to fly.

        This sits on top of, not instead of, the titre test sequence: microchip before vaccination, a 30-day wait, blood drawn for an FAVN or equivalent test at an approved lab, and a result of at least 0.5 IU/ml.

        Switzerland calls the following wait a three-month period rather than 90 days, though in practice it works out close to the same length of time, counted from the date of the blood draw, not the result. Miss the FSVO application step and none of the rest matters. Without that written permission in hand, Swiss border authorities will not admit the animal, regardless of how clean the titre test and health certificate are. Build the FSVO application into your very first week of planning, not your last.

    - heading: "Flying from Beirut to Zurich or Geneva"
      body: |
        Lufthansa Cargo via Frankfurt is the shortest overall routing, using the Frankfurt Animal Lounge before a quick onward hop to Zurich. Turkish Airlines Cargo via Istanbul offers a wider network and can be the better option when Frankfurt capacity is tight. Emirates SkyCargo via Dubai reaches either Zurich or Geneva, though the transit is longer than the European hub routes. Qatar Airways Cargo via Doha into Geneva is a workable Gulf alternative, but summer heat restrictions at Gulf hubs can affect live animal bookings. Confirm both the FSVO permission and your titre test result before booking any of these, since Swiss customs will check the FSVO letter alongside the health certificate on arrival.

faqs:
  - question: "What is FSVO advance permission and why does only Switzerland need it?"
    answer: "It is written authorisation from the Swiss Federal Food Safety and Veterinary Office, required for pets travelling from countries the FSVO classes as high rabies risk, which includes Lebanon. Most EU destinations on this route do not require any equivalent advance permission step, which makes Switzerland's process genuinely different."
  - question: "How far ahead do I need to apply for FSVO permission?"
    answer: "At least 3 weeks before your planned travel date. Apply it in parallel with the titre test sequence rather than waiting until the rest of the paperwork is finished, since it does not depend on the titre test result."
  - question: "Is Switzerland's wait the same as the EU's 90-day rule?"
    answer: "It is described as a 3-month waiting period rather than 90 days, and in practice runs close to the same length. It is still counted from the date of the blood draw, not from the date you receive the titre test result."
  - question: "Can I fly directly from Beirut to Zurich?"
