content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Russia to the United Kingdom"
  overview: |
    There is no direct flight between Russia and the UK for a pet, or for anyone else. That has been true since February 2022, and nothing in the current sanctions picture suggests it changes soon. The good news buried in that bad news: Russia is a Great Britain "listed" country, so once your pet clears the routing problem, the paperwork itself is lighter than most owners expect. No titre test, no 3-month wait.

    The route that actually works runs through Istanbul or, less commonly, Dubai. Two separate cargo bookings, a supervised transfer, and careful timing between the Russian export paperwork and the UK's 10-day health certificate window. It is a logistics problem wrapped around a fairly simple set of veterinary rules, and treating it as the reverse, an easy flight with a complicated form, is where most delays start.
  sections:
    - heading: "Why there is no direct flight, and what that means for booking"
      body: |
        Russia closed its airspace to aircraft from the UK, the EU and several other states in late February 2022. The EU, UK and allied governments responded with their own bans on Russian-registered carriers, including Aeroflot, entering their airspace. Both sets of restrictions remain in force in mid-2026, and the EU's sanctions package covering Russian aviation has been renewed rather than allowed to lapse.

        Turkey never joined either ban, which is why Istanbul has become the default connection point. Russian and Turkish carriers still fly Moscow to Istanbul; Turkish Airlines and other Western carriers still fly Istanbul to London. Your pet needs a booking on each leg, cleared by each airline's cargo team separately, because no airline currently sells a single through-fare across the gap. Dubai works the same way as a second option, usually only chosen when Istanbul cargo capacity is full or the dates do not line up.

        Source: theflyingengineer.com and OPSGROUP's Ukraine/Russia airspace tracker, both cross-checked against the European Commission's sanctions-on-transport page, current as of mid-2026.
    - heading: "Russia's listed status: the one piece of good news"
      body: |
        Great Britain classifies non-EU countries as either "listed" or "not listed" for pet travel. Listed countries only need a Great Britain pet health certificate. Not-listed countries also need a rabies titre test, with blood drawn at least 30 days after vaccination and a fixed 3-month wait from the sampling date.

        Russia appears on the listed table on gov.uk's official guidance (checked July 2026), between Réunion and Saint Barthélemy, alongside countries like Canada, Japan and the USA. That means a Russia-origin pet skips the titre test and the 3-month wait entirely. What it does not skip: the ISO microchip before vaccination, the 21-day wait after a first rabies vaccination, tapeworm treatment for dogs 24-120 hours before landing, and a Great Britain pet health certificate issued within 10 days of arrival.

        This is worth stating plainly because several UK pet travel guides list Russia as unlisted, which was true before Brexit-era reclassifications but is not what gov.uk's current table shows.
    - heading: "Coordinating the Russian export paperwork with the UK's 10-day window"
      body: |
        Rosselkhoznadzor's process starts with Veterinary Certificate Form No. 1, applied for through the eCert system and issued by your local State Veterinary Service within 5 days of travel. At the airport, a Rosselkhoznadzor officer exchanges it for International Veterinary Certificate Form No. 5a on the day you fly.

        Your UK-format pet health certificate has its own clock: no more than 10 days old when your pet lands in Great Britain. With a two-leg journey and a transit stop, that means booking the vet appointments for both certificates around a single confirmed departure date, not a rough travel week. An IPATA-registered agent with Russia experience is the most reliable way to keep both clocks aligned.
