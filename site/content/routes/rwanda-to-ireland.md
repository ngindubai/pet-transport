  h1: "Pet Transport Rwanda to Ireland"
  overview: |
    Three separate requirements stand between a pet in Kigali and a home in Dublin: the FAVN titre test that every EU-unlisted country faces, the TRACES pre-arrival notification Ireland asks of everyone, and a tapeworm treatment specific to dogs that catches out owners who assume it's a UK-only rule. Together they set a realistic window of 20 to 26 weeks, with Brussels Airlines offering the steadiest single-connection routing.

  sections:
    - heading: "The tapeworm rule most guides miss"
      body: |
        Ireland requires a praziquantel treatment for dogs, administered 24 to 120 hours before arrival, and recorded by the treating vet. Many owners assume this only applies to dogs coming from Great Britain, since that's how it's often summarised. It doesn't. The rule covers dogs entering Ireland from every country except Finland, Malta, Norway and Northern Ireland, which means a Rwanda-origin dog needs the treatment just as much as one crossing the Irish Sea.

    - heading: "The titre test and the 90-day wait"
      body: |
        Rwanda sits outside the EU's approved country list, so the standard unlisted-country sequence applies: microchip, rabies vaccination, a blood draw for the FAVN titre test at least 30 days later, and a fixed 90-day wait from that draw date. None of it moves faster for an urgent travel date, and the wait runs from the draw, not the result.

    - heading: "TRACES: the step that applies regardless"
      body: |
        Most EU states stop at a valid health certificate. Ireland adds a pre-arrival notification through TRACES, the EU's Trade Control and Expert System, arranged ahead of travel by the vet or transport agent. It runs alongside the titre test result and the health certificate, not instead of them, and DAFM treats a missing TRACES notification the same way it treats a missing certificate.

    - heading: "Getting from Kigali to Dublin"
      body: |
        Brussels Airlines and RwandAir both fly Kigali to Brussels, and Brussels handles live animal cargo as routine business, giving either carrier a workable single-connection path on to Dublin. KLM Cargo via Nairobi is the alternate route, drawing on the established KLM/Kenya Airways link out of East Africa.
