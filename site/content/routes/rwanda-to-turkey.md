content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Rwanda to Turkey"
  overview: |
    Kigali to Istanbul is a direct flight, which is not something every East African capital can offer. Turkish Airlines runs it non-stop, and that single fact takes a genuine variable, a mid-journey cargo transfer, off the table for this route.

    What doesn't change is Turkey's own entry standard. A rabies titre test is required from Rwanda, with a 90-day wait counted from the day blood is drawn. The direct flight makes the journey simpler. It doesn't make the paperwork faster.

  sections:
    - heading: "The advantage of a direct Kigali-Istanbul flight"
      body: |
        A lot of African routes in this build need a Gulf or European stopover to reach Turkey. Kigali doesn't. Turkish Airlines' direct service means a single cargo handover, at Kigali International (KGL), with no intermediate handling agent to coordinate and no second set of documents to present partway through the journey. RwandAir's own routing via a Gulf connection remains a fallback if the direct flight's cargo capacity is booked out.

        RAB, the Rwanda Agriculture and Animal Resources Development Board, doesn't require a separate export permit for a personal pet. Its endorsement of the health certificate is the export document itself, reformatted to Turkey's own model rather than the EU-style certificate used on Rwanda's European routes.

    - heading: "What Turkey requires on entry"
      body: |
        The titre test is the step that actually sets the timeline. Blood has to be drawn at least 30 days after the rabies vaccination, the result needs to clear 0.5 IU/ml, and then a full 90 days has to pass from the draw date, not from when the laboratory reports back. A booster given after the test still triggers its own 21-day wait, so time any revaccination carefully against your travel plans.

        Arrive without a valid, correctly timed result, and Turkish authorities apply 21 days of quarantine at the owner's expense rather than turning the pet away outright, but it's a costly and stressful outcome worth avoiding.
