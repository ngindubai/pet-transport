  sections:
    - heading: "Routing from Ljubljana to Warsaw"
      body: |
        The most straightforward routing uses Austrian Airlines from Ljubljana (LJU) to Vienna (VIE), then an onward service to Warsaw Chopin (WAW). The Ljubljana to Vienna leg takes under 1 hour; the Vienna to Warsaw leg adds around 1.5-2 hours. Small pets (up to 8kg including carrier) travel in the cabin throughout.

        Lufthansa via Frankfurt is an alternative. Frankfurt has more extensive cargo facilities than Vienna, which can be an advantage for large dogs that need to travel by cargo rather than as cabin or checked-baggage animals.

        LOT Polish Airlines operates from Warsaw and can be useful for the return journey or for connecting from another Polish city.

    - heading: "Cargo from Ljubljana: practical considerations"
      body: |
        Ljubljana Airport (LJU) is a small facility. Its cargo handling for live animals is limited, and not all large-animal cargo routes are available directly. For small pets in the cabin, this is irrelevant; the cabin process works the same as at any EU airport.

        For large dogs that need to travel by cargo, however, the most practical approach is often to route through Vienna or Frankfurt. This may mean driving from Ljubljana to Vienna (about 3.5 hours) to access a full cargo service, then flying from VIE to Warsaw. A specialist pet transport agent can help plan this routing efficiently.

    - heading: "Polish entry requirements"
      body: |
        Poland's Chief Veterinary Inspectorate (GIW) follows EU rules for incoming pets. Microchip (ISO 11784/11785), valid rabies vaccination (21-day wait for first vaccination), and EU pet passport or AHC are required. No titre test, no quarantine, no import permit.

        The AHC must be endorsed within 10 days of crossing the Polish border. For a flight via Vienna, the 10-day window starts when you arrive in Warsaw, not when you depart Ljubljana.

        Poland has no national breed ban. Certain breeds must be muzzled and kept on leads in public under Polish law, but these are domestic regulations rather than entry conditions.
