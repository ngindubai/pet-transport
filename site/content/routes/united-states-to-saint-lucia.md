    Pit bulls get an outright ban entering Saint Lucia. Rottweilers get something different: a restriction, which isn't the same thing and is worth checking specifically rather than assuming it means an automatic no. That distinction, ban versus restriction, is the kind of detail a generic Caribbean pet-travel page tends to flatten into one blanket breed rule.

Microchip, a rabies vaccination given 30 days ahead, no titre test confirmed as required, and no routine quarantine for a pet arriving with the paperwork in order. The one sequencing quirk worth knowing: Saint Lucia's health certificate has to be submitted as part of the import permit application itself, not just produced on arrival, so the vet appointment and the permit application need to happen in the right order.

  sections:
    - heading: "Ban, or restriction: the breed distinction that matters"
      body: |
        Saint Lucia's published rules name several bull-type breeds as banned outright: Pit Bull Terrier, Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Staffordshire Bull Terrier, British Staffordshire Terrier, Japanese Tosa Inu, Dogo Argentino and Fila Brasileiro. No certificate changes that outcome.

        Rottweilers sit in a different category, restricted rather than banned, which in practice usually means extra conditions rather than automatic refusal. Given how much weight that word carries, confirm the current specific condition directly with the Veterinary and Livestock Services Division before assuming either a Rottweiler can travel freely or that it can't travel at all.

    - heading: "A permit that needs the certificate before it, not after"
      body: |
        Most routes on this site treat the health certificate as something presented at the border. Saint Lucia's import permit process works differently: the certificate has to accompany the permit application itself, which the Division typically processes within around two business days once everything is submitted.

        That makes the order of operations matter. Get the USDA certificate finalised first, then submit the permit application, rather than trying to run both processes in parallel and hoping they converge in time.
