  h1: "Pet Transport from Guyana to the United Kingdom"
  overview: |
    British Airways is the only carrier that flies a dog or cat out of Georgetown toward London without a change of airline: a twice-weekly service on a Boeing 777 that touches down in St Lucia before continuing to Gatwick, same flight number throughout. Guyana doesn't appear on either of the UK's listed-country tables, though, so that direct-ish flight doesn't buy any shortcut on paperwork. It runs the same unlisted-country track as most of South America: a titre test, a fixed wait measured in calendar months, and a GB health certificate.

    Add the CDC's high-risk classification for dog rabies in Guyana into the mix, which rules out routing a dog through a US gateway on American Airlines, and the practical routing narrows to one sensible option. Cats have more flexibility. Dogs, in practice, fly British Airways.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why the titre test sets the travel date, not the flight schedule"
      body: |
        Once the microchip and vaccination are done in the right order, the titre test blood draw has to happen at least 30 days later. Whatever the result, GB then requires a further 3 calendar months to pass, counted from the day blood was drawn, not the day the lab confirms a pass. Owners who assume the wait starts on the result date typically lose 2 to 4 weeks they hadn't planned for.

        Start to finish, from a standing start, 4 to 6 months is realistic. A pet with an already-current rabies vaccination that only needs the titre test and wait can move in 3 to 4 months.

    - heading: "The one flight that matters, and why the alternative is riskier for dogs"
      body: |
        BA2159 is a genuinely useful fact for this route: same aircraft, same flight number, Georgetown to Gatwick via a technical stop in St Lucia, twice a week on a Boeing 777. IAG Cargo doesn't require a freight forwarder for Caribbean-origin live animal bookings either, which simplifies the paperwork compared with most other regions this site covers.

        American Airlines flies Georgetown to Miami and to JFK nonstop, and on paper that looks like a faster route into a wider transatlantic network. It isn't, for a dog. American Airlines Cargo won't take a new dog booking originating in a country on the CDC's high-risk rabies list, and Guyana is one of them. That's a US import-side rule, unconnected to GB's own requirements, but it still closes off the Miami and JFK options for dogs. Cats aren't covered by that CDC list, so a US-routed connection stays open for them if British Airways' twice-weekly schedule doesn't line up.

    - heading: "GLDA's export certificate: fast, but it isn't the clock that matters"
      body: |
        Getting a pet legally out of Guyana is the easy part of this route. GLDA requires an export permit application, a vet exam confirming the rabies vaccination timing, and the International Export Health Certificate itself, signed off by a Senior Veterinary Officer and the CEO or Deputy CEO, with the GLDA seal. The whole thing costs G$5,000, about USD 24, and takes 4 working days once submitted.

        None of that speed changes the GB side. The certificate confirms the pet is healthy and legally exportable. It says nothing about whether the titre test and 3-month wait are done. Time the GLDA certificate close to your actual flight, not to when you first start the process.
