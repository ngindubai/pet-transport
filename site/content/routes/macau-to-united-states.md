content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Macau to the United States"
  overview: |
    China sits on the CDC's list of high-risk countries for dog rabies. Macau, despite sharing a mainland border, does not. That exclusion, which also covers Hong Kong and Taiwan, means a pet leaving Macau's Special Administrative Region enters the US on the same straightforward path as one from almost anywhere else: microchip, current rabies vaccination, and a completed CDC Dog Import Form.

    The friction on this route sits in the middle, not at either border. Macau International Airport carries no transpacific cargo, so every pet makes a road or ferry transfer to Hong Kong first.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Macau isn't treated as high-risk by the CDC"
      body: |
        The CDC's high-risk list names mainland China but explicitly excludes Macau, Hong Kong and Taiwan. Miss that distinction, easy to do if you're reading a generic 'China' guide rather than one written for this specific SAR, and you'd assume a far heavier process than actually applies: no CDC Dog Import Form complications tied to high-risk status, no additional serology testing, no restriction to a handful of designated high-risk entry airports.

        Cats have it simpler again. The CDC has no rabies vaccination requirement at all for cats entering the US, from Macau or anywhere else.

    - heading: "The Hong Kong bottleneck"
      body: |
        Macau's own airport, MFM, doesn't run transpacific cargo, so the practical path is a road transfer across the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macau Bridge, or a ferry, about an hour door to door, into Hong Kong International Airport, then a direct Cathay Pacific Cargo flight to a US gateway city.

        Book that transfer as its own line item with its own crate handling check. It's short, but it involves clearing a second jurisdiction's cargo terminal with a live animal, and slots fill fast around peak travel periods.
