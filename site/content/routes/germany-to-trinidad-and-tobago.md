content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Germany to Trinidad and Tobago"
  overview: |
    There's no nonstop flight from Germany to Trinidad, unlike the UK's single-carrier Gatwick-Piarco service on British Airways. Every German pet needs a transfer, most practically through Miami on Lufthansa or through London on a connecting BA service, before it ever reaches Piarco.

    The paperwork runs on the same lines as the UK and US routes already documented on this site: a rabies titre test, an Animal Production and Health Division import permit, and a Brucella canis test for any dog that hasn't been neutered. Trinidad and Tobago has operated without routine quarantine for compliant pets since April 2013, so a clean file matters more here than a long wait once the pet lands.

  sections:
    - heading: "Getting off the ground: why Germany needs a transfer"
      body: |
        Lufthansa's Frankfurt-Miami service is nonstop and well established, run through a dedicated Animal Lounge at the Frankfurt hub for crate handling ahead of the transatlantic leg. Miami itself has no direct pet cargo product into Trinidad, so an American Airlines or Caribbean Airlines connection covers the final Miami-Piarco hop.

        The alternative runs through London: a Frankfurt connection into Heathrow or Gatwick, then British Airways' established nonstop Gatwick-Piarco service via IAG Cargo, the same routing UK-origin pets already use. Either way, a German pet picks up an extra leg the UK's single-carrier option doesn't need.

    - heading: "The titre test and permit sequence"
      body: |
        APHD asks for the import permit application at least one month ahead of travel, though 1.5 months is a safer buffer given processing volumes. Running alongside it is the titre test itself, FAVN or RFFIT, drawn at least one month after vaccination and sent to an approved laboratory. Order matters: the microchip goes in first, then the vaccination, then the blood draw. Reverse any part of that sequence and the result is void.

        Intact dogs face one extra step most cat owners never see: a negative Brucella canis test, ideally done at the same lab visit as the titre. Cats and neutered dogs skip it entirely, which is easy to assume applies universally until it doesn't.
