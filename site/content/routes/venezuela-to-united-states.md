  h1: "Pet Transport from Venezuela to the United States"
  overview: |
    Simon Bolivar International Airport, the gateway that normally handles Caracas's international traffic, has not run a normal schedule since a magnitude 7-plus earthquake hit northern Venezuela on 24 June 2026. Airlines have shifted operations to Valencia's Arturo Michelena airport in the meantime, and even American Airlines' newly restored Miami-Caracas nonstop, flying again for the first time in about seven years, is currently grounded by the same closure. Anyone planning to move a pet on this route needs to treat the flight itself as the open question, not a formality to book once the paperwork is sorted.

    The paperwork carries its own weight regardless of which airport ends up handling the flight. Venezuela sits on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies, so a dog needs the microchip-before-vaccine sequence, a titre test from a CDC-approved laboratory, and arrival at one of a handful of US airports with a CDC-registered animal care facility. Cats clear the federal side far more lightly. Budget nine to fourteen weeks even in a best case, and expect that figure to move if Maiquetia's reopening slips.

  sections:
    - heading: "Getting out of Venezuela while Maiquetia is closed"
      body: |
        Copa Airlines has added extra flights between Panama City and Valencia to cover the gap, on top of resuming some domestic Venezuelan routes to Maracaibo, Barcelona and Barquisimeto, while Caracas itself stays off the schedule. Avianca has done the same on the Bogota to Valencia sector, running two daily rotations at last confirmation, with onward connections to Miami available from its Bogota hub.

        Neither of these is a settled, permanent routing. Both were announced as contingency measures tied to specific dates, extendable depending on how the airport recovery goes. A pet booking made today needs a live-animal cargo confirmation close to the actual travel date, not a schedule locked in months ahead. American Airlines' Miami-Caracas service, resumed only in April 2026 after roughly seven years away, is suspended for the same reason, and there is no published date for when it restarts.

    - heading: "The CDC's high-risk country rules, and what happens without a passing titre"
      body: |
        A dog vaccinated in Venezuela cannot simply fly into any US airport. CDC restricts arrivals from high-risk countries to a short list of airports that host a CDC-registered animal care facility, Miami and JFK among them, and a reservation at that facility has to be booked before departure.

        The titre test itself is where the timeline gets built. Blood has to be drawn at least 30 days after the rabies vaccination, and the result needs to land at least 28 days before your flight touches down, tested at a CDC-approved lab. Kansas State University's Rabies Laboratory, a commonly used option, runs the FAVN test for around USD 90 with a three to four week turnaround. Get the maths wrong and you either delay the flight or arrive without a valid result, which converts straight into a mandatory 28-day quarantine at the arrival facility, paid for by the owner.

    - heading: "Cats, and the parts of the process that do not change for them"
      body: |
        Federally, a cat needs almost none of this. No CDC rabies vaccination requirement, no titre test, no facility reservation. What stays in place is everything on the Venezuela side: INSAI's export certificate, the vaccination card, the deworming certificate, and for dogs specifically, a heartworm blood test inside 30 days of export that cats and puppies under six months are exempt from. Airlines also tend to want their own health certificate regardless of species, so a lighter federal rule for cats is not the same as no paperwork at all.
