  overview: |
    Simon Bolivar International Airport at Maiquetia has not handled scheduled commercial flights since the earthquakes of 24 June 2026. That single fact reshapes this whole route. American Airlines had only just restarted its own Miami-Caracas service in April, ending a seven-year gap, and was days from launching Miami-Maracaibo when the ground shook.

    Even once flights stabilise, a pet cannot simply book onto whichever passenger service is running. American, Delta and United have all pulled civilian pets from their cargo holds in 2026, leaving that space for military and Foreign Service moves only. Add Venezuela's status as a CDC high-risk country for dog rabies, and this becomes one of the more demanding South American routes on this site, not because of one hard rule but because several things have to align at once.

    None of this is unworkable. Avianca still carries pet cargo, still flies into CDC-facility US cities, and Venezuela's own INSAI paperwork is well understood. It just needs real lead time and a routing plan that gets checked again the week before travel, not months out.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why the airline that flies the route may not carry your pet"
      body: |
        It is tempting to see American Airlines back in the Venezuelan market and assume the job is half done. It is not, for a pet. American resumed daily Miami-Caracas flights on 30 April 2026, its first Venezuela service since 2019, and had a Miami-Maracaibo route scheduled for 14 July. Both are passenger operations run through Envoy on Embraer 175 regional jets.

        Civilian cargo pets are a separate question entirely. American, alongside Delta and United, currently reserves its temperature-controlled cargo hold for active-duty US military and Foreign Service personnel travelling on official orders, not for the general public. A family relocating a Labrador from Caracas cannot simply book a seat and check the dog into the hold below. Avianca, by contrast, still runs a functioning pet cargo service and connects through Bogota to Miami, JFK and other US cities with CDC facilities. That is the practical carrier for this move, whatever the passenger headlines say about American's return.
    - heading: "The earthquake, the airport, and what it means for booking"
      body: |
        Two large earthquakes hit Venezuela on 24 June 2026, and the damage at Maiquetia was severe enough to close the airport to normal commercial traffic: cracked runway pavement, a damaged control tower, and structural failures inside the terminal. Iberia and Air Europa shifted their Venezuelan service to Valencia's Arturo Michelena airport instead, and Avianca has been running Bogota-Valencia flights to keep some connectivity alive while Maiquetia is repaired.

        There is no confirmed date for Maiquetia to reopen to scheduled commercial flights as this is written. Officials have talked about using a parallel runway sooner, but nothing is locked in. For a pet move, this means booking the export and CDC paperwork on a schedule that does not depend on Caracas reopening on a specific date, and reconfirming the actual routing, whether that is via Valencia, Bogota, or a reopened Maiquetia, in the final days before travel rather than trusting a booking made weeks earlier.
    - heading: "The CDC high-risk pathway, in practical terms"
      body: |
        Venezuela sits on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies, which means dogs need either a valid serology titre or a 28-day stay at a CDC-registered facility. The titre route needs blood drawn at least 30 days after the vaccination and at least 28 days before the flight lands in the US, so both gaps have to be satisfied together, not just one.

        Cats have none of this. Federally, a cat from Venezuela faces no CDC or USDA barrier at all, which makes a cat-only move considerably simpler than a dog's. For dogs choosing the titre route over quarantine, the Certification of Foreign Rabies Vaccination and Microchip form, completed by the Venezuelan vet, is the document that carries the most weight at the US arrival point alongside the CDC Dog Import Form receipt.
