content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Uruguay to Finland: 2026 Guide"
  overview: |
    Finland asks for one thing that most EU destinations do not: a tapeworm treatment timed to a narrow 24-to-120-hour window before your pet lands in Helsinki. That sits on top of the titre-test process every unlisted-country pet faces, since Uruguay has no EU rabies-control equivalence and Finland applies the standard third-country rules in full.

    The tapeworm rule exists because Finland, along with Ireland, Malta and Norway, has kept Echinococcus multilocularis out of its dog population and wants to keep it that way. For a Uruguayan pet, that means one extra vet visit scheduled precisely around the flight, not the paperwork.

  sections:
    - heading: "The titre test and 90-day wait, in full"
      body: |
        Uruguay's rabies control has not been assessed as equivalent to the EU's own standards, so Finland applies the unlisted third country process: FAVN or ELISA titre test at an EU-approved laboratory, minimum result 0.5 IU/ml, blood drawn at least 30 days after vaccination, then a 90-day wait from that draw date before entry.

        As with every unlisted-origin route in this build, the clock starts on the day the blood is drawn, regardless of how long the laboratory takes to report a result.
    - heading: "Why the tapeworm treatment window matters more here than elsewhere"
      body: |
        Most EU countries have no tapeworm requirement at all. Finland does, because Echinococcus multilocularis is not established in the Finnish dog population and the government wants to keep the border effective. Praziquantel, given 24 to 120 hours before arrival and documented by a vet, is the requirement.

        The practical effect: you cannot lock in the tapeworm treatment appointment until you have a confirmed flight date, and you cannot confirm the flight until the 90-day titre wait has passed. Work backward from your target travel date and build the final vet visit into the week before departure, not earlier.
