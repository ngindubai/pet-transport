content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Mongolia to Australia"
  overview: |
    Twenty-five MIAT flights and eight Korean Air flights leave Ulaanbaatar for Seoul every single week. That number matters more than usual here, because Mongolia sits outside DAFF's approved country groups entirely, and a pet cannot go straight from Ulaanbaatar to Melbourne under any circumstances. It has to live somewhere DAFF approves of first, for 180 unbroken days. South Korea happens to be Group 3 approved, and it's also the one place Mongolian pets already fly to in bulk.

    That coincidence doesn't shorten the calendar much. A rabies titre test, its own 180-day wait, and a mandatory Mickleham quarantine on arrival all still apply, whatever staging country is chosen. What changes is the flight logistics: no separate long-haul detour just to start the clock, which is a real saving of time and cost compared with routing through Europe first.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why can't a Mongolian pet fly straight to Australia?"
      body: |
        DAFF sorts every country of origin into Group 1, Group 2, Group 3 or non-approved. Mongolia isn't in any of the first three. That means no agent, however experienced, can book a direct Ulaanbaatar-to-Australia pet move. The pet has to establish 180 consecutive days of residency in an approved country, and only then does DAFF start processing the actual import.

        It's a fixed biosecurity requirement, not a paperwork bottleneck that can be smoothed over with the right form filled in correctly. The 180 days run regardless of the animal's health history.

    - heading: "Why South Korea, specifically?"
      body: |
        Most non-approved-country routes on this site send owners looking at the UK, USA or Singapore, each a dedicated long-haul flight from wherever the pet starts. Mongolia is different. MIAT already flies Ulaanbaatar to Seoul 25 times a week, its single busiest international route, and Korean Air adds another 8. South Korea is DAFF Group 3 approved.

        Put those two facts together and the 180-day clock can begin on a flight the owner might have booked anyway, without adding a special-purpose long-haul sector to the itinerary purely to satisfy DAFF's residency rule.

    - heading: "The RNATT test, and the quarantine trick worth knowing"
      body: |
        A rabies titre test still applies once the pet is in South Korea: blood drawn at least 30 days after vaccination, tested at a DAFF-approved lab, with a 180-day wait from the date the lab receives the sample. That wait can overlap with the residency period rather than stack on top of it, since both clocks run from roughly the same point if the test happens early.

        One detail is easy to miss and expensive to get wrong. If the microchip is identity-verified by the competent authority before the RNATT blood draw, and this happens at least 180 days before the flight, Mickleham quarantine drops to a 10-day minimum. Skip or delay that check and the default rises to 30 days, a meaningfully longer and costlier stay. Source: DAFF, agriculture.gov.au.

    - heading: "Landing in Melbourne, wherever you're actually headed"
      body: |
        DAFF only processes imported cats and dogs through Melbourne. Qatar Airways states this outright in its own carriage rules: animals travelling to Australia go through Melbourne, full stop, regardless of the owner's final city. A pet moving to Perth, Sydney or Brisbane still lands in Melbourne, completes Mickleham quarantine, and only then travels on domestically.

        Korean Air's own network reaches Melbourne via a same-airline connection through Sydney or Brisbane, useful because the pet doesn't change carriers mid-journey. Qatar Airways through Doha is a solid second option for the final leg.
