content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Honduras to Canada"
  overview: |
    Honduras appears on two separate high-risk lists at once: the CDC's for the US, and CFIA's for Canada. Only one of them asks for a blood test.

    Where the Honduras-to-US route on this site runs through a mandatory rabies titre test and a restriction to six specific US airports, the Canada route drops both. CFIA has no titre requirement for personal pets and no equivalent facility-airport rule. What's left is a microchip-before-vaccination sequence for dogs, a minimum age most sources put at 7 months, and Honduras's own routine SENASA export paperwork.

  sections:
    - heading: "High-risk list, but not the same high-risk rules"
      body: |
        CFIA's list of countries at high-risk for dog rabies includes Honduras, confirmed on the list dated 13 March 2025. That listing drives a real, published ban, but it's a ban on commercial dogs, meaning animals imported for resale, adoption, fostering, breeding, show or research. A personal pet travelling with its owner isn't caught by it.

        What the personal-pet pathway does still ask for is sequencing: the microchip has to go in before or on the same day as the rabies vaccination, not after. Get that order backwards and the vaccination record doesn't count toward Canada's requirements, no matter how current it looks.

    - heading: "No titre test changes the whole shape of this route"
      body: |
        Compare this to the Honduras-to-US route, which requires an FAVN titre test with a 30-day post-vaccination wait and a further 28-day gap before entry, easily adding two months to the timeline. Canada skips this entirely for personal pets. That single difference is most of why this route runs at roughly half the estimated timeline of the US one.

        It also means the 7-month minimum age figure, cited consistently across veterinary summary sources though not spelled out plainly on CFIA's general public pages checked for this guide, is worth confirming directly through CFIA's AIRS tool rather than assumed. Getting the age wrong by even a few weeks is a cheaper mistake to catch early than at the airport.

    - heading: "Houston works here. It doesn't work for the US route."
      body: |
        United flies both San Pedro Sula and Palmerola to Houston up to twice daily. On the Honduras-to-US route, that connection is a dead end for a dog, since Houston has no CDC-registered animal care facility and the CDC requires arrival at one of just six qualifying airports. On this route, Houston is fine. Canada is the actual destination, CFIA has no equivalent facility-airport rule, and the dog simply connects onward to Toronto.

        Avianca's more direct San Pedro Sula-Montreal service is still worth pricing first, since it avoids the extra US customs and agriculture clearance that comes with routing through any American hub as an intermediate stop, Houston included.
