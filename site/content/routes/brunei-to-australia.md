  h1: "Pet Transport from Brunei to Australia: 2026 Guide"
  overview: |
    DAFF places Brunei in Group 3, its tier for countries where rabies is present but considered well-controlled, alongside most of the EU, the UK, the US and Canada. That means the same demanding sequence applies here as on those routes: an RNATT titre test, a 180-day wait counted from when the laboratory receives the sample, and a stay at the Mickleham quarantine facility near Melbourne.

    The one lever that genuinely shortens the quarantine stay, from 30 days down to 10, is a formal identity verification completed before the RNATT blood draw, a distinct step from simply implanting the microchip first. Given the length of every stage here, this route realistically runs 30 to 42 weeks.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Group 3 means the long pathway"
      body: |
        DAFF's classification system splits approved countries into three tiers by rabies risk. Group 1 covers genuinely rabies-free places like New Zealand, needing no quarantine at all. Group 3, where Brunei sits, covers countries where rabies exists but is managed, and it carries the heaviest requirements of the three: a titre test, the 180-day wait, and mandatory quarantine on arrival.

        The 180-day clock is worth getting right. It starts when the DAFF-approved laboratory receives the blood sample, not the day it was drawn in Brunei, so international courier time genuinely eats into the timeline and should be planned for rather than assumed away.

    - heading: "The 10-day quarantine pathway, and why it's easy to miss"
      body: |
        Most Group 3 pets default to 30 days at Mickleham. A shorter 10-day stay is available, but only through one of two routes: proof of genuine Australian origin, which won't apply here, or a formal identity verification carried out by a competent authority in Brunei at a separate appointment before the RNATT blood sample is taken. This is a specific procedural step, not simply "microchip before blood draw," and getting the sequence or paperwork wrong can mean the RNATT has to be redone entirely, restarting the 180-day wait from scratch. Confirm the exact local process with DAFF or an experienced transport agent well before booking the blood draw.
