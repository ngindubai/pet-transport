  h1: "Pet Transport from the United States to Bolivia"
  overview: |
    No import permit. That is the standout fact for a personal pet moving from the US to Bolivia: unlike the commercial cargo rules that apply to trade shipments, an owner travelling with their own dog or cat needs no separate permit at all. What Bolivia does insist on is a health certificate that has been through both APHIS endorsement and legalisation at a Bolivian Consulate, a step with its own processing time that catches owners who plan around the vet appointment alone.

    Boliviana de Aviación's nonstop Miami to Santa Cruz service remains the one direct link between the two countries, and it happens to land at Viru Viru, one of the two airports Bolivian entry guidance names for pet arrivals. Departing from anywhere else in the US means a connection through Panama City on Copa or Bogota on Avianca, each adding a second carrier's pet policy to check.

  sections:
    - heading: "A lighter import process than several neighbours, with one real catch"
      body: |
        Bolivia does not require a rabies titre test for a pet entering from the US, and it does not require an import permit for a personal, owner-accompanied pet. It does insist the rabies vaccination itself falls within a specific window, at least 30 days before arrival and no more than 12 months before it, and that it comes from a single-dose product rather than a multi-year vaccine.

        The genuine catch is consular legalisation. The APHIS-endorsed health certificate has to be legalised at a Bolivian Consulate before travel, for a fee that varies by consulate, and that step runs on the consulate's schedule rather than yours. Leave it to the final week and it becomes the one part of this route capable of derailing an otherwise straightforward move.

    - heading: "One direct flight, two named arrival airports"
      body: |
        BoA's Miami to Santa Cruz Viru Viru service is the only nonstop option, and general Bolivian entry guidance also names El Alto International Airport in La Paz as a recognised arrival point for pets, so which one applies to you depends on where in Bolivia you are actually headed. For an East Coast departure into Santa Cruz, the BoA direct service removes an entire connecting leg most South American routes into Bolivia require.

        Copa through Panama City and Avianca through Bogota both reach Santa Cruz Viru Viru from a wider spread of US departure cities, useful if Miami is not convenient, but each turns the booking into two separate pet policies to satisfy rather than one. For most owners, that trade-off, Miami's single nonstop against a wider but two-leg network elsewhere, is worth weighing before booking either the flight or the crate.
