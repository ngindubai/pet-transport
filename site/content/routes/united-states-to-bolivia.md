  h1: "Pet Transport from United States to Bolivia"
  overview: |
    A USD 85 money order made out to the Bolivian Consulate is not something most pet owners expect to need, but it is exactly what stands between an APHIS-endorsed health certificate and one Bolivia will actually accept. No titre test, no CDC-style facility booking, nothing like the hoops a dog faces coming the other way into the US. The obstacle here is postal, not medical.

    APHIS Form 7001, completed by a USDA-accredited vet and endorsed by APHIS Veterinary Services, covers the American side for a modest USD 38 fee. Bolivia then wants that same certificate notarised and mailed to its consulate in Washington DC for legalisation before it counts as valid on arrival. Build the mail time in properly and the rest of the process is genuinely one of the more straightforward moves this site covers.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why the consulate step by post is the real bottleneck"
      body: |
        Most destinations that require consulate legalisation, Bolivia included when the direction runs from the UK, let you book an in-person appointment and walk out with a stamped document the same day. The US process runs differently. Everything goes by mail to 4420 Connecticut Avenue NW in Washington DC, with a money order rather than a card payment, and a prepaid return envelope you have to remember to include.

        That means a delay is not something you notice and fix on the spot. It shows up as a week of silence while an envelope sits in a queue. Owners who have been through it describe the round trip taking anywhere from one to three weeks depending on the season, longer around the December holidays when consular staff are stretched thin across every Latin American mission in the city.

    - heading: "What Bolivia does not ask for, and the one gap worth flagging"
      body: |
        No rabies titre test. No fixed waiting period tied to a blood draw. That alone puts this route well ahead of most South American destinations for US pet owners in terms of raw complexity.

        There is one loose end. SENASAG's general published framework for pets entering Bolivia references an import permit, the Permiso Zoosanitario de Importación, yet APHIS's own dedicated page for the US-to-Bolivia route does not mention it at all. Two official-adjacent sources, two different pictures. Rather than assume either is complete, get written confirmation from SENASAG or the consulate on whether the permit currently applies to a pet arriving from the United States before you finalise travel dates.

    - heading: "Getting to Santa Cruz"
      body: |
        Boliviana de Aviación flies the only nonstop sector between the two countries, Miami to Santa Cruz Viru Viru, at around 7 hours and up to twice daily. That single direct option removes a connection most South American routes force on US owners. If Miami is not workable, Copa Airlines through Panama City and Avianca through Bogotá both reach Santa Cruz, but each adds a second carrier's pet policy and a second leg where a live animal transfer has to go right.
