  h1: "Pet Transport from Russia to the United States"
  overview: |
    Two separate problems stack on top of each other on this route, and most owners only plan for one. The first is that no airline has flown nonstop between Moscow and any US city since March 2022. The second is that the CDC treats Russia as a high-risk country for dog rabies, which means a titre test, a strict document set, and arrival restricted to just six US airports that have a CDC-registered animal care facility on site.

    Solve them separately and you will book a flight that cannot land where your facility reservation needs it to. Solve them together, starting with the CDC side since it fixes your possible arrival airports before you even look at a flight, and the whole move is workable inside four to five months.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Russia counts as high risk, and what that actually requires"
      body: |
        The CDC's high-risk list is not a political judgement about the current relationship between the two countries. It reflects the genuine rabies picture on the ground: Russia has an active wildlife rabies reservoir, most notably in foxes and raccoon dogs, and CDC classifies countries on that epidemiological basis rather than on diplomatic status.

        For a dog vaccinated in Russia, that means the foreign-vaccinated pathway applies in full. Blood for the titre test has to be drawn at least 30 days after the primary rabies vaccination, and the result has to reach 0.5 IU/ml or above at a CDC-approved laboratory. The sample also has to be drawn at least 28 days before the dog actually enters the US, a separate clock from the 30-day rule, so in practice the earliest a compliant dog can travel is roughly 9-10 weeks after the vaccination that started the process, before lab turnaround and facility booking are even added in.

        Skip the titre, or fail it, and the alternative is a 28-day quarantine at the arrival facility, paid for by the owner. Almost every case where that happens is a planning failure rather than a genuinely unavoidable situation.

    - heading: "The six-airport rule, and why it decides your flight before your flight decides it"
      body: |
        This is the detail most other guides miss. A foreign-vaccinated dog from a high-risk country cannot simply land at any US international airport with the right paperwork. It has to land at one of six specific airports, each with its own CDC-registered animal care facility: Atlanta, JFK New York, Washington Dulles, Los Angeles, Philadelphia and Miami. The reservation at that facility has to be made before you fly, and the flight itself has to terminate there.

        Combine that with the routing problem and your options narrow fast. Turkish Airlines' Istanbul hub connects onward to JFK, Washington Dulles and Los Angeles, all on the approved list. Emirates out of Dubai reaches JFK, Los Angeles, Washington Dulles and Miami. Qatar Airways out of Doha covers the widest spread of the six, including Atlanta and Philadelphia. Whichever hub you choose, book the facility reservation first, then work backwards to a flight that actually lands where that reservation is.

    - heading: "Getting out of Russia while the paperwork clocks are still running"
      body: |
        On the Russian side, the Rosselkhoznadzor process mirrors what applies on other routes out of the country: Veterinary Certificate Form No. 1, applied for through the eCert system within 5 days of travel, exchanged at the airport for International Veterinary Certificate Form No. 5a on the day you fly. None of that touches the CDC's titre and facility requirements, which run on their own separate, much longer timeline starting months earlier.

        The two processes rarely fail on their own terms. They fail when the departure paperwork is rushed to match a flight date that was booked before the CDC side was properly worked out. Fix the CDC timeline first: vaccination, titre, facility reservation, then work the Russian export paperwork and the flight booking around whatever date that leaves you with.
