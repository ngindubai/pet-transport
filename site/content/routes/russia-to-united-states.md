  h1: "Pet Transport from Russia to the United States"
  overview: |
    Russia sits on the CDC's current high-risk list for dog rabies, and no airline has flown a direct route between Moscow and any US city since March 2022. Put those two facts together and a Russia-to-US dog move becomes one of the more involved routes on this site: a biological process (the titre test) that cannot be rushed, layered on top of a routing problem that has no fix in sight.

    Cats have it easier. There is no federal rabies rule for cats entering the US, so a cat only has to solve the flight problem: Moscow to Istanbul, Dubai or Doha, then a separate booking on to a US city. Dogs have to solve both the flight problem and the CDC's paperwork, in the right order, months in advance.

    None of this is unmanageable if it is planned early. The mistake owners make most often is treating the titre test like a form to fill in the week before travel, when it is really a lab result with a fixed minimum age that starts a clock running from the day of the blood draw.
  sections:
    - heading: "Why the CDC titre test pathway drives the whole timeline"
      body: |
        Because Russia is CDC high-risk, a dog vaccinated there cannot travel on a simple health certificate. The vet draws blood no sooner than 30 days after the qualifying rabies vaccination, and that sample goes to a CDC-approved laboratory rather than any local lab. Only once the result comes back showing an adequate antibody level, and only once that result has itself aged to the CDC's required minimum, can the dog fly.

        Skip this and the alternative is a 28-day quarantine at a CDC-registered animal care facility after arrival, at the owner's cost, with the dog examined and revaccinated on the spot. Most owners who have the lead time choose the titre route instead. It costs less and keeps the dog with the family rather than in a facility for a month.

        The practical effect on the calendar: allow at least 30 days from vaccination to blood draw, then another stretch for the lab result to age sufficiently, before travel is even possible. Add booking time for the routing below and 12 to 18 weeks becomes a realistic floor, not a worst case.
    - heading: "No direct flight, and why the airport you land at is not a free choice"
      body: |
        Russia closed its airspace to US aircraft in early 2022, and the US and its allies responded with bans on Russian-registered carriers. Both restrictions remain in force in mid-2026, with no confirmed date for either side to lift them. Turkey, the UAE and Qatar never joined those bans, which is why Istanbul, Dubai and Doha have become the standard connection points.

        That solves the flight, but not the CDC requirement. A dog from a high-risk country can only enter through one of a small number of US airports with a CDC-registered animal care facility, and the reservation at that facility has to be booked before travel and matched exactly to the arrival airport. Choosing a routing that happens to be cheap or convenient, but lands somewhere without a registered facility, is a rejected booking waiting to happen.

        Emirates via Dubai currently reaches the widest spread of CDC-registered gateways from its Moscow connection. Turkish Airlines via Istanbul and Qatar Airways via Doha both work too, but check which CDC-registered airport each one's US network actually serves before locking in the Russian-side flight.
    - heading: "Where the Russian export side is genuinely simple"
      body: |
        Set against the CDC process, Russia's own export paperwork is fairly light. For a standard move of one or two pets, there is no advance export permit. The State Veterinary Service issues Veterinary Certificate Form No. 1 through the Rosselkhoznadzor eCert system within 5 days of the flight, and a Rosselkhoznadzor officer exchanges it for International Veterinary Certificate Form No. 5a at the airport on departure day.

        The coordination challenge is timing that 5-day Russian window against a titre test result that may have been sitting ready for weeks. Book the confirmed flight date first, then work backwards to the Form No. 1 appointment, rather than getting the Russian certificate early and hoping the US-side paperwork catches up.

    answer: "Yes. The CDC's current high-risk country list for dog rabies includes Russia (cdc.gov/importation, updated April 2026). Dogs vaccinated in Russia need either the titre test pathway or a 28-day US quarantine to enter the country. Cats are not covered by this rule."
  - question: "Are there any direct flights from Russia to the US for pets?"
    answer: "No. There have been no direct flights between Russia and the US since the airspace closures of early 2022, and US sanctions currently keep Russian carriers such as Aeroflot out of US airspace. Every pet move currently runs via a third country, most commonly Istanbul on Turkish Airlines, Dubai on Emirates, or Doha on Qatar Airways."
  - question: "Can my dog arrive at any US airport from Russia?"
    answer: "No. Dogs from CDC high-risk countries must arrive at one of the US airports with a CDC-registered animal care facility, currently JFK, IAD, ATL, MIA, SFO and LAX. A confirmed reservation at that specific facility is required before travel, and the routing has to be booked to land there."

A cat's timeline is shorter, since only the flight routing applies."
