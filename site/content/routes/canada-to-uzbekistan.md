content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Canada to Uzbekistan: 2026 Guide"
  overview: |
    No airline connects Canada and Uzbekistan nonstop, so every pet on this route flies through Istanbul, Doha or Frankfurt on the way to Tashkent. The paperwork runs in parallel: a Canadian vet completes the export certificate, an official CFIA veterinarian endorses it in person before departure, and Uzbekistan then wants that certificate translated into Russian or Uzbek and notarized before the pet lands. None of it is unusual by international standards. It just has to happen in the right order, with the CFIA appointment booked early enough that a delay doesn't strand the travel date.

  sections:
    - heading: "The CFIA endorsement is the step people leave too late"
      body: |
        A vet can write the export certificate any time in the run-up to travel. The problem is the next step: an official CFIA veterinarian has to sign off on it in person, and CFIA has no way to do that once the animal has left the country. Miss the appointment window and the whole schedule slips, sometimes by weeks, because slots aren't always available on short notice. The fee itself is small, roughly CAD 20 to 40, so cost isn't the obstacle here. Timing is.

        Get the appointment booked as soon as the travel date is fixed, not after the flight is booked.
    - heading: "Uzbekistan's own rules are lighter than they look"
      body: |
        A microchip before the rabies vaccine, the vaccine at least 30 days out, a certificate inside 10 days of travel, translated and notarized. That's essentially the whole list for up to two personal pets. No titre test appears in the published requirements, and no import permit or mandatory quarantine applies either, as long as those basics are met.

        What trips people up isn't the biology. It's the paperwork format. A perfectly valid Canadian certificate in English, arriving without a certified Russian or Uzbek translation, can still cause problems at the border checkpoint. Sort the translation out in Canada, well before the flight, rather than hoping to arrange it in Tashkent.
    - heading: "Choosing a hub"
      body: |
        Istanbul, via Turkish Airlines, offers the most frequent connections and a purpose-built live-animal handling service at the airport. Frankfurt, via Uzbekistan Airways' nonstop onward leg, is arguably the cleanest single connection into Tashkent once you're past the transatlantic sector. Doha is newer to this specific pairing: Qatar Airways only began flying it in June 2024, and the aircraft on that leg are narrowbody, which matters for crate size on a larger dog.

        None of these is objectively wrong. Pick based on crate dimensions, season, and which carrier's cargo team responds fastest when you call.
