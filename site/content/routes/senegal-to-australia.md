  h1: "Pet Transport from Senegal to Australia"
  overview: |
    Emirates flies Dakar to Dubai five times a week and on to Singapore without a change of airline. That's the one piece of good news on this route. Everything else runs on the same clock as any other non-approved-country move to Australia: Senegal isn't on any of DAFF's approved lists, so direct import doesn't exist as an option.

    The workaround is a 180-day residency in Singapore, DAFF's Group 2 stepping-stone country of choice for owners across Africa and Asia. Only once that period is complete, and a DAFF import permit is in hand, does the familiar 10-day Mickleham quarantine near Melbourne come into play.

    Total time from a standing start: closer to a year than a season. Cost sits well into five figures once Singapore living costs are added. Neither number is unusual for this pathway; it's the same shape as the equivalent route from India or Thailand.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Senegal sits outside DAFF's system entirely"
      body: |
        DAFF classifies exporting countries into Groups 1 through 3 for direct import, based on rabies control standards it assesses independently. Senegal has never appeared on any of these lists. That's a distinct decision from, say, the CDC's high-risk classification that applies to Senegalese dogs entering the United States; the two systems don't reference each other, and Australia's answer would be the same even if Senegal's American classification changed tomorrow.

        In practice this means BICON simply won't accept an import permit application listing Senegal as the country of departure. The pet has to already be resident somewhere DAFF does recognise.

    - heading: "A rare single-carrier stepping-stone flight"
      body: |
        Most non-approved-country routes on this site need two separate airlines for the journey to the staging country. Senegal is an exception. Emirates operates the Dakar-Dubai sector directly, roughly nine and a quarter hours, several times a week, and continues on to Singapore on its own network. One booking, one carrier, one set of cargo handling standards throughout the first leg.

        That doesn't remove the 180-day residency requirement once your pet lands in Singapore, but it does simplify the logistics of getting there, compared with piecing together connections on two or three different airlines.

    - heading: "The Singapore stage and the Australian finish"
      body: |
        Singapore runs its own risk classification for incoming pets, separate from DAFF entirely. Depending on how AVS currently treats Senegal, your pet might clear Singapore without a hold, or might need a period at Singapore's Animal Quarantine Centre first. Confirm this before booking the Dakar flight, since it changes how quickly the 180-day Australian clock can start.

        Once that clock runs out and the DAFF permit is confirmed, the final Singapore-to-Melbourne leg is routine: Singapore Airlines Cargo or Qantas Freight, then 10 days at Mickleham before your pet is released to you.

    answer: "No. Senegal doesn't appear on any of DAFF's approved country lists, so direct import isn't permitted. Your pet needs to be relocated to an approved country, typically Singapore, and live there continuously for 180 days before an Australian entry can be arranged."

    answer: "Emirates operates both the Dakar-Dubai sector and onward Dubai-Singapore flights, giving a single-carrier route for the first leg of this move. You'll still need to satisfy Singapore's own entry conditions on arrival before the 180-day Australian residency clock can begin."
  - question: "Why does Senegal count as high-risk for the US but non-approved for Australia?"
    answer: "These are two unrelated classification systems. The CDC's high-risk list governs American entry for dogs and is based on documented rabies case data. DAFF's approved country groups are Australia's own biosecurity assessment and don't reference the CDC's list at all. Senegal's absence from DAFF's system would stand even if its US classification changed."
  - question: "How long does this whole move take?"
    answer: "Plan for 45 to 60 weeks from a standing start. Most of that is the fixed 180-day Singapore residency, plus whatever Singapore's own entry process adds, the DAFF permit processing window, and the final 10-day Mickleham quarantine."
  - question: "Is there a shortcut for cats rather than dogs?"
    answer: "No. DAFF's non-approved-country pathway and the 180-day Singapore residency requirement apply equally to cats and dogs. There's no species-based exemption in the Australian system, unlike the lighter US federal rules that apply to cats specifically."
