  h1: "Pet Transport from Laos to Germany"
  overview: |
    Fifteen days is all Laos's Department of Livestock and Fisheries needs to process an export licence. That number barely registers against the far longer clock running on the German side, where Laos's status outside the EU's low-risk list triggers the full titre test and 90-day wait sequence.

    Every route out of Vientiane also transfers through a regional hub before the long-haul sector begins. Bangkok, fed by Lao Airlines' twice-daily service, is the most reliable option into Frankfurt.

  sections:
    - heading: "DLF's export process and why it rarely sets the pace"
      body: |
        The Department of Livestock and Fisheries, under the Ministry of Agriculture and Forestry, requires at least 15 days' notice before it will inspect an animal and issue the paired Animal Health Certificate and Export Licence. In practice, that window runs comfortably inside the EU's own 90-day wait, so DLF's paperwork tends to finish well before the pet is actually cleared to travel.

        The genuine constraint on this route is the German import sequence, not the Lao one. Submit the DLF application once the titre wait is close to finishing, rather than treating it as an early priority.

    - heading: "Why the titre test applies, and the Bangkok routing"
      body: |
        Laos sits outside the EU's list of low-risk third countries, so Germany requires the full sequence: microchip before vaccination, a 30-day gap before the blood draw, a result of at least 0.5 IU/ml, then a fixed 90 days from the draw date before entry.

        Few, if any, EU-approved laboratories operate inside Laos, so the blood sample typically needs to be couriered out, commonly to a laboratory in Thailand. That adds real time beyond standard lab turnaround. Once the wait period clears, Thai Airways Cargo's Bangkok-Frankfurt service, fed by Lao Airlines' Vientiane-Bangkok flights, is the most established routing, with Frankfurt's Animal Lounge handling arrival.
