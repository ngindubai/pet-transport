content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Canada to Guatemala"
  overview: |
    Neither government asks for much here. CFIA doesn't require an export permit for a personal pet leaving Canada, just a signed health certificate. Guatemala, on its side, skips the titre test and the import permit that some other origin countries face, settling for a vaccination card, a health certificate, and a small OIRSA inspection fee at the border.

    What's missing from both sides is a flight. No airline runs Canada to Guatemala City nonstop, so this route always means a connection, most reliably through Miami, San Salvador or Panama City, with Avianca or United picking up the second leg.

  sections:
    - heading: "Two light-touch paperwork sides, one real bottleneck"
      body: |
        Add up the requirements on both ends and there genuinely isn't much: a CFIA-compliant vet certificate leaving Canada, a rabies vaccination card and matching health certificate landing in Guatemala. Compare that to a route where either government demands a titre test, a pre-departure import permit, or a fixed waiting period, and this is about as uncomplicated as document requirements get on this site.

        Which is precisely why the flight matters more than usual. With so little paperwork friction to manage, the connection through a US or Central American hub becomes the entire planning task. Confirm live animal acceptance on both legs before you commit to a date, since a missed connection here isn't offset by any spare time built into the paperwork side.

    - heading: "Air Canada's seasonal service versus a dependable connection"
      body: |
        Air Canada runs limited seasonal service toward Guatemala City, but treating it as a guaranteed year-round cargo option would be a mistake without confirming current acceptance directly. Avianca's connecting network through Miami, San Salvador or Panama City, or United via Houston, are the more consistent bookings, even though neither is a single nonstop sector.

        Whichever routing you choose, get written confirmation of live animal cargo acceptance on every leg before locking in your Canadian vet appointment dates.

    - heading: "What OIRSA actually checks, and the translation detail owners miss"
      body: |
        OIRSA's inspection at the Guatemalan port of entry is a document and visual check, not a laboratory process. Bring physical copies of the health certificate and vaccination card; a photo on a phone is not a reliable substitute if the printer at the airport queue is down.

        The certificate itself has to be understood by the inspecting officer. A Canadian health certificate issued in English is common and generally accepted, but if it isn't already bilingual, arrange a Spanish translation before you fly rather than hoping it gets sorted on arrival. It rarely does, and a held pet at the border is a worse outcome than an extra afternoon with a translator beforehand.
