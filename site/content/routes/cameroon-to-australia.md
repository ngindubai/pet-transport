content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Cameroon to Australia"
  overview: |
    Six flights a week. That is how often Air France leaves Douala for Paris nonstop, and it happens to be the detail that decides how a Cameroonian pet gets to Australia at all. DAFF will not touch a direct application from Cameroon. The country sits outside every one of its approved groups, so the only route in runs through 180 unbroken days of residency in an approved country first. France, already linked to Douala by that six-times-weekly Air France service, is the practical choice.

    None of this is quick. Once the residency clock starts, a rabies titre test has to be drawn and cleared, a microchip verification has to be timed correctly to shrink Melbourne's quarantine stay from 30 days to 10, and the DAFF import permit has to be lodged through BICON before the final flight is even booked. Thirty-two weeks is closer to a floor than an estimate.

    Compare that with the same passport heading to the UAE instead, covered elsewhere on this site: MOCCAE asks for a titre test and an import permit, nothing more. No residency, no third country, no 180-day wait. Same animal, same origin, a completely different bar to clear.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why France, and why it works"
      body: |
        DAFF's rule for non-approved countries like Cameroon is blunt: 180 consecutive days living in a Group 1, 2 or 3 approved country, with the full import protocol completed from that country of residence, not from the pet's actual country of origin. France qualifies as Group 3, and Air France's existing Douala-Paris service means the first leg of this journey is a route that already exists for entirely unrelated reasons.

        That matters more than it might seem. Several non-approved-country routes on this site involve flying to a staging country with no natural air link at all, adding a detour before the residency clock can even start. Cameroon's owners get a head start simply because Air France already flies where they need to go.

    - heading: "The RNATT test and the quarantine shortcut"
      body: |
        Once resident in France, the pet needs a rabies vaccination on record and an RNATT blood draw at least 30 days later, with the sample reaching an approved laboratory somewhere between 180 days and a year before the Australia-bound flight. Getting the timing wrong here does not just delay the test, it can also cost weeks of quarantine at the other end.

        Here is the detail that is easy to miss. Mickleham's quarantine period drops from a default 30 days to a minimum of 10 days, but only if the pet's microchip identity is officially verified before the RNATT blood draw, and that verification happens at least 180 days before export. Book the verification appointment early, deliberately, and in that order.

    - heading: "Getting from Paris to Melbourne"
      body: |
        Qantas does not currently fly Paris to Australia directly, so most agents route pet cargo through Singapore instead, using Singapore Airlines and the SATS Coolport live animal handling facility at Changi, one of the better-regarded transshipment points for this kind of cargo. Singapore itself happens to be a DAFF Group 2 country, so the transit point sits inside the approved-country network even though it is not where the residency was served.

        Every arriving pet, whatever the routing, lands at Melbourne and goes straight to Mickleham. There is no other quarantine facility in the country, and no way to choose a different port of entry regardless of the eventual Australian city.

  - question: "Why is France recommended as the staging country for this route?"
    answer: "France is a DAFF Group 3 approved country, and Air France already flies Douala to Paris nonstop six times a week. That existing air link means the 180-day residency can begin on the very first flight out of Cameroon, without a detour to reach a country with no natural connection."
  - question: "How long does Mickleham quarantine last for a pet on this route?"
    answer: "A minimum of 10 days, but only if the microchip is officially verified before the RNATT titre blood draw and that verification takes place at least 180 days before export. Get the sequencing wrong and the quarantine period extends to 30 days. Source: DAFF."
  - question: "Does a Cameroonian pet need this same 180-day process to enter the UAE instead?"
    answer: "No. MOCCAE, the UAE's regulator, treats Cameroon as a high-risk country requiring a titre test and an import permit, but sets no residency requirement in a third country and no fixed waiting period after a passing titre result. The two destinations apply very different standards to the same passport."
  - question: "Is Melbourne the only place a pet from Cameroon can arrive in Australia?"
    answer: "Yes, for quarantine purposes. Mickleham Post Entry Quarantine Facility in Melbourne is the only DAFF-approved facility for imported cats and dogs, so every pet lands there regardless of the eventual Australian destination city."
