  overview: "Five months. That's the realistic minimum for getting a dog or cat from Beirut into Portugal once you account for the titre test wait and Lebanese paperwork. Portugal treats Lebanon as an EU-unlisted third country, so the full sequence applies: microchip, rabies vaccination, an FAVN titre test at an EU-approved lab, and a 90-day wait counted from the blood draw itself. DGAV, Portugal's food and veterinary authority, checks every document against the animal at Lisbon's cargo terminal on arrival. Cats face exactly the same requirements as dogs."

  sections:
    - heading: "Flights from Beirut to Lisbon: the four cargo options"
      body: |
        Turkish Airlines Cargo is usually the easiest booking from Beirut (BEY), routing through Istanbul (IST) with regular onward capacity to Lisbon (LIS). Its network reach across Europe makes it a sensible default. Lufthansa Cargo runs BEY to Frankfurt (FRA), where pets pass through the dedicated Frankfurt Animal Lounge before a connecting flight to Lisbon. That facility is properly staffed for live animal handling, which matters on a long journey. Emirates SkyCargo connects BEY to Dubai (DXB) and then on to Lisbon. Reliable, but the Dubai leg adds transit hours your pet has to sit through. Qatar Airways Cargo offers a similar Gulf routing via Doha (DOH), also dependable, though summer heat embargoes at Gulf hubs can restrict live animal bookings on certain dates.

        Whichever carrier you choose, book the cargo space only once your Ministry of Agriculture endorsement and titre test result are confirmed. Airlines will not hold a booking indefinitely, and pet cargo slots on any of these routes fill up during peak relocation months.

    - heading: "The FAVN sequence and why the 90 days matter"
      body: |
        Lebanon sits outside the EU's list of approved third countries, so Portugal applies the full unlisted-country process under EU Regulation 576/2013. Microchip first. Rabies vaccination second, never the other way round. Wait 30 days, then draw blood for the FAVN titre test at an EU-approved laboratory. The result needs to clear 0.5 IU/ml.

        Here's the part people miss: the 90-day countdown starts on the day blood is drawn, not the day the lab sends back the result. Results can take two to four weeks. If you wait for the result before mentally starting the clock, you have lost a month you did not need to lose. Plan the whole timeline around the blood draw date from day one.

        Add it up: vaccination, 30-day wait, blood draw, 90-day wait. That's a minimum of four months before Portugal will even consider letting your pet in, and that is before Lebanese admin is factored in.

    - heading: "What happens when your pet lands at Lisbon"
      body: |
        DGAV, Portugal's food and veterinary authority, checks every arriving pet against its paperwork at the LIS cargo terminal. The health certificate has to be in EU format, endorsed by the Lebanese Ministry of Agriculture, and no older than 10 days from the vet's examination date to the day of travel. A mismatch between the microchip number on the certificate and the chip actually read from your pet's scanner is one of the most common hold-ups DGAV flags.

        There's no routine quarantine for a pet that arrives with everything in order. That is the reward for getting the paperwork right. But an incomplete file, a chip number typo, or a certificate that has expired by even a day can mean your pet sits at the cargo terminal while the paperwork gets sorted, at your expense. Build a buffer of a few days either side of the 10-day certificate window so a delayed flight does not invalidate the whole document.
