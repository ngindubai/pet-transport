content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Tunisia to the United States"
  overview: |
    A dog leaving Tunisia for the US carries a heavier compliance load than the flight itself suggests. Tunisia sits on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies, last confirmed 15 April 2026, which triggers a titer test, a mandatory reservation at one of just six CDC-registered animal care facilities nationwide, and entry restricted to the airport where that facility sits.

    Cats are a different story almost entirely. No federal rabies paperwork applies to them at all, just a general health certificate and standard airline documentation.

    There is no nonstop service between Tunis and the US. Every itinerary runs through a European hub first, usually on Tunisair into Paris, Frankfurt or Rome, before a second carrier picks up the transatlantic leg. Budget three to four months for a dog, considerably less for a cat.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why dogs and cats sit on completely different tracks"
      body: |
        The CDC's post-2024 framework is built around dog rabies risk specifically. Cats fall outside it. A cat travelling from Tunisia needs a DGSV-endorsed health certificate and normal airline paperwork, nothing more at the federal border level. Some individual US states ask for proof of rabies vaccination once a cat takes up residence there, so check the destination state separately: that's a state matter, not a CDC one.

        Dogs carry the full weight of the high-risk-country process. Minimum age of 12 weeks for the first vaccination. That vaccination given at least 28 days before entry. A titer drawn at least 30 days after vaccination and at least 28 days before entry. A completed CDC Dog Import Form generating a single-entry receipt. Miss any one element, and the dog is diverted into the 28-day quarantine track rather than walking straight through the facility.

        Get the dates wrong by even a few days and the whole sequence has to restart. That's the part owners underestimate most.

    - heading: "No direct flight, so plan the connection like it's part of the paperwork"
      body: |
        Tunis-Carthage International Airport has no service to the United States. Tunisair's own network reaches Paris CDG, Frankfurt, Rome and Madrid, and from any of those hubs a second carrier, Air France, Lufthansa or Delta among them, covers the transatlantic sector.

        That means treating the connection itself as a compliance step, not just a scheduling detail. The dog's arrival airport in the US has to be one of the six CDC-registered facility airports (Miami, JFK, Atlanta, Los Angeles, Philadelphia or Washington Dulles), so the second leg has to terminate there specifically. Frankfurt into Lufthansa's Miami or JFK service is currently one of the more reliable pairings; Paris into an Air France or Delta onward flight is the alternative most owners use.

        Confirm pet acceptance separately with each carrier. A booking that works on paper for Tunisair's first leg can still fail if the connecting airline's cargo hold is embargoed for heat, or simply has no space left on that particular flight.

    - heading: "What the titer test actually buys you"
      body: |
        A passing FAVN or equivalent titer result, at least 0.5 IU/ml, is what separates a dog that walks through the CDC-registered facility in under an hour from one that gets held for 28 days at the owner's expense. The USD 2,500-plus quarantine cost dwarfs the USD 150 to 300 the test itself runs. There is no argument for skipping it on a high-risk-country route, whatever timeline pressure exists.
