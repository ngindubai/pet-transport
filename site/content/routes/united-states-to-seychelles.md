content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from United States to Seychelles: 2026 Guide"
  overview: |
    Seychelles is genuinely rabies-free, which leads plenty of owners to assume a US-origin pet gets an easy ride. It doesn't. The National Biosecurity Agency's own import rules treat almost every visiting country, the US included, as a rabies-affected origin, because the US itself isn't on the short list of countries Seychelles formally recognises as free of the disease.

    That triggers the full pathway: a titre test, a fixed three-month wait from the blood draw, and a mandatory month in Seychelles' only quarantine facility on Mahe. Realistically, this route runs five to seven months from a standing start.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why rabies-free doesn't mean quarantine-free"
      body: |
        Seychelles' 2024 import rules make quarantine mandatory by law for every arriving pet. The only variable is length: four weeks for a fully compliant animal from a rabies-affected origin, and the US falls squarely into that bracket, alongside the UK and most of Europe, since none of them sit on Seychelles' own recognised rabies-free list.

        The titre test itself needs a result above 0.5 IU/mL, and Seychelles then adds a fixed three-month wait counted from the blood draw date, not the result date. Combined with the NBA's own recommended four-month permit lead time, this sits among the longer routes anywhere in this build.

    - heading: "A wider disease panel than most destinations ask for"
      body: |
        Beyond rabies, Seychelles asks for something unusual: dogs need clearance for Brucella canis, Trypanosoma evansi, Babesia gibsoni, Leishmania and Erlichia canis, unless the exporting country's vet can certify the US free of a given disease. It's a genuinely broader health screen than most of the destinations covered in this build, and worth raising with the USDA-accredited vet early rather than discovering it close to the travel date.
