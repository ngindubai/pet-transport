content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Algeria to the United States"
  overview: |
    Algeria sits on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies, which hands every dog owner on this route a genuine choice: a rabies antibody titre test, or a 28-day quarantine at a CDC-registered facility, at full cost, on arrival. There's no way around picking one.

    Geography adds its own layer. No airline flies Algiers to the US directly, so the flight itself has to go through Istanbul or Paris, both genuinely capable of handling the cargo booking, neither a shortcut. A cat sidesteps almost the entire process; a dog does not.

  sections:
    - heading: "The choice the CDC hands every high-risk-country dog"
      body: |
        Algeria's place on the CDC's high-risk list means a dog's foreign vaccination record alone isn't enough. The Certification of Foreign Rabies Vaccination and Microchip form, endorsed by DSV, has to accompany a rabies antibody titre test, blood drawn at least 30 days after vaccination and at least 28 days before the flight.

        Pass that test and there's no quarantine, just the mandatory exam and revaccination at a CDC-registered facility on arrival. Skip it, or miss the timing window, and the alternative is a 28-day quarantine at the same type of facility, run entirely at the owner's expense. Both paths still require the reservation to be booked in advance.

    - heading: "No direct flight, two workable hubs"
      body: |
        Air Algérie doesn't serve the United States or Istanbul, so its own network is irrelevant to this particular move despite covering plenty of European routes. Turkish Airlines via Istanbul carries the pet through Turkish Cargo's AVI service and its Pet Lounge for longer layovers, with onward links to a wide spread of US cities.

        Air France's Algiers to Paris route is one of the busiest connections out of North Africa, and CDG's long-haul network reaches most major US gateways from there. Either hub works; the deciding factor is usually which one lines up with the reserved date at the CDC-registered facility on the US end.

    - heading: "Algeria's own paperwork, and where it meets the CDC form"
      body: |
        DSV's endorsement process for a standard export certificate is well established: French or Arabic documentation, checked and signed off before the pet leaves. The complication is that the CDC's own form for foreign-vaccinated dogs is in English, and DSV's usual workflow doesn't obviously say how it wants to handle that mismatch.

        The safe approach is a direct question to DSV well before travel, alongside arranging a certified translation as a backup, rather than assuming the standard export certificate automatically satisfies the American form's endorsement requirement.

    - heading: "What changes for a cat"
      body: |
        Almost everything. None of the CDC's high-risk classification, the titre test or the facility reservation applies to cats travelling from Algeria. A cat needs DSV's standard export certificate, the same document any pet leaving Algeria requires, and clears the US border on that alone.
