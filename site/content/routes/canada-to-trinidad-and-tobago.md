  h1: "Pet Transport from Canada to Trinidad and Tobago"
  overview: |
    Air Canada and Caribbean Airlines both fly nonstop between Toronto and Piarco, between them covering roughly 10 to 13 flights a week. Getting a booking is rarely the bottleneck on this route. The paperwork is.

    Trinidad and Tobago's Chief Veterinary Officer places Canada in Category 2 on its rabies risk list, the same tier as the United States and mainland Mexico. That means a FAVN or RFFIT titre test, a formal import permit from the Chief Veterinary Officer, and a health certificate timed to land within 7 days of arrival. None of that is waived just because two airlines fly the route directly.

  sections:
    - heading: "Category 2, and why it surprises Canada-based owners"
      body: |
        Canada's own import rules for pets arriving from most countries are light. No titre test, no import permit for a personal move, just a rabies certificate with the right details on it. Owners planning the reverse trip sometimes expect Trinidad and Tobago to be equally relaxed toward them.

        It is not. The Ministry of Agriculture, Land and Fisheries sorts every origin country into a category list, last revised 5 January 2023, and Canada sits in Category 2 rather than the more forgiving Category 1 reserved for a shorter list of rabies-free-listed nations. A Category 2 pet needs a FAVN or RFFIT titre test, blood drawn at least a month after vaccination, with the result sent directly to the Chief Veterinary Officer rather than simply filed with the owner's paperwork.

        There is one piece of good news buried in the rules: Trinidad and Tobago has run without routine quarantine for compliant pets since April 2013. Get the documents right and your dog or cat walks off the plane, no holding facility involved.

    - heading: "Two nonstop carriers, one runway"
      body: |
        Toronto Pearson to Piarco is a genuine nonstop sector on two separate airlines, which is not something every Trinidad and Tobago route on this site can say. Air Canada flies it around four times a week on Boeing 737 aircraft, flight numbers AC984 and AC1460. Caribbean Airlines, the flag carrier, runs it more frequently still, closer to 10 to 13 flights a week in both directions combined.

        That gives real scheduling flexibility once the regulatory side is sorted. It does not remove the brachycephalic restriction both carriers apply to their holds year-round, so a flat-faced breed still needs a specialist conversation before you commit to a travel date.

    - heading: "Budgeting for the titre test process"
      body: |
        Most of the CAD 2,000-4,000 total on this route sits in three places: the titre test itself, the cargo or checked-baggage fare, and agent fees if you use one. The lab test runs CAD 220-400, which is modest next to the flight cost, but the real expense of a failed or delayed result is time, not money. A titre that comes back below 0.5 IU/mL means revaccinating and starting the 1-month wait again from scratch.

        Trinidad's Carnival, usually in February, is worth building around rather than through. Seat availability on both Air Canada and Caribbean Airlines tightens in the weeks either side of it, and cargo handling capacity gets squeezed by the same demand. Booking the flight leg 6 to 8 weeks out, once the titre result and import permit are close to confirmed, avoids paying a peak-season premium on top of an already document-heavy move.
