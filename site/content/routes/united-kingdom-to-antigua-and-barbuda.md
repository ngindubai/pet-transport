  overview: |
    APHA has a certificate with Antigua and Barbuda's name on it: number 6592, now on its third version, published through gov.uk's export health certificate service. That level of destination-specific detail isn't universal across this site's routes, and it makes the UK side of this move more predictable than most.

    Antigua and Barbuda's own requirements turn on one fact: the UK is a rabies-free country. That single classification means the rabies vaccination and titre pathway that applies to arrivals from the US, Canada or Trinidad simply doesn't apply here. Instead, the Veterinary and Livestock Division wants a Veterinary Import License, applied for at least two weeks ahead, and a health certificate covering the standard core vaccinations.

    Only one airline flies this route nonstop, and it doesn't carry pets in the cabin. Budget 6 to 8 weeks, most of it paperwork rather than flight logistics.

  sections:
    - heading: "What certificate 6592 actually involves"
      body: |
        Export Health Certificate 6592 is applied for online, through a Government Gateway and Defra account for owners in England, Scotland or Wales, or through DAERA's own portal for Northern Ireland. An APHA-registered official vet completes and signs it. The guidance stresses using the current version, now version 3, since an outdated form can be rejected on arrival.

        This is a genuinely different starting point from destinations where no dedicated certificate exists at all. It doesn't remove the need for a vet appointment or planning time, but it does mean the paperwork template is already settled rather than adapted from a general form.

    - heading: "Antigua's own side: an import licence, not a titre test"
      body: |
        Because the UK counts as rabies-free under Antigua and Barbuda's classification, the pet doesn't need a rabies vaccination certificate or a titre test to enter. What it does need is a Veterinary Import License from the Veterinary and Livestock Division, applied for at least two weeks before travel, and an Official Government Health Certificate confirming the standard core vaccinations rather than a rabies-specific one.

        One breed rule is worth flagging early: pitbulls, and dogs that could pass for one, are not permitted to land in Antigua and Barbuda at all. A dog of an ambiguous type needs kennel club certification proving it isn't a pitbull before booking, not after.

faqs:
  - question: "Does my pet need a rabies vaccination to enter Antigua and Barbuda from the UK?"
    answer: "No. Because the UK is a rabies-free country, pets travelling from it follow Antigua and Barbuda's rabies-free-country pathway, which asks for core vaccinations, distemper, hepatitis, leptospirosis, parvovirus and parainfluenza for dogs, rather than a rabies vaccination certificate. The rabies-vaccination pathway applies to arrivals from rabies-endemic countries such as the US or Canada."
  - question: "What is Export Health Certificate 6592?"
    answer: "It's APHA's dedicated export certificate for cats and dogs travelling from Great Britain to Antigua and Barbuda, currently on its third version. It's applied for online through APHA's export service, with a separate DAERA process for Northern Ireland, and completed by an APHA-registered vet."
  - question: "Can pitbulls travel to Antigua and Barbuda?"
    answer: "No. Antigua and Barbuda does not permit dogs of the pitbull breed to enter. Dogs that physically resemble the breed need certification from a recognised kennel club confirming they are not pitbulls, arranged well before booking travel."
  - question: "Is there a nonstop flight from the UK to Antigua?"
    answer: "Yes, one. British Airways flies Gatwick to Antigua as BA2157, roughly 8 hours 50 minutes on a Boeing 777, and it's the only nonstop service. Pets travel as cargo only, booked through PetAir UK, since British Airways doesn't carry pets in the cabin on any route."
  - question: "How long does the Veterinary Import License take?"
    answer: "Apply at least 2 weeks before the intended travel date, per the Veterinary and Livestock Division's published guidance. Starting earlier gives a buffer against any delay in processing or in gathering the supporting vaccination records."
