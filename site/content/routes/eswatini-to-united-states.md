  h1: "Pet Transport from Eswatini to United States"
  overview: |
    Two high-risk classifications stack up on this route. Eswatini is CDC high-risk for dog rabies. So, separately, is South Africa, the country every pet transits through on the way out. Neither fact alone is unusual for African routes to the US. Together, they mean the Johannesburg connection needs more care than a typical transfer.

    Add the six-airport rule. Only Atlanta, Los Angeles, Miami, JFK, Philadelphia and Washington Dulles have a CDC-registered animal care facility, and every dog from a high-risk country must land at one of them. Delta's Johannesburg to Atlanta nonstop hits that mark directly. United's Johannesburg to Newark nonstop, popular as it is, does not, because Newark isn't on the list.

    Cats sidestep almost all of this. No CDC process touches them. What doesn't change for either species is the flight reality: nothing leaves Eswatini directly for anywhere long-haul. Johannesburg is the gateway, every time.
  sections:
    - heading: "Why the airport you land at matters more than the airline"
      body: |
        The CDC's high-risk pathway for dogs requires a serology titre test or a 28-day quarantine, plus a vet certification endorsed by Eswatini's Department of Veterinary and Livestock Services. That part is standard for any high-risk-country route.

        What's specific to this corridor is the choice between Delta and United out of Johannesburg. Delta flies nonstop to Atlanta, one of the six CDC-facility airports. United flies nonstop to Newark, which currently has none. A dog cannot legally complete this journey on the United routing, because landing anywhere without a CDC facility and then connecting domestically isn't permitted for a high-risk-country dog, titre test or not. Check the current CDC list before booking regardless; airport facilities can be added or removed.

    - heading: "The Johannesburg transit: two high-risk countries, one connection"
      body: |
        South Africa is itself on the CDC's high-risk list, separate from Eswatini's own classification. For a passenger, transiting Johannesburg airside is routine. For a dog in cargo, the transfer between the regional flight from Eswatini and the international flight to the US needs to happen as a genuine interline handover, without the animal being processed as though it were entering South Africa.

        This is a logistics point, not a regulatory one currently spelled out in fine detail by the CDC for transit-only stops, but it's worth confirming directly with your cargo agent and the receiving CDC facility before you commit to a routing. A properly arranged airside transfer avoids any ambiguity about which countries the dog has actually "been in" during its journey.
