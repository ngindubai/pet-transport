  h1: "Pet Transport from Ivory Coast to the United Kingdom"
  overview: |
    Cote d'Ivoire doesn't appear on either of the UK's rabies-listed country tables, Part 1 or Part 2, placing it in the same unlisted bracket as most of West Africa. That single classification decides almost everything else about this route: a rabies titre test, a 90-day wait after a passing result, and a preparation window measured in months rather than weeks.

    The logistics side is more workable than the paperwork suggests. Royal Air Maroc and Ethiopian Airlines both run genuine single-carrier one-stop connections into London Heathrow, via Casablanca and Addis Ababa respectively, keeping a pet under one airline's care for the whole journey rather than transferring between carriers mid-route.

    Budget four and a half to five and a half months from the first vet visit to wheels-down at Heathrow. Most of that time is the titre test's own fixed clock, not anything a pet transport agent can speed up.
  sections:
    - heading: "Why the titre test drives the whole timeline"
      body: |
        The UK's pet travel rules split the world into listed and unlisted countries. Listed countries, mostly the EU plus a short list of approved nations, skip the titre test entirely. Cote d'Ivoire sits outside both Part 1 and Part 2, so it doesn't get that shortcut.

        The test itself isn't complicated: a blood sample, drawn at least 30 days after the rabies vaccination, sent to an APHA-approved laboratory, checking for an antibody level of at least 0.5 IU/ml. What makes it slow is the mandatory 90-day wait that follows a passing result, a wait that exists regardless of how quickly the lab turns the sample around. Fail the test and the whole 30-day-plus-90-day clock restarts from a fresh blood draw.

        There's no way to compress this. Owners who start the process assuming a few weeks' notice will be disappointed; four and a half months is closer to reality once vaccination timing and lab scheduling are factored in.
    - heading: "Getting the Ivorian paperwork right: DSV and MIRAH"
      body: |
        The Direction des Services Veterinaires, operating under the Ministry of Animal and Fisheries Resources, is the government body that has to endorse the export health certificate before any airline will accept it at Abidjan. Unlike some neighbouring countries' veterinary authorities, DSV doesn't publish a standard processing time or fee schedule online.

        That's not a reason to panic, but it is a reason to start early and confirm details directly with a DSV-registered vet rather than assuming a fixed timeline. Build a buffer of at least two weeks into the paperwork stage on top of the titre test's own schedule.

        The health certificate itself needs to be dated within 10 days of travel, so it's the last document to arrange, not the first. Everything else, microchip, vaccination, titre test, DSV endorsement, has to be locked in before this final step makes sense.
    - heading: "Choosing a route out of Abidjan"
      body: |
        Royal Air Maroc's Abidjan to Casablanca to London Heathrow routing and Ethiopian Airlines' Abidjan to Addis Ababa to London Heathrow routing both keep the pet on a single carrier's network for the entire journey, which reduces the handling risk that comes with transferring an animal between airlines mid-trip.

        Air France runs up to two flights daily between Abidjan and Paris, giving the most frequent departures of any option here, though a separate onward leg to London still needs arranging. Brussels Airlines only flies the Abidjan to Brussels sector once a week, so it works best as a backup rather than a primary plan unless your travel date happens to align with the Saturday departure.

        Whichever carrier you choose, check the breed restrictions early. Snub-nosed dogs and cats face limits or outright exclusion from the hold on every airline covering this route.
