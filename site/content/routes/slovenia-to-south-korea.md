  sections:
    - heading: "Getting out of Ljubljana: why Frankfurt is the default hub"
      body: |
        Ljubljana Joze Pucnik Airport has a limited long-haul footprint. Lufthansa is the dominant operator here, running around 22 weekly departures, almost all to Frankfurt, with a seasonal service to Munich. That makes Frankfurt the natural first stop for a pet cargo booking, connecting onward to Lufthansa's nonstop FRA-ICN service of roughly 11 hours 20 minutes.

        A Vienna routing via Austrian Airlines is worth asking your cargo agent about, since Lufthansa Group's regional network touches Vienna too, but it should be treated as a possible alternative rather than a confirmed standing option. For most bookings out of Ljubljana, Frankfurt with Lufthansa is the more dependable answer.

        Whichever hub you use, book both legs of the journey together. A live animal shipment with a missed connection is a different order of problem than a delayed suitcase.
    - heading: "The UVHVVR certificate and what changes on arrival"
      body: |
        South Korea does not accept the EU pet passport for this route. You need a Korean QIAS-format certificate instead, issued by a UVHVVR-authorised official veterinarian and endorsed by UVHVVR, Slovenia's food safety and veterinary authority, before departure. This authorisation is centralised in Ljubljana, so factor in travel time if you live elsewhere in the country.

        The certificate carries a 10-day validity window from issue, so book the appointment close to departure rather than weeks ahead. Getting the timing wrong in either direction, too early and it expires, too late and it's not ready, is the most common planning error on this route.

        On arrival at Incheon, QIAS holds EU-origin pets for inspection, typically 1 to 3 days. That's considerably shorter than the quarantine period for pets from rabies-endemic non-EU countries, but your pet will still spend at least one day at the facility before release.
