content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Canada to Montenegro"
  overview: |
    Canada carries its own listing on the EU's approved rabies country list, the same status that makes the Moldova and North Macedonia routes on this site genuinely light. None of that helps here. Montenegro's published guidance describes a titre test requirement with no exemption named for any country of origin, EU members, the UK, the US and Canada all included.

    A FAVN or RNATT test drawn at least 30 days after vaccination, then a wait of more than three calendar months from a passing result, sets the real timeline. That's a different shape entirely to Albania or North Macedonia, two other EU-candidate states covered on this site, both of which extend the same exemption Canada gets elsewhere in the region.

  sections:
    - heading: "A titre test that doesn't discriminate by origin"
      body: |
        Several Western Balkan candidate states handle rabies serology the same way Brussels does: a pet arriving from a country on the EU's Annex II list skips the blood test. North Macedonia works this way for a Canadian pet. Moldova works this way too.

        Montenegro, on the guidance available, does not. The requirement reads as a flat rule regardless of where the pet is coming from: FAVN or RNATT test at least 30 days post-vaccination, then more than three calendar months' wait from a passing result. No carve-out for Canada's own approved-country status turned up in any source checked for this page.

        That gap between sources is itself the honest finding here. Montenegro's own AFSVPA is the authority to check with directly, rather than assuming a rule that applies elsewhere in the region carries over. Build the three-month wait into planning regardless; having the paperwork ready early costs nothing, and finding out too late that it was needed costs a great deal.

    - heading: "Getting to Podgorica from a Canadian gateway"
      body: |
        Lufthansa's direct Toronto-Frankfurt or Toronto-Munich services connect onward to Podgorica or Tivat, using Frankfurt's dedicated Animal Lounge for the hold transfer and keeping the pet within a single airline group's handling chain. Austrian Airlines runs its own direct Toronto-Vienna service roughly six times a week, connecting on to Podgorica, though it will not carry a flat-faced dog or cat in the hold under any circumstances.

        Turkish Airlines flies both Toronto-Istanbul and Istanbul-Podgorica sectors, which looks like an obvious third option on paper. In practice, confirm pet acceptance on this specific combination directly with the airline before relying on it; treat Lufthansa or Austrian as the dependable choices and Turkish as one to verify rather than assume.
