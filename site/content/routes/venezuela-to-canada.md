content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Venezuela to Canada"
  overview: |
    Every other Canada route built on this site so far has treated CFIA's high-risk rabies list as somebody else's problem. Not this one. Venezuela has sat on that list since at least 13 March 2025, confirmed still current when this page was researched on 16 July 2026, which puts a Venezuela-origin dog through a different process than, say, a Brunei-origin dog landing at the same airport.

    None of that involves a titre test. CFIA does not run the CDC's serology-and-quarantine approach for high-risk countries; it runs its own set of conditions instead, built around microchip timing, a minimum age at entry, and a short list of airports equipped to receive these dogs. Layer that on top of INSAI's own export checklist, and a closed main airport in Caracas, and eight to twelve weeks is a realistic planning window, not a worst case.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Venezuela is not a Brunei-style Canada route"
      body: |
        CFIA splits the world into countries it treats as ordinary and countries it flags as high-risk for dog rabies. Venezuela falls into the second group, alongside most of Latin America bar a handful of exceptions such as Argentina, Chile and Mexico.

        For a dog, that status changes three things: the microchip has to go in before, or on the same day as, the rabies vaccination, not simply "recommended" as it is for a standard import. Current guidance also points to a minimum age at the point of Canadian entry, on top of the usual three-month vaccination floor. And entry is steered toward a shorter list of major airports with a CFIA presence, rather than any Canadian port of entry. Cats sidestep almost all of it, since CFIA sets no federal rabies vaccination requirement for cats in the first place.

    - heading: "Getting out of Venezuela while Maiquetia is closed"
      body: |
        Avianca has kept a Bogota to Toronto nonstop service running throughout the disruption, flights AV204 and AV254, roughly 18 times a week. Since Maiquetia's closure the airline has added a Bogota to Valencia contingency leg on top of that, which means a Venezuela-origin pet can, in principle, stay on one carrier from Valencia through Bogota and on to Toronto.

        That is not guaranteed to hold for months. Copa Airlines offers an alternative via Panama, connecting to Air Canada's own Panama City service if Avianca's routing does not suit your dates. Reconfirm cargo capacity close to the actual travel date either way, since these are contingency flights layered onto a still-recovering airport, not a restored permanent timetable.

    - heading: "What is solid and what needs a direct check with CFIA"
      body: |
        Venezuela's presence on CFIA's high-risk list is confirmed directly against the current published list. That part is solid. What is less settled, at least in what is publicly documented outside CFIA's own interactive tool, is the exact detail behind two specific points: whether a personal pet dog from a high-risk country needs its own CFIA import permit, on top of the baseline rule that personal pet imports of one or two animals normally do not, and the precise minimum age figure at entry.

        Current third-party guidance is fairly consistent on both points, but consistency across secondary sources is not the same as a confirmed primary source. Given how much weight these two details carry for a Venezuela-origin move, treat them as needing a direct CFIA or AIRS check before committing to vaccination dates or flight bookings, not as settled fact.
