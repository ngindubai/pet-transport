content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Papua New Guinea to the United Kingdom"
  overview: |
    Papua New Guinea sits outside both of the UK's rabies-listed country groups, Part 1 and Part 2. That single classification, confirmed against the retained EU Annex II list DEFRA still applies to Great Britain, adds a mandatory titre test and a 90-day wait to a route that already has no nonstop flight.

    None of that makes the move impossible. It makes it slow. A dog or cat leaving Port Moresby needs the full unlisted-country pathway: microchip, vaccination, a blood draw sent to an approved lab, then three months of waiting before travel can even be booked. Layer on a routing through Brisbane or Singapore and a local pet export industry that handles very few UK-bound cases, and 22 to 28 weeks becomes a realistic figure, not a padded one.

    Cats and dogs face identical UK-side rules on this route, since the titre requirement comes from the country classification, not the species.

  sections:
    - heading: "What unlisted status actually adds"
      body: |
        Every non-EU country sits in one of three UK buckets: Part 1, Part 2, or unlisted. Papua New Guinea is in the third. Listed countries skip the blood test entirely; unlisted countries never do, regardless of how the disease situation looks on the ground.

        The blood has to be drawn at least 30 days after vaccination and tested to a minimum 0.5 IU/ml at an APHA-approved lab. Then comes the 90-day wait, counted from the draw date. Draw the sample too early, or lose the paperwork trail somewhere along the courier route to an overseas lab, and the whole 30-day step has to be repeated before the 90-day clock can even start.

    - heading: "Getting a pet out of Port Moresby"
      body: |
        NAQIA runs the export side from its Port Moresby headquarters. There's no separate "export permit" form; the government-endorsed health certificate, issued within 14 days of travel after an exam inside the final 48 hours, does that job. RSPCA PNG can help arrange the paperwork and the airway bill with the cargo handler, which matters on a corridor where few agents have handled this exact route before.

        Air Niugini is the only realistic way out of Port Moresby, flying to Brisbane and to Singapore. Neither destination is London, so a second carrier picks up from there.

    - heading: "Two carriers, two hubs, one crate"
      body: |
        Via Brisbane, Qantas connects onward, but pets to or from Australia have to be booked through a registered pet travel specialist rather than a standard cargo desk, and its crate limits (115 x 60 x 80cm, 42kg maximum) rule out larger dogs on that leg. Via Singapore, Singapore Airlines runs a nonstop cargo service into Heathrow, which for many owners turns out to be the cleaner second leg.

        Either way, the crate has to satisfy every carrier on the itinerary, not just the first one. Confirm dimensions with each cargo desk before booking, not after.

faqs:

    answer: "Yes. Papua New Guinea is not on the UK's Part 1 or Part 2 list of rabies-listed countries, so it is treated as unlisted. That means a rabies titre test, blood drawn at least 30 days after vaccination, tested at an APHA-approved FAVN or ELISA lab, followed by a 90-day wait from the draw date before travel."
  - question: "Is there a direct flight from Port Moresby to London?"
    answer: "No. Air Niugini flies out of Port Moresby to Brisbane and Singapore, but neither reaches London directly. Qantas can connect from Brisbane, and Singapore Airlines runs a nonstop cargo service from Singapore to Heathrow, so most owners route through one of those two hubs."
  - question: "Will my pet need to quarantine on arrival in the UK?"
    answer: "Not if the paperwork is right. There's no routine quarantine for pets arriving with a valid AHC, correct vaccination timing, and a passed titre test. Penalty quarantine of up to 120 days only applies when something in the documentation or route is wrong."
  - question: "Why does Papua New Guinea need the titre test when some Pacific countries don't?"
    answer: "It's the UK's country classification that decides this, not the local rabies situation. Papua New Guinea isn't on either the Part 1 or Part 2 list, so it defaults to the unlisted-country pathway, which always includes the titre test. Some other Pacific nations are listed and skip that step; Papua New Guinea currently is not."
