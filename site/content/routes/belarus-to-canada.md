  h1: "Pet Transport from Belarus to Canada"
  overview: |
    Two separate governments grounded Belarusian aircraft, on two different dates, for two different reasons. Brussels and London acted within days of the May 2021 Ryanair diversion. Canada followed nearly a year later, in March 2022, banning Belarusian and Russian aircraft together in response to Belarus's role in the invasion of Ukraine. Neither restriction has been reversed. For a pet owner, the practical result is identical either way: no flight leaves Minsk for anywhere in the West.

    Belarus also sits on CFIA's list of countries it treats as high-risk for dog rabies. That doesn't mean a titre test, the safeguard the US CDC uses on the same route. CFIA's own tool is an age rule instead.

  sections:
    - heading: "Two bans, two dates, one identical outcome"
      body: |
        Belavia's exclusion from EU and UK airspace began within days of the forced Ryanair landing in May 2021. Canada's own suspension came later and on separate grounds entirely, announced by Transport Minister Omar Alghabra in March 2022 as part of a wider measure covering both Belarusian and Russian aircraft, tied to Belarus's support for the invasion of Ukraine rather than the earlier hijacking incident.

        Neither ban has a published end date; both remain in force until further notice. For a dog or cat leaving Belarus, the two separate origins of the restriction don't change anything practical. There's no scheduled service out of Minsk to Canada, the EU or the UK, and every route starts with a car.

    - heading: "CFIA's high-risk rule, and the one thing it doesn't ask for"
      body: |
        Belarus appears on CFIA's list of countries at high-risk for dog rabies, last reviewed 13 March 2025. That triggers a 15-digit microchip requirement, a CFIA import permit, and entry restricted to Toronto Pearson, Montreal Trudeau, Vancouver, Calgary or Halifax. Compare that to the US route covered elsewhere on this site, where the CDC's equivalent high-risk classification forces a rabies titre test or a 28-day facility quarantine.

        CFIA doesn't ask for either. Its safeguard is a minimum age instead: a dog must be at least 7 months old at entry if it originated in, lived in, or transited Belarus within the previous 6 months. No blood test, no laboratory turnaround measured in weeks, just a date on the calendar that has to fall after the puppy's birth by the right margin.

    - heading: "Getting from Minsk to a Canadian airport"
      body: |
        Vilnius, about 184km and 2 hours 40 minutes by road via the M7, is the nearer option. Warsaw, roughly 550km away, carries more onward capacity, including LOT Polish Airlines' nonstop Warsaw-Toronto service, six times a week on a Boeing 787. That single flight is the one genuinely straightforward part of this route once the overland leg is behind it.

        Frankfurt is a workable alternative for owners closer to a route through Germany, with both Air Canada Cargo and Lufthansa Cargo running freighter and cargo capacity onward to Toronto or Montreal. Lufthansa's Animal Lounge at Frankfurt eases a longer layover if the connection doesn't line up cleanly.

    - heading: "A cat skips almost all of this"
      body: |
        CFIA's high-risk framework is built around dog rabies specifically. A cat leaving Belarus needs the standard Belarusian export certificate and a basic vaccination record, nothing close to the age rule, the import permit, or the designated-airport restriction a dog faces. The overland transfer and the flight booking still apply, since Belarus's airspace ban doesn't discriminate by species, but the paperwork burden drops sharply.
