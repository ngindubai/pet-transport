  h1: "Pet Transport from Canada to Paraguay"
  overview: |
    Copa Airlines won't fly a dog or cat into its Panama hub on a Friday, Saturday or Sunday. That single scheduling rule, built around a transit vet who only works weekdays, shapes this route more than any single piece of Paraguayan paperwork does, and it catches out owners who book a weekend departure without checking cargo rules first.

    Once the calendar is sorted, the regulatory side is comparatively light. CFIA's export process is a single vet visit and an endorsement appointment; SENACSA doesn't ask for a titre test on this direction, though it does cap non-commercial imports at five pets and refuses several breeds outright.

  sections:
    - heading: "Copa's weekday rule, and the Sao Paulo alternative"
      body: |
        Copa's Tocumen hub in Panama City is the natural connection point between Canada and Asuncion, since it's the only airport with a direct route into Paraguay from North America. The catch is timing: live animal cargo only moves Monday through Thursday, because the vet responsible for inspecting transiting pets at Tocumen doesn't work weekends. Plan a booking around that constraint from the start rather than discovering it after a flight is already reserved.

        Air Canada's daily nonstop Toronto-Sao Paulo service, connecting to LATAM's Sao Paulo-Asuncion leg, is a genuine alternative when Copa's schedule doesn't line up. It adds a transfer, but it isn't tied to the same weekday restriction.

    - heading: "What CFIA actually requires"
      body: |
        A licensed Canadian vet writes the export certificate. An official CFIA veterinarian then endorses it in person, at a booked appointment, while the pet is still in the country; CFIA has no mechanism to endorse a certificate once the animal has already flown. The fee sits around CAD 20 to 40, and there's no separate export permit to apply for on top of it.

    - heading: "SENACSA's side: lighter than expected, with two hard limits"
      body: |
        Paraguay doesn't publish a mandatory titre test requirement for pets entering under its standard non-commercial route, which keeps this direction lighter than many South American imports. Two limits still apply without exception: no more than five pets per owner before the shipment counts as commercial, and a banned breed list, Pit Bull-type dogs, American Bully, Cane Corso and several mastiffs among them, that refuses entry regardless of paperwork.
