content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Canada to Belarus"
  overview: |
    LOT Polish Airlines flies Toronto to Warsaw nonstop six times a week, a genuinely straightforward transatlantic leg. Nothing about the rest of this move is quite that simple. No carrier flies into Minsk at all, so the Warsaw flight is only the first half of getting a pet home. The second half is roughly 550km of road and a border crossing into a country that still hasn't been told, at least not publicly, whether its own paperwork rules treat a Canadian certificate the same tight way it treats an American one.

    Belarus's vaccine rules add a genuine planning wrinkle before any of that. A dog or cat needs a single-year rabies vaccine, not the three-year version most Canadian vets give by default, plus a wider panel most owners don't expect a rabies-specific rule to also demand.

  sections:
    - heading: "A direct flight that only solves half the journey"
      body: |
        Toronto-Warsaw runs six times a week on LOT's Boeing 787, roughly nine and a half hours, and it's the cleanest booking in this entire process. Air Canada Cargo and Lufthansa Cargo both offer a Frankfurt alternative, useful if a routing through Germany fits the crate size or schedule better.

        Whichever hub is used, the flight itself only reaches an EU airport. Belarus's own airspace restrictions, unrelated to Canada's separate ban on Belarusian aircraft, mean the final roughly 550km into Minsk happens entirely by road, crossing an EU Border Control Post with the translated certificate in hand.

    - heading: "The vaccine Belarus won't accept, and the ones it also wants"
      body: |
        Belarus's single-year-only rabies rule catches out plenty of Canadian owners, since the three-year vaccine is standard practice at most Canadian vet clinics. A pet running on that longer cycle needs a fresh, specifically single-year dose before this move, not a booster check against the existing record.

        Less obvious is that Belarus also expects a dog to be current on distemper, hepatitis, viral enteritis, parvovirus and adenovirus vaccinations, and a cat on panleukopenia, on top of the rabies requirement. It's a fuller vaccine panel than the country's rabies-specific classification alone would suggest, worth confirming in full with a vet rather than assuming rabies coverage satisfies Belarusian officials on its own.

    - heading: "A certificate timeline nobody has published for Canada"
      body: |
        USDA APHIS has stated, in writing, that a US health certificate for Belarus must be endorsed within 5 days of travel. Nothing equivalent appears on CFIA's public pages for a Canadian-issued certificate. That's not the same as no rule existing; it more likely means the same tight window applies but hasn't been spelled out for Canadian exporters the way it has for American ones.

        The safer plan treats the CFIA endorsement appointment, the translation, and the flight booking as one tightly sequenced block rather than three separate errands. Confirm the live requirement directly with Belarus's State Veterinary and Food Supervision Department before locking in dates, since acting on an assumption here carries real risk for a pet that's already mid-journey.

    - heading: "What happens after the border crossing"
      body: |
        Clearing the crossing with valid paperwork isn't the final step. Belarus requires the pet to be registered with local veterinary authorities within one month of arrival, a genuine administrative obligation. Confirm the exact registration process with a local vet shortly after arrival, since it sits entirely outside anything CFIA or the Canadian side of this move covers.
