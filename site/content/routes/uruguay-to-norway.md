  overview: |
    Norway sits outside the EU but mirrors its pet travel rules closely, which means a Uruguayan pet faces the same unlisted-country sequence it would face entering Denmark or Germany: microchip, vaccination, FAVN titre test, then a 90-day wait from the blood draw date. On top of that, Norway adds one requirement that applies to every dog regardless of origin: a praziquantel tapeworm treatment 24 to 120 hours before landing at Oslo Gardermoen.

    That last rule is broader than Finland's or Ireland's version, since Norway does not carve out exemptions for any origin country. Every dog gets treated, every time, close to the travel date.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Uruguay faces the full unlisted-country process for Norway"
      body: |
        Norway is not an EU member, but it applies equivalent standards for pet travel through its EEA relationship with the bloc. Uruguay has no EU rabies-control equivalence, so Mattilsynet requires the same FAVN or ELISA titre test, 0.5 IU/ml minimum result, and 90-day post-draw wait that Denmark, Sweden or Germany would require.

        The clock starts on the blood draw date. A fast laboratory turnaround does not shorten the wait, and there is no exception for owners with a tight moving deadline.
    - heading: "The tapeworm rule: universal, not origin-specific"
      body: |
        Where Ireland exempts a handful of countries and Finland applies the rule only to non-EU arrivals, Norway requires the praziquantel treatment for every dog entering the country, no matter where it is coming from. That includes dogs arriving from other EU or EEA states, not just unlisted third countries like Uruguay.

        The practical upshot for this route: schedule the final vet visit, covering both the export certificate and the tapeworm treatment, for the days immediately before your confirmed flight, since the treatment window closes 24 hours before arrival and opens only 120 hours out.
