content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Japan to Denmark"
  overview: |
    Japan is on the EU approved third-country list under EU Implementing Regulation 2026/636. This removes the two biggest barriers on most Asian-to-EU routes: the FAVN titre test and the 90-day waiting period. Moving a pet from Japan to Denmark is a relatively compact process, with preparation typically taking four to eight weeks. The central step is the Animal Quarantine Service (AQS) examination in Japan and the EU-format health certificate endorsement. Denmark has no quarantine on arrival and no tapeworm requirement.

  sections:
    - heading: "What makes the Japan to Denmark pet move different from other Asian routes?"
      body: |
        Most pets travelling from Asia to Denmark need a FAVN rabies antibody titre test and a 90-day waiting period before entry. Japan is one of a small number of countries exempt from both, as a listed third country under EU Implementing Regulation 2026/636. The same rules that apply when a pet moves from Canada or the United States to Denmark apply from Japan.

        The AQS (Japan's Animal Quarantine Service, under MAFF) manages the export side. Your vet must be AQS-registered, and the final health certificate examination must take place at a designated Animal Quarantine Station at one of Japan's major international airports: Tokyo Narita (NRT), Haneda (HND), or Osaka Kansai (KIX). A regular vet clinic cannot issue this endorsement alone. The health certificate is issued in the EU format and is valid for 10 days.

        On the Danish side, the Danish Veterinary and Food Administration (FVST) inspects pets arriving at Copenhagen Kastrup Airport. For compliant pets from Japan, there is no quarantine period and no tapeworm treatment requirement (unlike Norway and Finland).

    - heading: "Which airlines carry pets from Japan to Denmark?"
      body: |
        No direct flights operate from Japan to Copenhagen. All cargo routes use a European hub connection. Lufthansa Cargo accepts live animals on the Tokyo Narita to Frankfurt leg, with an onward connection to Copenhagen via SAS or Lufthansa partners. KLM Cargo offers the NRT to Amsterdam route, with AMS to CPH connections via KLM or SAS. Finnair is an efficient option via Helsinki, where the connection time to Copenhagen is short.

        Confirm live animal cargo acceptance on every individual segment. The long-haul and short-haul legs are usually operated by different carriers with different live animal policies, and a booking on one does not guarantee acceptance on the other.

    - heading: "What does it cost to move a pet from Japan to Denmark?"
      body: |
        Total costs typically range from EUR 1,200 to EUR 3,500 depending primarily on your pet's size and crate dimensions. The AQS examination and endorsement costs JPY 30,000 to JPY 80,000 (roughly EUR 200 to EUR 530). Cargo from NRT or HND to CPH via a hub runs EUR 800 to EUR 2,400. The Danish border inspection fee is typically EUR 100 to EUR 250. An IATA-compliant crate costs EUR 70 to EUR 280 if you need one.

        An IPATA-registered agent managing the AQS appointment, cargo coordination, and documentation typically adds EUR 300 to EUR 700, which is often worth it for first international moves.

  - question: "What is the AQS and why is it needed for the Japan to Denmark move?"
    answer: "Japan's Animal Quarantine Service (AQS) is the authority that endorses health certificates for pets leaving Japan. The certificate must be issued by an AQS-registered vet at a designated Animal Quarantine Station. This endorsement gives the certificate official standing in Denmark. A standard vet clinic cannot fulfil this role."
  - question: "Is there quarantine when arriving in Denmark from Japan?"
    answer: "No quarantine for compliant pets. FVST inspectors at Copenhagen Airport check the microchip, vaccination records, and AQS-endorsed certificate. If documentation matches the pet and is within validity, your animal clears without quarantine."
  - question: "Does Denmark require tapeworm treatment for dogs from Japan?"
    answer: "No. Denmark does not require Echinococcus tapeworm treatment, unlike Norway and Finland. This makes the Japan to Denmark route simpler than some other Nordic destinations."
