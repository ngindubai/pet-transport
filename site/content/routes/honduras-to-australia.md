content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Honduras to Australia: 2026 Guide"
  overview: |
    An Avianca flight already leaves San Pedro Sula for Montreal several times a week. That single existing service does more for a Honduras-based owner planning this move than any amount of paperwork, because Honduras itself has no direct pathway into Australia at all: it sits outside DAFF's Group 1, 2 and 3 approved-country lists, along with almost every other country in Latin America.

    What that means in practice is a mandatory stopover, not at an airport but in a country. The pet has to live somewhere DAFF recognises, continuously, for 180 days, before an Australian import file can even open. Once that period is served, the rest of the process runs the standard track: an RNATT titre test, a BICON import permit, and quarantine at Mickleham near Melbourne.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Honduras isn't on DAFF's list, and what that actually requires"
      body: |
        DAFF sorts every country of origin into Group 1 (New Zealand and its close territories), Group 2 (a set of rabies-free Pacific and Indian Ocean nations) or Group 3 (rabies-controlled countries such as the UK, the US and most of Western Europe). Honduras isn't in any of them, and neither is the overwhelming majority of Latin America, bar a small number of Caribbean islands.

        The consequence isn't a heavier document list from Honduras itself. It's a residency requirement layered on top: 180 consecutive days living in an approved country, with proof, before Australia will process anything. A transit stop, however long the layover, doesn't count.

    - heading: "Canada, not the US, is the staging country worth pricing first"
      body: |
        Most owners moving from a non-approved country face a genuine logistics puzzle over where to stage the residency period. Honduras-based owners have a shortcut: Avianca already flies San Pedro Sula to Montreal directly, a route this site's own Honduras-to-Canada guide covers in full, and Canada carries DAFF Group 3 status.

        That matters for a reason beyond convenience. A US staging plan brings the CDC's high-risk-country rules into play for dogs, including a restriction to landing at one of six specific CDC-registered facility airports. Canada has no equivalent constraint, so a Honduras-to-Montreal move sidesteps a layer of complexity that a Miami-based staging plan would add.

    - heading: "What happens once the 180 days are up"
      body: |
        Once residency is served, the process converges with DAFF's standard Group 3 pathway: microchip and identity verification ahead of the RNATT blood draw, the titre test itself, a BICON import permit application that DAFF's own figures put at 20 to 123 business days, and finally Mickleham quarantine, 10 days if the identity check happened correctly and in good time, 30 days if it didn't.

        Add the SENASA export step from Honduras and the residency period itself, and 40 to 52 weeks is a realistic floor for the whole move, not an outside estimate.
