  h1: "Pet Transport from Madagascar to the United Kingdom"
  overview: |
    Madagascar sits outside both of Great Britain's listed-country tables. That single fact drives almost everything else on this route: a rabies titre test, a blood sample sent to an approved laboratory, then a fixed three-month wait that starts on the day the needle goes in, not the day the result arrives.

    There's a second complication that has nothing to do with paperwork. No airline flies Antananarivo to London non-stop. Every option connects through a hub: Air France via Paris, Kenya Airways via Nairobi, or Ethiopian Airlines via Addis Ababa. Madagascar's own national carrier, despite the name recognition, has had a patchy international schedule in recent years and shouldn't be assumed as a working option without checking first.

    Allow five to six months from a standing start. Some of that time is fixed by regulation and cannot be compressed, however early the flight gets booked.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Madagascar's unlisted status controls the calendar"
      body: |
        Great Britain sorts every country of origin into one of three groups: Part 1, Part 2, or unlisted. Madagascar falls into the third. That means a pet needs the full unlisted-country package: microchip before vaccination, the vaccination itself, a blood draw at least 30 days later, and then a flat three-month wait once the lab confirms a result of 0.5 IU/ml or higher.

        Work backwards from a travel date. The earliest a titre-eligible pet can fly is roughly four months after vaccination, once the 30-day pre-draw gap and the three-month post-draw wait are both accounted for. Add the time DSV needs to endorse the export certificate and the time it takes to book a connecting flight, and five to six months is a realistic minimum, not a worst case.
    - heading: "Getting a pet from Antananarivo to a hub, then to Heathrow"
      body: |
        Three routings do most of the work on this pairing. Air France flies Antananarivo to Paris Charles de Gaulle several times a week and accepts small pets in the cabin alongside cargo for larger animals, useful if a dog or cat is under the cabin weight limit. Kenya Airways runs Antananarivo to Nairobi almost daily and connects onward to Heathrow. Ethiopian Airlines covers Antananarivo to Addis Ababa on a similar frequency, with its own Heathrow service from there.

        None of these is a single-carrier, single-booking journey. A pet transport agent who has actually handled a Madagascar departure is worth the fee, since coordinating a live animal handover between two carriers at an unfamiliar hub isn't something to leave to a standard travel booking.
