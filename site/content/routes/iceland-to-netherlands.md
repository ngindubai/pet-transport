content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Iceland to the Netherlands"
  overview: |
    Twelve times a week or so, a plane leaves Keflavik for Schiphol, and unlike several other Iceland routes on this site, there is genuinely no titre test hiding in the paperwork here. The Netherlands treats Iceland the same way it treats an EU member state for pet travel, so a microchip, a current rabies vaccination and a MAST-endorsed certificate cover the whole regulatory side.

    The one thing worth checking twice is which airline you book. Icelandair and Transavia both fly this sector nonstop, but only one of them has an established live animal cargo process since Icelandair stopped taking pets as checked baggage in November 2024.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why there's no titre test on this leg"
      body: |
        Iceland sits inside the same Annex II grouping as Norway, Switzerland and Liechtenstein, territories the EU treats as equivalent to a member state for pet movement. That status runs both ways. A pet moving from the Netherlands to Iceland faces MAST's own Category 1 rules and a 14-day quarantine on arrival, covered on the reverse route page. Moving the other direction, into the Netherlands, a pet clears entry on the same terms as a Dutch dog coming home from a Spanish holiday.

        No blood draw, no 90-day or 180-day wait tacked on. Just the microchip, the 21-day vaccination wait, and a certificate signed and endorsed close to the flight date.

    - heading: "Two nonstop carriers, one cargo-ready"
      body: |
        Icelandair's Keflavik-Amsterdam service is well established, and since late 2024 every pet on it travels as manifested cargo through Icelandair Cargo rather than as checked baggage at the passenger desk. Transavia operates the same nonstop city pair, largely for leisure and business passengers rather than freight, so its live animal capability on this specific route needs confirming directly rather than assumed.

        For most owners, that makes Icelandair Cargo the more straightforward booking, if not necessarily the cheaper one once cargo fees are compared against a low-cost carrier's own hold policy.

    - heading: "One quirk worth flagging: the breed rules differ from Germany's"
      body: |
        Unlike Germany, which bans several bully-type breeds outright at federal level, the Netherlands dropped its own national pit bull ban in 2008 in favour of a behaviour-based approach: a dog that shows aggression can be tested and, if it fails, restricted or removed, regardless of breed. There is no fixed list of banned breeds blocking entry at the Dutch border the way there is on several other routes covered on this site. Individual municipalities can still hold their own local policies, so it is worth checking the specific town you are moving to rather than assuming a blanket national answer either way.

No titre test applies."
