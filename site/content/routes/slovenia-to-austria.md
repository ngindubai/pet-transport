  sections:
    - heading: "Drive or fly: both are practical for this route"
      body: |
        Slovenia and Austria share a border. The most direct road route from Ljubljana to Vienna runs via the A2 motorway through Graz, covers approximately 375km, and takes around 3.5 hours. This is a realistic option for most pets, particularly dogs that travel well in a car or cats in a carrier.

        For those who prefer to fly, Austrian Airlines operates a direct Ljubljana (LJU) to Vienna (VIE) service. The flight takes under 1 hour. Small pets (up to 8kg including carrier) travel in the cabin. Larger dogs go in the hold as checked baggage. Austrian Airlines is part of the Lufthansa Group and has a clear, well-documented pet policy.

        Ljubljana Airport (LJU) is a relatively small facility. Cargo options from LJU are limited compared to a major hub. For large dogs that need to travel by cargo rather than as checked baggage, it may be more practical to use Vienna as a cargo hub, even for this short journey.

    - heading: "Documentation: what UVHVVR-authorised vets issue"
      body: |
        Slovenia's UVHVVR (Administration for Food Safety, Veterinary Sector and Plant Protection) authorises vets to issue EU pet passports. Any authorised Slovenian vet can record your pet's microchip and vaccination history in an EU pet passport. This is the simplest long-term solution for a pet that may travel again.

        For a one-way move, an AHC issued by an official Slovenian vet is an acceptable alternative. The AHC must be endorsed within 10 days of your arrival in Austria. Since this is a short journey, timing this is straightforward.

        Austria requires the microchip to have been in place before or on the date of the first rabies vaccination. If your pet's records show the microchip was placed after the first vaccine, consult an official vet before travelling about how to document this correctly.

    - heading: "Austrian state rules after arrival"
      body: |
        Austria has no federal breed ban. However, the nine Bundeslaender each set their own animal welfare and dog-keeping rules. Vienna is the most regulated: owners of certain breeds (including Rottweilers and several bully-type dogs) must hold a Sachkundenachweis (competency certificate) and have their dog assessed. Styria, Carinthia, and other states have their own, sometimes different, requirements.

        For most dogs and all cats, these state rules will not affect the move itself. They apply to keeping the animal in Austria, not to entry. Check the rules for your specific destination state via the BMSGPK website (sozialministerium.at) or the state government site.
