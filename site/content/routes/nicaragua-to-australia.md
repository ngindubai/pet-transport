content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Nicaragua to Australia"
  overview: |
    Managua has no direct flight to Melbourne, and that turns out to be the smaller problem. Nicaragua doesn't appear on DAFF's approved country list at all, so no paperwork completed at home gets a pet on a plane to Australia. The fix isn't a longer form. It's a second country, and 180 days of living there, before Australia will even open the file.

    The practical version of that fix starts with a flight Nicaragua already has: American Airlines' daily non-stop from Managua to Miami, a route that carries none of the CDC's own titre-test burden since Nicaragua isn't on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies. Once the pet is in the US, the 180-day residency clock for a non-approved origin runs alongside the same titre test and quarantine process a US-origin pet already completes on its own well-worn route into Australia.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Nicaragua can't fly direct, whatever the paperwork says"
      body: |
        DAFF divides the world into approved and non-approved countries for pet import. Group 1 covers New Zealand and a handful of Pacific territories. Group 2 and Group 3 cover a longer list, including the UK, the US, Japan, Singapore and most of Western Europe, each with its own titre-test and quarantine terms. Nicaragua sits outside all three groups.

        That classification has nothing to do with an individual animal's health. A perfectly vaccinated, microchipped, titre-tested dog in Managua still cannot board a flight straight to Melbourne, because DAFF's rule is about the country of origin, not the pet.

    - heading: "The 180-day residency clock, and why the US is the practical choice"
      body: |
        A pet from a non-approved country becomes eligible for Australian import only after living continuously for 180 days in an approved country immediately before departure, and meeting that country's own requirements while resident there. DAFF counts the clock from the date the pet actually arrives in the approved country, not from a vaccination date or a titre draw.

        Nicaragua already has a same-day option for the first leg of that move: American's daily non-stop into Miami, a route that's fast precisely because Nicaragua isn't on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies, so no titre test slows down the US entry itself. From there, the US is a Group 3-equivalent approved country for DAFF purposes, and it runs its own established, if lengthy, process into Australia. Living out the 180-day clock there means the residency requirement and the titre-test wait can run largely in parallel rather than as two separate blocks of time.

    - heading: "What happens once the transit country prep is done"
      body: |
        Once resident in the approved country, the process mirrors what a pet born and raised there would face: two rabies vaccinations at least 30 days apart, an RNATT or FAVN titre test at a DAFF-approved laboratory showing at least 0.5 IU/ml, and a DAFF import permit applied for through BICON.

        The one difference worth flagging plainly: DAFF reviews non-approved-origin applications case by case rather than against the routine published timetable. Budget more time than the standard 20 to 30 working days quoted for a straightforward approved-country permit, and expect the possibility of additional questions from DAFF about the animal's history in Nicaragua before it relocated.

        On arrival in Australia, every pet, regardless of origin, completes 10 days at the Mickleham Post Entry Quarantine Facility near Melbourne. There is no alternative facility and no exemption for this route.

    - heading: "Costs and the airlines that actually fly this route"
      body: |
        The Managua-to-Miami leg is genuinely cheap by comparison with the rest of the journey, usually a few hundred to just over a thousand US dollars on American Airlines. The long-haul sector from the US to Melbourne runs considerably higher, and Qantas or Delta are the carriers to use for it.

        One booking to avoid: United Airlines has suspended its PetSafe cargo programme for the general public on US-to-Australia routes, a restriction that applies regardless of where the pet started its journey. All told, a realistic all-in budget for this route sits between USD 7,000 and USD 14,000, before factoring in six months of living costs in the transit country.
