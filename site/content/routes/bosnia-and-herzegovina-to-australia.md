  h1: "Pet Transport from Bosnia and Herzegovina to Australia"
  overview: |
    Two separate obstacles sit on this route, and neither is optional. DAFF classifies Bosnia and Herzegovina as non-approved, so 180 consecutive days of residency in an approved country come before any Australian paperwork opens. The UK is the sensible choice, since it's already established as the staging option on this site.

    Getting there is its own problem. Wizz Air and Ryanair fly Sarajevo to London nonstop, and neither carries a pet beyond a certified assistance dog. The connecting-carrier workaround this site already documents on Bosnia and Herzegovina's UK route has to run first, before the 180-day clock for Australia can even begin.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why non-approved status means a country change, not just a wait"
      body: |
        DAFF sorts every country into Group 1, 2, 3, or non-approved, and Bosnia and Herzegovina sits in the last category, along with most of the Balkans. That's a materially different starting point from an approved country, where a pet can be prepared at home and exported directly. Here, the pet has to actually live somewhere else, continuously, for 180 days, before DAFF opens a file at all.

        The UK, DAFF Group 3 approved, is the natural pick. It isn't picked at random, though: it's the same country this site's Bosnia and Herzegovina-to-UK route already uses, so the flight logistics and the destination overlap rather than doubling up.

    - heading: "The flight problem comes first, and it's the same one twice"
      body: |
        Sarajevo has a direct flight to London on two carriers, and both are genuinely useless for a pet. Wizz Air's policy excludes all animals except certified assistance dogs, in any class. Ryanair's position is identical.

        That forces a connecting itinerary through Istanbul on Turkish Airlines or Vienna on Austrian Airlines, exactly the workaround this site's Bosnia and Herzegovina-to-UK page already covers. For the Australia route, that connection isn't a detour on top of the main plan. It's the only way to reach the country where the 180-day residency requirement even starts running.

    - heading: "What happens once the residency clock is ticking"
      body: |
        A rabies course started in Bosnia and Herzegovina doesn't count once resident in the UK; DAFF only recognises vaccinations given inside an approved country. Restart the two-dose course locally, draw the RNATT blood sample early in the 180 days, and the wait for the titre result overlaps with the residency requirement rather than extending it.

        Qantas covers the final Heathrow to Melbourne leg once both clocks clear. Mickleham's weekday-only arrival rule and Melbourne's status as the sole Australian entry port for pets both apply here exactly as they do on every other non-approved-country route this site covers.
