content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Macau to France"
  overview: |
    Hong Kong sits on the EU's list of territories exempt from the rabies antibody titre test. Macau, reachable from Hong Kong by a roughly hour-long bridge crossing or ferry, does not appear on that list at all, in either of its two parts.

    That single gap in the paperwork is the whole story of this route. A Macau-origin pet faces the same titre test and three-month wait as one leaving a country with no rabies-exemption status whatsoever, plus a mandatory Hong Kong transfer before it ever reaches a France-bound cargo hold.

  sections:
    - heading: "The EU status gap between Macau and Hong Kong"
      body: |
        The relevant EU country list, run since 22 April 2026 under Delegated Regulation (EU) 2026/131 and its accompanying implementing rules, names the territories and countries exempt from the rabies titre test. Hong Kong is one of them. Macau, despite sharing a land border crossing and a rabies risk profile with its neighbour across the Pearl River Delta, simply isn't named.

        That's not a drafting oversight to second-guess; it's the current legal position, and it means the paperwork path from Macau runs the long way round. No shortcut, no benefit of the doubt at the border.

    - heading: "What the unlisted-country pathway actually requires"
      body: |
        Blood drawn at least 30 days after the rabies vaccination, tested at an EU-approved laboratory, needs to come back at 0.5 IU/mL or above. From the date of that blood draw, not the vaccination date, a three-month wait runs before the pet can enter France. Get the maths wrong on which date starts the clock and the whole schedule slips by weeks.

        A microchip has to go in before the vaccination, not after, and the vaccination itself only counts as valid from 21 days after it's given. Line these up in order. IAM-recognised vets in Macau handle this routinely for other destinations, so ask specifically for the EU non-commercial movement format when booking the appointment.

    - heading: "Where the pet is allowed to land"
      body: |
        France doesn't accept unlisted-country pets at just any international airport. Entry has to go through a designated Traveller's Point of Entry, one with on-site SIVEP veterinary staff able to check the microchip, the health certificate and the vaccination record against the animal itself. Paris Charles de Gaulle handles the bulk of this traffic, with Orly, Lyon-Saint-Exupéry, Nice and Marseille-Provence also approved.

        Book the cargo routing with that in mind. A connection that technically reaches France but lands somewhere without a designated checkpoint isn't a workable plan for this route.

    - heading: "One requirement that genuinely doesn't apply"
      body: |
        Here's a detail worth getting right rather than over-applying out of caution: dogs entering mainland France don't need the Echinococcus multilocularis tapeworm treatment. That rule exists for a specific group of countries, Finland, Ireland, Malta, Norway and Northern Ireland among them, that maintain a tapeworm-free status France itself doesn't claim.

        If the same pet is continuing on to one of those countries after France, the treatment becomes relevant again, timed 24 to 120 hours before that onward crossing. For France as the final stop, skip it. Vets who default to "always treat for tapeworm" on EU-bound pets are applying a rule that, here, doesn't belong.
