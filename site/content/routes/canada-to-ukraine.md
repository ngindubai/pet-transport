  h1: "Pet Transport from Canada to Ukraine"
  overview: |
    LOT Polish Airlines flies nonstop from Toronto Pearson to Warsaw six times a week on a Boeing 787, a genuinely easy booking by the standards of this route. Everything after landing in Warsaw is where the real planning happens, since the destination is a country with no working commercial airport at all.

    Two separate authorities govern this move: the CFIA on the way out, Ukraine's own state veterinary service on the way in. The flight is the least complicated part of the whole thing.

  sections:
    - heading: "The Canadian side is genuinely straightforward"
      body: |
        There's no general export permit to apply for. A licensed Canadian vet completes a standard international certificate, an official CFIA veterinarian endorses it at a booked appointment, and that's the Canadian half of the paperwork done. Budget CAD 20-40 for the CFIA endorsement itself, on top of whatever the vet charges for the exam and certificate.

        The one CFIA quirk worth knowing: Ukraine doesn't have a bilateral certificate template the way EU member states do, so the vet fills in a general international form rather than a country-specific one. It still needs to carry everything Ukraine's own rules ask for.

    - heading: "Toronto to Warsaw is easy. Warsaw to Ukraine isn't."
      body: |
        LOT's nonstop service between Toronto Pearson and Warsaw Chopin runs six times weekly on a Boeing 787, taking around eight and a half hours. Air Canada Cargo and Lufthansa Cargo both offer Frankfurt-routed alternatives for owners starting from other Canadian cities.

        Ukrainian civilian airspace has been closed to commercial flights since 24 February 2022 and remains closed in mid-2026. So the pet clears Polish customs in Warsaw, then travels by road toward one of the working border crossings, most commonly Shehyni-Medyka or Krakovets-Korczowa near Lviv, or a Romanian crossing further south. There is no shortcut around this leg.

    - heading: "What Ukraine checks at the border"
      body: |
        Microchip before vaccination, a rabies vaccine given between 21 days and 12 months before entry, and a titre result no younger than 3 months and no older than 24 at the point of crossing. On top of that, dogs need a negative Toxoplasmosis test and cats a Dermatophytosis test, tests that have no equivalent in the Canadian export process and are easy to miss if the vet visit isn't planned around Ukraine's specific rules rather than a generic export checklist.

        None of this is negotiable at the border. A missing test result means the pet doesn't cross that day, whatever the flight itinerary said.

    - heading: "Realistic costs for this route"
      body: |
        Expect somewhere between CAD 2,000 and CAD 4,500 in total, factoring in the Canadian vet work, the CFIA endorsement, the extra Ukraine-specific tests, a crate, the Toronto-Warsaw cargo fare and the overland transfer once in Poland. The overland leg is the least predictable line item, since it depends on which crossing is operating and how much a local transport agent charges for that stretch.

        Get the paperwork right and there's no quarantine on either end. Get one test wrong and the timeline stretches, sometimes by weeks, while it's corrected.
