content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from the United Kingdom to Belarus"
  overview: |
    There's no rabies titre test on this route, and no 3-month wait either. Belarus treats the UK as a normal-risk country of origin, so the paperwork looks lighter than most non-EU destinations on the surface.

    The catch sits in two places most owners don't expect. Belarus won't recognise a multi-year rabies vaccine, the kind most UK vets administer by default, so many pets need a fresh single-year dose before the clock even starts. And once the flight lands in Warsaw or Vilnius, the pet still has a road journey and a Belarusian veterinary registration to complete within a month, not just a border check to clear.

  sections:
    - heading: "No titre test, but a vaccine rule that catches people out"
      body: |
        Belarus's animal-health authority, the State Veterinary and Food Supervision Department, doesn't classify the UK as high-risk for rabies, so the antibody titre test that trips up unlisted-country routes elsewhere simply doesn't apply here. A rabies vaccination given at least 30 days before entry, alongside the microchip and the translated certificate, is enough.

        Where it gets specific is the vaccine itself. Belarus accepts only single-year formulations. A pet running on a three-year UK vaccine, which is standard practice for most adult dogs and cats, doesn't have a valid record by Belarusian standards and needs a fresh single-year dose administered on purpose for this move.

    - heading: "Getting into Belarus without a direct flight"
      body: |
        Belavia's exclusion from EU and UK airspace since May 2021, tightened again in December 2025, means no scheduled service touches Minsk from Britain. British Airways and LOT both fly Heathrow to Warsaw several times a day; airBaltic's Riga hub offers a Vilnius-adjacent alternative. Either way, the flight ends outside Belarus, and the pet crosses the final border by road.

        Build the translation into the timeline here, not after the fact. Belarusian officials expect the Export Health Certificate in Russian or Belarusian, and a certificate arriving at the border only in English risks delay at the crossing.

    - heading: "What happens after landing"
      body: |
        Clearing the border with valid documentation isn't the final step. Belarus requires the pet to be registered with local veterinary authorities within one month of arrival, a genuine administrative obligation rather than a suggestion. Owners settling in Minsk or elsewhere should confirm the exact registration process with a local vet shortly after arrival, since it sits outside anything APHA or the UK side of the move covers.
