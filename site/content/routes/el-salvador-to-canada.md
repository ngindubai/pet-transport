content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from El Salvador to Canada"
  overview: |
    El Salvador sits on Canada's list of countries classified high-risk for dog rabies, a fact that sounds more alarming than it turns out to be for most owners. The restriction that followed, in September 2022, bans commercial dog imports from these countries: dogs for resale, breeding, fostering, show or research. A dog that is genuinely someone's pet, travelling to live with that same owner, isn't covered by the ban at all.

    What a personal pet dog does need is a CFIA import permit, a minimum age of 7 months on arrival, a microchip implanted before or on the day of vaccination, and entry through one of a handful of designated airports with an animal inspection facility. Notably absent from that list: a titre test. Unlike the US CDC pathway or the UAE's MOCCAE process, CFIA doesn't ask for one on this route.

  sections:
    - heading: "The high-risk classification, and who it actually restricts"
      body: |
        Canada's 2022 measure targeted commercial dog imports specifically, not personal pets. A commercial dog, under CFIA's definition, includes animals meant for resale, adoption, fostering, breeding, show, exhibition or research. A personal pet, by contrast, is one that lives with its owner and won't be handed to anyone else once it lands. That distinction is the whole story here: a family dog moving house with its owner from El Salvador is not the kind of import this rule was built to stop.

        What does apply is a stricter set of conditions than a low-risk-country dog would face: minimum age 7 months at arrival, the microchip implanted no later than the day of vaccination, and a CFIA import permit obtained in advance.

    - heading: "No titre test, but a fixed airport list"
      body: |
        Compare this route with El Salvador to the US or the UAE and one thing stands out: CFIA does not require a rabies titre test for a personal pet dog from a high-risk country. That alone makes the paperwork lighter than either of those routes. What CFIA does insist on is the airport of arrival. Only a handful of Canadian airports have the animal inspection facility needed to process a dog from a high-risk country: Toronto Pearson, Montreal-Trudeau, Vancouver, Calgary and Halifax Stanfield. Land somewhere else and the dog simply cannot be processed there.

        Cats face none of this. CFIA's high-risk measures address the canine rabies variant specifically, so a cat travels on the ordinary, lighter process regardless of El Salvador's classification.

    - heading: "Where the flight and the airport rule line up"
      body: |
        Avianca's nonstop service out of San Salvador happens to land at two of CFIA's five designated airports: Toronto Pearson and Montreal-Trudeau. That's a genuine convenience on this route, since owners heading to either city can book a direct flight that also satisfies the entry-point requirement, rather than routing through a US hub purely to reach an approved Canadian airport. Vancouver, Calgary or Halifax still need a connection, since no nonstop SAL service reaches them.
