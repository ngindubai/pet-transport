content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Nicaragua to Canada"
  overview: |
    Nicaragua is absent from the Canadian Food Inspection Agency's list of countries classed as high-risk for dog rabies, a list last reviewed on 13 March 2025. That single omission matters more than it looks: it removes the titre test, the 7-month minimum age rule, and the mandatory ISO microchip requirement CFIA attaches to a dog from a country that does appear on the list.

    What's left is comparatively light: a current rabies vaccination, IPSA's export paperwork on the Nicaraguan side, and a flight with at least one stop, since nothing connects Managua to Toronto or Montreal non-stop.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why the high-risk list matters more than the flight"
      body: |
        CFIA splits dog imports into two tracks. A dog from a high-risk country needs a 15-digit ISO microchip fitted before vaccination, must be at least 7 months old at entry, and (for commercial dogs specifically) is banned outright under the measure CFIA introduced on 28 September 2022. Nicaragua sits outside that list entirely, based on CFIA's own published table.

        A personal pet dog or cat from Nicaragua only needs a rabies vaccination certificate, in English or French, from a licensed vet, identifying the animal by breed, colour, weight and microchip if fitted. No titre test, no fixed minimum age beyond the vaccine's own requirements, and no import permit, since permits apply to commercial shipments, not a relocating owner's pet.

    - heading: "IPSA's side of the paperwork"
      body: |
        IPSA's general requirements for a dog or cat leaving Nicaragua are short: a health exam within 2 weeks of departure, a rabies vaccination no more than 365 days old, and parasite treatment with an approved product. There's no separately published pet-specific export fee schedule, so a direct call to IPSA's Ventanilla Unica desk is worth making before booking, particularly to confirm which vets currently hold endorsement authority for this route.

        A microchip isn't legally required by either country for this direction, but fitting one is inexpensive and removes any ambiguity about which certificate belongs to which animal, especially useful if you're travelling with more than one pet.

    - heading: "Three ways to reach Toronto or Montreal"
      body: |
        American Airlines' daily non-stop from Managua to Miami, paired with an onward US domestic or connecting flight to Toronto or Montreal, is the most established option. United's Houston gateway offers a genuine alternative for a different Canadian departure city.

        Copa Airlines, via Panama City, avoids US transit but only accepts pet cargo Monday to Thursday, so a weekend arrival plan won't work on this routing. Avianca also carries a meaningful share of Managua-Toronto bookings, usually as a connecting rather than operating carrier on the Canadian leg, so confirm the specific interline arrangement when booking.

    - heading: "Cats face an even lighter path"
      body: |
        Everything above centres on dogs, since CFIA's high-risk classification system is dog-specific. Cats entering Canada face a simpler standard regardless of origin: no country-risk list, no import permit, and, as of 2026, no additional requirements beyond a valid rabies certificate for cats 3 months or older. A cat under 3 months faces no restrictions at all.

        That doesn't mean skipping the paperwork. IPSA's export-side requirements on the Nicaraguan end apply the same way to cats as to dogs, so the certificate, exam and parasite treatment are still needed before departure.
