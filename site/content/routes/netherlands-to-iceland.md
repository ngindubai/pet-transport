content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from the Netherlands to Iceland"
  overview: |
    A fully vaccinated Dutch dog, complete with an EU pet passport, still spends a minimum of 14 days in isolation the moment it lands at Keflavik. That single fact catches out more owners on this route than the paperwork itself does. The Netherlands sits on MAST's Category 1 list, which spares the pet a further 90-day wait that Category 2 countries face, but it changes nothing about the quarantine.

    Two things need budgeting from day one: the pre-departure process, and the two weeks your pet spends at an approved isolation station after arrival, before you can bring it home.

  sections:
    - heading: "What Category 1 status buys, and what it does not"
      body: |
        The Netherlands' place on MAST's Category 1 list works entirely on the pre-export side. The titre test blood draw needs to happen at least 30 days after vaccination, the same as most EU routes, and once the result clears at 0.5 IU/ml or above, there is no further fixed wait tacked on. A Category 2 country, such as the United States or Canada, faces an additional 90-day wait from that same blood draw.

        Category 1 status has no bearing on what happens at the Icelandic border. Every dog and cat entering Iceland, EU-passported or not, spends a minimum of 14 days at Einangrunarstodin i Reykjanesbae or Einangrunarstodin Mosel. Compare that with the reverse route on this site, Iceland to the Netherlands, which needs no quarantine at all. The asymmetry is real, and it is Iceland's own biosecurity rule, not a reflection on how clean the Dutch paperwork is.

    - heading: "Two nonstop flights, only one built for cargo"
      body: |
        Icelandair and Transavia both fly Amsterdam Schiphol to Keflavik direct. Until late 2024, Icelandair let owners check a pet in as excess baggage at the airport. That option closed on 1 November 2024, and a pet now moves through Icelandair Cargo as manifested freight instead, booked through a cargo agent rather than the passenger desk.

        Transavia's presence on the same city pair does not automatically mean it offers an equivalent service for live animals. Confirm directly with Transavia before assuming it is a straightforward alternative, since low-cost carriers do not all run the same cargo infrastructure a legacy carrier like Icelandair maintains.

    - heading: "MAST's own form, not the standard EU one"
      body: |
        Most EU-to-EU pet moves rely on the standard EU Animal Health Certificate. MAST does not accept it for entries into Iceland. Instead, the Dutch vet needs MAST's specific Category 1 form, D1 for a dog or C1 for a cat, endorsed by the NVWA rather than filed on the usual AHC template. A vet unfamiliar with Iceland-bound moves can easily default to the wrong form, so flag this at the first appointment rather than assuming a standard EU certificate will be accepted at Keflavik.

faqs:
  - question: "Does my Dutch dog really have to quarantine in Iceland if it has an EU pet passport?"
    answer: "Yes. Iceland does not exempt EU or EEA pets from its 14-day quarantine. The rule protects Iceland's rabies-free, largely disease-free island status and applies regardless of how complete the paperwork is. The Netherlands' Category 1 status shortens the pre-export titre test timeline, not the quarantine itself."
  - question: "How much does Iceland's mandatory quarantine cost?"
    answer: "Roughly ISK 220,000 for a dog's 14-day stay (about EUR 1,450), and around ISK 100,000-110,000 for a cat (roughly EUR 670-730), at either of Iceland's two approved isolation stations. This is on top of the MAST import permit fee, which is around ISK 40,000."
  - question: "Can I fly my pet with Transavia instead of Icelandair on this route?"
    answer: "Confirm it directly before assuming so. Transavia flies the same nonstop Amsterdam to Keflavik sector, but it does not have the same established live animal cargo process Icelandair Cargo runs since Icelandair stopped taking checked baggage pets in November 2024. Do not book on the assumption the two carriers handle animals identically."
  - question: "Is the titre test different for the Netherlands than for a non-EU country moving to Iceland?"
    answer: "Yes, in one respect. The Netherlands is on MAST's Category 1 list, so once the titre test clears at 0.5 IU/ml or above, there is no extra fixed wait before travel. A Category 2 country faces a further 90-day wait from the blood draw date. The 14-day quarantine on arrival applies either way."
  - question: "Does MAST accept a standard EU pet passport for this route?"
    answer: "No. MAST requires its own Category 1 certificate, Form D1 for dogs or Form C1 for cats, completed by the Dutch vet and endorsed by the NVWA, rather than the standard EU Animal Health Certificate used for most other EU-to-EU moves."
