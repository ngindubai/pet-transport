content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Russia to Canada"
  overview: |
    Five airports. That's the entire list of places in Canada equipped to receive a dog from a country CFIA rates high-risk for rabies: Toronto, Montreal, Vancouver, Calgary and Halifax. Russia sits on that high-risk list, so a Russian-origin dog cannot simply land wherever the cheapest flight happens to go.

    Layer that onto the routing problem. There has been no direct flight between Russia and Canada since Transport Canada closed Canadian airspace to Russian aircraft in the last days of February 2022. Every move goes through Istanbul or, more recently, Dubai, and whichever hub is chosen, the final leg still has to land at one of those five approved airports.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why Russia counts as high risk, and what that requires"
      body: |
        CFIA's high-risk classification reflects Russia's documented wildlife rabies reservoir, not the state of diplomatic relations between the two countries. For a dog, that means the full high-risk pathway: a 15-digit ISO microchip before vaccination, an RNATT titre test at 0.5 IU/ml or above with blood drawn at least 30 days after vaccination, a CFIA import permit obtained in advance, and a minimum age of 7 months on arrival, a full month older than the floor that applies from most other countries.

        Commercial dog imports from high-risk countries, Russia included, have been banned outright by CBSA since 2022. That ban doesn't touch a genuine personal pet move, which is what this pathway covers, but it does mean there's no shortcut through a commercial import channel if the personal-move paperwork isn't in order.

    - heading: "The five-airport rule, and why it decides the flight before the flight decides itself"
      body: |
        A dog arriving under the high-risk pathway can only land at Toronto Pearson, Montreal Trudeau, Vancouver International, Calgary International or Halifax Stanfield, the only Canadian airports with the required animal inspection facility. That has to be confirmed before the flight is booked, not after.

        Turkish Airlines' Istanbul hub already connects to Toronto at full schedule, which makes it the established default. Since Aeroflot resumed direct Moscow-Dubai flights on 1 June 2026 and FlyDubai increased its own frequency through the summer, a Dubai-Toronto routing via Emirates has become a workable second option too, useful if Istanbul capacity or timing doesn't fit.

    - heading: "Getting out of Russia while the CFIA clock runs separately"
      body: |
        On the Russian side, the process mirrors what applies on comparable routes: Veterinary Certificate Form No. 1, applied for through eCert within 5 days of travel, exchanged at the airport for International Veterinary Certificate Form No. 5a on departure day. None of that touches CFIA's titre test and import permit requirements, which run on their own timeline, starting with the vaccination and blood draw weeks earlier.

        Get the CFIA side moving first. A missed titre test window or a permit still in process is a harder problem to fix at short notice than the Russian export paperwork, which can be arranged close to a confirmed departure date once everything else is settled.

    - heading: "If it's a cat, not a dog, most of this doesn't apply"
      body: |
        CFIA's high-risk country rules, the titre test and the 7-month age floor included, are written for dogs. A cat travelling from Russia faces a considerably lighter Canadian entry process: a health certificate and proof of rabies vaccination cover most of what CFIA asks for, with no equivalent high-risk pathway attached to the species. Confirm which animal you're actually moving before assuming the process described above applies, since building a dog's timeline and budget around a cat move wastes months of unnecessary preparation.
