  sections:
    - heading: "Why Brussels is a simpler landing than most European capitals"
      body: |
        Brussels Airlines and RwandAir both fly directly from Kigali to Brussels. That matters more than it sounds, because on almost every other route in this batch, the destination city is a further connecting flight beyond whichever hub the airline uses. Here, the hub and the destination are the same place.

        Ethiopian Airlines Cargo still routes through Addis Ababa before reaching Brussels, and KLM Cargo goes via Nairobi and then Amsterdam with a short final hop, so those two options carry the usual extra transit leg. But if you book Brussels Airlines or RwandAir, your pet's journey from Kigali involves one flight, not two.

        That does not shorten the regulatory timeline. It just means less time in transit and one fewer set of connection risks for the animal itself, which is worth factoring in when comparing quotes across the four carriers, particularly for an older pet or one that finds travel stressful.

    - heading: "The titre test sequence and FASFC's checks on arrival"
      body: |
        Rwanda sits outside the EU's approved list, so Belgium applies the full sequence under EU Regulation 576/2013 and Commission Delegated Regulation 2020/692: microchip first, rabies vaccination second, a 30-day wait, then blood drawn for an FAVN titre test at an EU-approved laboratory. The result must clear 0.5 IU/ml, and the 90-day wait that follows is counted from the blood draw date, not the date the result comes back.

        On arrival, FASFC, the Federal Agency for the Safety of the Food Chain, inspects the pet and its paperwork at the BRU cargo terminal. The health certificate must be in EU format, endorsed by RAB in Rwanda, and no older than 10 days. Compliant pets face no quarantine. Anything incomplete can be held or, in the worst case, refused entry at the owner's expense, so double check the certificate dates and the microchip number before departure.
