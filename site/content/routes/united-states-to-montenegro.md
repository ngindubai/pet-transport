  h1: "Pet Transport from the United States to Montenegro"
  overview: |
    A dog leaving Montenegro for New York skips the rabies titre test entirely. Montenegro is absent from the CDC's high-risk dog rabies list, so the low-risk pathway applies and no blood test is needed. Making the same trip the other way round does not appear to come with the same courtesy.

    Guidance specific to Montenegro's own import rule describes a titre test requirement with no country exemption named anywhere we could find, the United States included. That means a FAVN or RNATT test at least 30 days after vaccination, then a wait of more than three calendar months from a passing result. It is the opposite of the asymmetry already documented on this site's Montenegro-to-US page, and worth knowing before assuming reciprocity where none appears to exist.

    The USDA side of the paperwork is comparatively quick, a health certificate endorsed within a matter of days. The Montenegro side is where the real planning window sits.

  sections:
    - heading: "Why the CDC's leniency doesn't run in both directions"
      body: |
        The CDC's binary system splits the world into high-risk and low-risk countries for dog rabies, and Montenegro sits in the low-risk half. That is genuinely good news for a Montenegro-origin dog headed to the US.

        It says nothing about what Montenegro itself asks of an incoming US pet. Montenegro's own published guidance appears to apply its titre requirement without reference to the CDC's classification, or to the US's own listing on the EU's approved third-country table. A US owner who has read up on the easier reverse trip and assumes the same leniency applies here is the person most likely to be caught out by a three-month wait discovered too late.

        Given how sparse and inconsistent the public sourcing is on Montenegro's exact rule, AFSVPA is the authority to check with directly. Treat the titre requirement as the working assumption for planning purposes regardless.

    - heading: "Getting there: Air Serbia's cabin limit and the alternatives"
      body: |
        Montenegro's own airports have no direct US service. Belgrade is the shortest connection, and Air Serbia's nonstop flight from there to New York JFK, roughly five times a week, is the fastest option on the route. It is cabin only, with an 8kg combined limit for pet and carrier, so it only suits small dogs and cats.

        For anything larger, Turkish Airlines via Istanbul is a genuine option here, since its well-known refusal to carry pets into the UK does not extend to US-bound flights. Lufthansa via Frankfurt or Munich, and Austrian Airlines via Vienna, round out the realistic choices, the latter at a EUR 150 routing surcharge.

        Book at least four to six weeks ahead regardless of carrier. None of these connections are daily nonstops from Montenegro's own airports, so crate handover timing between flights is worth confirming directly with the airline before the titre wait ends.
