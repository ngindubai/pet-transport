  h1: "Pet Transport from Cameroon to Canada"
  overview: |
    Cameroon appears on the Canadian Food Inspection Agency's list of countries classed high-risk for dog rabies, a list last updated 13 March 2025. Read that in isolation and it sounds like trouble. It isn't, not for a family pet. CFIA's September 2022 measure banned commercial dog imports from every high-risk country outright; a personal pet flying with its own owner was explicitly left out of that ban, and the agency has only said it might add rules for personal pets later, not that it has.

    MINEPIA, Cameroon's livestock and animal industries ministry, still runs the export side the same way it does for the UK or US routes: an export permit, a vet inspection, and an endorsement on the health certificate. What changes on this route is the flight. No airline flies Douala or Yaounde to Canada nonstop, so the choice comes down to Royal Air Maroc's Casablanca hub or Ethiopian's Addis Ababa hub, both offering a single-carrier path the whole way through.
  sections:
    - heading: "A high-risk classification that, so far, only bites commercial imports"
      body: |
        CFIA groups countries by rabies risk, and Cameroon sits in the high-risk group. For a rescue organisation or breeder trying to bring dogs into Canada commercially, that classification is now a hard wall: no import permits have been issued for commercial dogs from high-risk countries since 28 September 2022.

        A family pet is a different story. The 2022 notice is specific on this point: personal pet dogs and assistance dogs weren't included in the ban, and CFIA said only that it would "explore options" to strengthen those rules later. Later hasn't arrived yet, as far as any published CFIA requirement goes. That's genuinely useful to know, since it's easy to read "high-risk country" and assume the CDC-style titre-test-and-quarantine machinery that applies to a US-bound dog from Cameroon must apply here too. It doesn't. Canada's system and America's aren't the same, and conflating them adds cost and delay that this route doesn't actually require.

    - heading: "MINEPIA's checklist, and the one thing worth double-checking"
      body: |
        MINEPIA's process for this route looks the same as it does heading to the UK or US: an export permit application, a pre-travel vet exam, and an endorsement on the certificate itself. Apply at least six to eight weeks out, since a physical inspection of the animal is possible before MINEPIA signs off.

        One thing to flag rather than assume: Cameroon's export certificate for the US route needs an original ink endorsement from USDA APHIS, a US administrative quirk, not a Cameroonian one. Canada's CFIA doesn't run an identical wet-ink workflow, and there's no published joint template between MINEPIA and CFIA for this specific corridor. Confirm with your vet or agent exactly what format Canadian officials will accept on arrival, rather than assuming either the US or UK paperwork template transfers automatically.

    - heading: "Casablanca or Addis Ababa: picking the connection"
      body: |
        Royal Air Maroc's Casablanca to Montreal service runs close to daily, on the airline's own Boeing 787s, and connects to its Douala and Yaounde network on the other end. Ethiopian Airlines offers a genuine alternative: Douala to Addis Ababa, then a nonstop Addis Ababa to Toronto sector Ethiopian flies about seven times a week. Air France, via Paris, rounds out the options for anyone whose dates don't suit the other two.

        None of these is a short trip. Factor total transit time, not just the number of stops, into any decision about a brachycephalic breed or an older, less resilient pet.
