content:
  h1: "Pet Transport from Belarus to the United States"
  overview: |
    Two rules collide on this route, and neither bends. The CDC insists a dog from Belarus, one of its listed high-risk countries for dog rabies, lands at the specific US airport hosting its reserved animal care facility. Belarus itself insists no flight leaves the country at all, not for the US, not for the EU, not for anywhere, since the 2021 airspace ban that followed the forced landing of Ryanair Flight 4978.

    Solve both at once and the move works. A dog gets 30 days of paperwork to clear before its blood can even be drawn for the titre test, then a further 28-day wait before it can board. A cat, oddly, sails past almost all of it.

  sections:
    - heading: "Two constraints that both dictate the airport"
      body: |
        Belarus's own problem is geography by decree: Belavia lost EU and UK airspace access within days of the May 2021 Ryanair diversion, a ban tightened further in December 2025. There has been no scheduled flight between Minsk and any Western airport since. Every pet move starts with roughly 550km of road to Warsaw, crossing an EU Border Control Post along the way.

        The CDC's rule works from the other end. A dog whose 6-month travel history includes a high-risk country must arrive at an airport where a CDC-registered animal care facility has a confirmed reservation, with no domestic connection permitted afterward. Get the Warsaw departure booked before that reservation is locked in, and there's a real risk the two don't line up on the same routing.

    - heading: "Why Belarus counts as high-risk, and what that actually buys a dog"
      body: |
        The CDC's high-risk list runs to well over a hundred countries, and Belarus is on it. That triggers the foreign-vaccinated pathway: a Certification of Foreign Rabies Vaccination and Microchip form, endorsed by Belarus's own territorial veterinary office, plus a rabies antibody titre test.

        The test itself isn't punitive on timing, at least compared with some destinations. Blood drawn 30 days after vaccination and a result confirmed 28 days before travel clears a dog for entry without quarantine, a shorter run than the 3-calendar-month wait GB imposes on unlisted-country pets. Skip the titre altogether and the alternative is a 28-day quarantine at a CDC-registered facility, at the owner's full cost. Either way, the reservation at that facility still has to be made in advance, since the exam and revaccination it provides happen regardless of whether the titre passed.

    - heading: "Getting off the ground, twice"
      body: |
        Once the overland leg reaches Warsaw, LOT Polish Airlines flies direct to Chicago O'Hare, JFK, Newark and Miami, all through LOT Cargo rather than a passenger booking. Turkish Airlines via Istanbul is a genuine alternative, particularly where a single agent needs to run both the Belarus exit and the US-side reservation; Istanbul's Pet Lounge handles connecting animals on layovers over 75 minutes.

        On the US side, American PetEmbark coordinates the cargo handover at whichever airport carries the CDC facility booking. None of this is a single-ticket itinerary. It's three separate bookings, road, transatlantic flight and facility reservation, that all have to point at the same dates.

    - heading: "The move a cat makes instead"
      body: |
        Strip a cat out of this and almost the whole article stops applying. No CDC high-risk classification touches cats. No titre test, no facility reservation, no Certification of Foreign Rabies Vaccination form. A cat needs the same Belarusian export certificate any pet needs to leave the country, and then it clears the US border on that alone.

        The overland transfer to Warsaw and the flight booking still apply, since Belarus's airspace ban doesn't discriminate by species. It's the American paperwork that disappears.
