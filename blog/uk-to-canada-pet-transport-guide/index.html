Pet Transport UK to Canada: Rabies Rules, Cargo Costs and 2026 Timelines

By Pet Transport Global Editorial Team  ·  19 June 2026  ·  11 min read

Need help moving your pet internationally? Get a free quote

Canada is one of the more straightforward international destinations for UK pet owners. There is no quarantine, no rabies titre test, no long waiting period, and no complex import permit system for personal pets. The CFIA (Canadian Food Inspection Agency) manages arrivals, and provided the paperwork is in order your pet clears Canadian customs in the same inspection hall as your luggage.

That said, straightforward does not mean without process. The UK Export Health Certificate that dogs and cats need for this route must be signed by an APHA-authorised Official Veterinarian, not an ordinary vet, and it must be issued within 72 hours of departure. The dog-versus-cat distinction in Canadian rules catches people out. And airline cargo bookings on this route involve several steps that are easy to miss if you approach them like a standard flight.

This guide covers what the move actually costs, what CFIA requires for dogs and cats respectively, which airlines carry pet cargo from the UK to Canada, and what the health certificate needs to say. The figures and regulatory details here are current for 2026.

How much does it cost to move a pet from the UK to Canada in 2026?

For a medium-sized dog travelling unaccompanied cargo from London Heathrow to Toronto Pearson or Vancouver, the total all-in cost typically falls between £1,800 and £3,200. Cats and small dogs, because of their smaller crate dimensions, generally come in between £1,200 and £2,200. These figures cover the full chain: airline cargo fee, IATA-compliant crate, Official Veterinarian’s fee, APHA endorsement, and agent coordination where used.

Here is where the money goes:

Airline cargo fee: Air Canada and British Airways World Cargo price live animal cargo by the greater of actual weight or volumetric weight. A 25kg dog in a standard 91cm x 60cm x 67cm crate will be priced on volumetric weight, which typically works out at around 30 to 36kg equivalent. Budget £600 to £1,400 depending on the airline, crate size, and route. Prices to Vancouver tend to be slightly higher than to Toronto because of the additional distance.

IATA-compliant travel crate: £80 to £300 new, depending on size. The crate must meet IATA standards: solid rigid base, ventilation on at least three sides, secure door fittings, and absorbent bedding on the floor. Soft-sided crates are not accepted as unaccompanied cargo. If you buy a crate yourself, check the specific airline’s approved crate requirements, as airlines publish their own lists and not all IATA-compliant crates are accepted by all carriers.

Official Veterinarian (EHC 2923) fee: £150 to £350. The EHC 2923 must be issued by an APHA-authorised Official Veterinarian, not a standard vet. APHA-authorised OVs have specific training for export health certification, and the clinic needs to be registered with APHA. Many specialist pet travel vets in the UK hold this authorisation. The clinical examination must happen within 72 hours of departure.

APHA endorsement fee: £43 to £120 depending on the number of pages. APHA endorsement of the EHC adds one to two working days to the process. Factor this into your timing when the vet appointment is booked.

IPATA agent coordination fee (if used): £400 to £1,000. An IPATA-registered agent handles the cargo booking, the Official Veterinarian appointment, APHA endorsement, and the receiving-end coordination in Canada. For owners who are new to international pet moves or working to a tight timeline, the agent fee saves time and reduces the risk of documentation errors. Some owners with experience of cargo travel manage the process independently; most choose an agent.

Always ask for an itemised quote. A single lump-sum figure with no breakdown makes it difficult to spot where costs are padded or where something is missing. Items to check: whether the crate is included or extra, whether the destination side (CFIA inspection, animal handling at the Canadian airport) is covered, and what happens if the flight is delayed.

What does CFIA actually require for dogs and cats arriving from the UK?

This is where the UK-to-Canada route has an important split that many owners do not expect: Canada’s federal rules treat dogs and cats differently.

For dogs (over 3 months old):

Dogs require a current rabies vaccination certificate. The certificate must be written in English or French, identify the animal by description and permanent identification (microchip or tattoo), state the vaccine product name, give the date of vaccination, and be signed by a licensed veterinarian. Canada does not specify that it must be an APHA-endorsed form, but in practice the UK Export Health Certificate (EHC 2923) covers all of this and satisfies CFIA requirements. Dogs under 3 months old have separate requirements; contact CFIA or your agent directly if you are moving a puppy under that age.

Puppies under 8 months old travelling unaccompanied (without the owner on the same flight) need a health certificate issued within 48 hours of arrival in Canada and must have a CFIA import permit. This is a less common scenario but worth flagging if it applies.

For cats:

Under CFIA federal rules, cats do not require a rabies vaccination to enter Canada. This is a genuine distinction and one worth knowing. A vet health certificate is still recommended because airlines require documentation for any live animal cargo booking, and CFIA inspectors may request it at the border. But the rabies vaccination itself is not a CFIA requirement for cats. Individual provinces can set stricter rules, so it is worth confirming with your destination province if you want complete certainty.

No import permit for personal pets:

Canada does not require an import permit for personal pets (dogs or cats) accompanying their owner. Import permits are required for commercial imports: animals being brought in for sale, re-homing, breeding, or temporary placement through rescue organisations, or shipments of more than two dogs. Personal relocation moves, including moves where the pet travels as unaccompanied cargo on the same booking as the owner’s flight, fall outside the commercial import category.

No quarantine:

Canada does not quarantine dogs or cats from the UK. CFIA inspectors are present at major international airports including Toronto Pearson (YYZ), Vancouver (YVR), Montreal (YUL), Calgary (YYC), Ottawa (YOW), and Halifax (YHZ). They check the health certificate, verify the microchip, and inspect the animal. If the documentation is complete, the inspection is typically done within 30 to 60 minutes.

Declare your pet on the customs declaration form on the aircraft. Failing to declare an animal at a Canadian port of entry is an offence under the Health of Animals Act.

Breed restrictions to check:

There is no federal breed ban for pet imports to Canada. However, Ontario has prohibited Pit Bull Terriers and related types (American Pit Bull Terrier, American Staffordshire Terrier, Staffordshire Bull Terrier) under the Dog Owners Liability Act since 2005. If your destination is Ontario or an Ontario municipality, a dog that falls under this description cannot enter the province. Several Manitoba municipalities, including Winnipeg, have similar bans. Check the specific province and city before arranging transport.

How long does the move take door to door?

The UK-to-Canada timeline is significantly shorter than high-restriction routes such as Australia or New Zealand, where titre tests and quarantine add months to the process.

If your dog or cat’s rabies vaccination is current and your dog is over 3 months old, the preparation timeline can be as short as two to four weeks. The main tasks are:

  1. Confirm the animal’s microchip and vaccination records are complete and legible.
  2. Arrange the cargo booking through an IPATA agent or directly with the airline’s cargo division. Cargo bookings for live animals fill up; four to six weeks advance booking is a reasonable target for popular routes.
  3. Source an IATA-compliant crate of the correct size and let your pet acclimatise to it before travel.
  4. Book the Official Veterinarian (EHC 2923) appointment for within 72 hours of the scheduled departure date. This appointment cannot happen earlier because the certificate’s validity is tied to the examination date.
  5. Allow one to two working days for APHA endorsement after the OV appointment.
  6. The flight itself is 8 to 9 hours direct from London Heathrow to Toronto, and 9 to 10 hours to Vancouver.

If you are starting from a puppy with no vaccination history, you need to factor in a primary rabies vaccination and allow at least the 28-day standard post-vaccination period before travel (this is not a CFIA requirement, but standard veterinary practice for vaccination efficacy). The total timeline in that case is typically six to eight weeks.

Air Canada operates a dedicated animal facility called the Pet Stop at Toronto Pearson International Airport. Pets arriving on Air Canada cargo services are taken to the Pet Stop, which has temperature-controlled enclosures, separate areas for dogs and cats, and staff trained in live animal handling. If your pet’s onward connection within Canada requires an overnight stop at Pearson, the Pet Stop can accommodate it.

Which airlines fly pet cargo from the UK to Canada?

Pet cargo on UK-to-Canada routes is dominated by two airlines. Both require bookings through a registered agent for live animal shipments.

Air Canada: The primary option. Air Canada operates direct cargo-carrying services from London Heathrow to Toronto Pearson (YYZ), Vancouver International (YVR), and Montreal (YUL). The AC Animals programme accepts dogs and cats on these routes. Air Canada has its own list of restricted breeds and brachycephalic (flat-faced) animals; snub-nosed dogs including Bulldogs, Pugs, Boxers, and Boston Terriers, and flat-faced cats including Persians and British Shorthairs, face booking restrictions. Confirm your breed’s status before booking, as the list is reviewed periodically. Air Canada cargo bookings for live animals must be made through an IPATA, IATA, or Animal Transportation Association (ATA) registered agent.

British Airways World Cargo: BA operates direct services from Heathrow to Toronto Pearson and Vancouver via IAG Cargo. Like Air Canada, BA publishes its own list of breed restrictions and does not accept brachycephalic breeds on its cargo services. Cargo bookings are made through authorised agents.

WestJet Cargo: WestJet covers Canadian domestic routes and some transborder services, and can be involved in the onward leg once a pet has arrived in Canada. WestJet is not typically used for the transatlantic portion from the UK.

Connections: Not all Canadian destinations have direct cargo services from the UK. Halifax, Ottawa, Edmonton, and Calgary are generally reached with a connection through Toronto or Vancouver. An IPATA agent can advise on the connection options and whether any additional documentation or handling is required at the transit point.

Seasonal embargoes: Air Canada and British Airways both have temperature restrictions that affect live animal cargo. Both airlines restrict cargo movement when temperatures at the origin or destination airport fall outside a safe range (typically below -12C or above 29C at ground level). For UK-to-Canada routes, the primary risk is Canadian winter temperatures, particularly at prairie airports. This is rarely a problem for Toronto or Vancouver but worth checking if your destination city is inland.

What does the health certificate need to say?

The official document for UK-to-Canada pet exports is EHC 2923 (version 5), published by APHA. It must be completed and signed by an APHA-authorised Official Veterinarian following a clinical examination conducted within 72 hours of the scheduled departure.

The certificate confirms the following details about the animal:

  • Species, breed, sex, date of birth, coat colour, and distinguishing markings
  • Microchip or tattoo number (the method of permanent identification)
  • Name and address of the owner
  • Country of origin and destination
  • Rabies vaccination details: the vaccine product name, batch number, date of vaccination, and the date the current vaccination expires (dogs only; cats are covered by the certificate but the rabies section is not required for cats)
  • A declaration by the Official Veterinarian that the animal was clinically examined and found to be healthy and fit to travel at the time of examination

The “72-hour rule” means the examination date and the departure date must be within three calendar days of each other. If your flight is on a Friday, the earliest the OV can examine your pet is the Wednesday of that week. If the examination happens outside that window, the certificate is invalid for Canada entry and a new one is needed.

After the OV signs the certificate, it goes to APHA for government endorsement. APHA processes these by post and electronically; most applications are endorsed within one to two working days. The endorsed certificate is the document that travels with your pet and that CFIA inspectors check on arrival.

Keep the original certificate with the pet in the cargo documentation envelope. A copy should also be in your own travel documents. CFIA may ask to see it at the passenger terminal even if your pet is travelling as unaccompanied cargo.

To find an APHA-authorised Official Veterinarian in England, Scotland, or Wales, call APHA on 03000 200 301. The APHA register is updated regularly. Not every vet who says they can issue export certificates is actually registered; confirm the OV authorisation directly with APHA if you are unsure.
