Pet Transport UK to New Zealand: 2026 Process for One of the Strictest Borders

By Pet Transport Global Editorial Team  ·  15 June 2026  ·  11 min read

Need help moving your pet internationally? Get a free quote

Moving a dog or cat from the UK to New Zealand is one of the most document-intensive pet relocations in the world. New Zealand applies some of the tightest biosecurity rules on the planet, and the process has real time constraints that you cannot compress. For a medium dog, the total cost runs to approximately £4,800 to £7,200, and the timeline is a minimum of 5 months from the date you start the process.

The core requirement that most people do not anticipate is the RNATT titre test: a blood test measuring your pet’s rabies antibody levels. New Zealand requires the blood draw to take place between 3 months and 24 months before your pet arrives in the country. That 90-day minimum wait is non-negotiable and it drives the whole timeline. Miss the window, and your pet does not travel on the date you planned.

How New Zealand’s pet import rules compare to Australia

If you have looked at the UK-to-Australia process as a comparison point, the New Zealand route is broadly similar in structure but with two meaningful differences.

First, the minimum wait after the RNATT titre test is 90 days for New Zealand, compared to 180 days for Australia. This makes the New Zealand timeline shorter, but only if everything runs smoothly. A failed titre test, documentation error, or permit delay quickly erodes that advantage.

Second, New Zealand’s authority is MPI (Ministry for Primary Industries) rather than Australia’s DAFF (Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry). The permit application, approved laboratory list, and health certificate format are all MPI-specific.

Both countries require 10 days of post-arrival quarantine and have no waivers for this requirement.

The step-by-step process for UK pets entering New Zealand

The sequence is fixed. Steps cannot be done out of order, and the timing constraints on each step affect every step that follows.

  1. Microchip implanted (ISO 11784/11785, 15-digit transponder): must be in place before any vaccination if not already done
  2. Rabies vaccination: primary course completed and recorded by a licensed vet, with batch numbers documented
  3. RNATT titre test: blood drawn at an MPI-approved laboratory in the UK, showing a result of 0.5 IU/ml or above
  4. 90-day wait: from the date of the blood draw, not the date results are received; the blood draw date is what counts
  5. MPI import permit: apply through the MPI Biosecurity Import Permit portal; allow 5 to 15 working days for approval
  6. Tapeworm and parasite treatment: within 14 days before departure and at least 24 hours before departure, using an MPI-approved product
  7. APHA health certificate and endorsement: issued by an APHA-accredited vet within 10 days of departure; APHA endorsement adds 3 to 5 working days if done by post
  8. Airline cargo booking: live animal cargo space confirmed with the airline cargo desk for each flight segment
  9. Quarantine pre-booking: 10-day quarantine slot confirmed at Auckland or Christchurch before travel
  10. Travel and arrival: MPI staff collect your pet at Auckland or Christchurch Airport and transport them to the quarantine facility

The minimum total elapsed time from step 1 to arrival is around 20 to 22 weeks if everything goes right first time. A failed titre test, a slow permit response, or any documentation issue adds weeks.

The RNATT titre test: timing, labs, and what to do if it fails

The RNATT (Rabies Neutralising Antibody Titre Test) measures how well your pet’s immune system has responded to the rabies vaccination. New Zealand requires a result of at least 0.5 IU/ml. Below that threshold, the test has failed and the 90-day clock does not start.

For UK pets, blood must be tested at a laboratory on MPI’s approved list. APHA Weybridge in Surrey is the most commonly used UK lab for this purpose. Your vet draws the blood, packages it to the laboratory’s spec, and sends it off. Results typically take 10 to 15 working days.

The 90-day wait starts on the date the blood was drawn, which is the date your vet collected the sample. Not the date you sent it. Not the date the result came in. The blood draw date.

The maximum time between blood draw and arrival in New Zealand is 24 months. If your pet had a passing titre test more than two years ago, it needs to be repeated.

If the test fails: your vet will typically recommend a rabies booster and a repeat blood draw after an appropriate interval. Each new blood draw that passes starts a new 90-day clock. This is why it is worth starting the titre test process well ahead, even if your travel date is not yet fixed. A passing test result is valid for two years. A test booked now gives you a two-year window to travel if the result comes back above threshold.

The MPI import permit: how to apply and what delays it

The MPI import permit is the formal approval for your specific pet to enter New Zealand. You apply through MPI’s online Biosecurity Import Permit portal. The application requires:

  • Your pet’s full microchip number
  • Species, breed, age, and sex
  • Country of origin (UK)
  • Vaccination history with batch numbers and expiry dates
  • RNATT titre test result (must be in the passing range)
  • Your New Zealand contact address and quarantine facility preference

MPI’s standard processing time is 5 to 15 working days from receipt of a complete application. Applications with documentation gaps or inconsistencies take longer. Submit a complete application the first time.

Common documentation issues that delay MPI permits:

  • Microchip number that differs by one digit between the vet record, the titre test form, and the permit application
  • Vaccination records missing batch numbers (NZ requires batch number for rabies vaccines)
  • Titre test result not on MPI’s approved laboratory list
  • Health certificate format not matching current MPI requirements

Note on the July 2026 IHS transition: MPI updated its Import Health Standard (IHS) for companion animals in mid-2026. If you are applying for a permit after July 2026, verify that you are working from the current IHS document on the MPI website, as some format requirements and approved product lists changed with this update. Applications based on the older IHS may be returned for amendment.

New Zealand quarantine: Auckland or Christchurch, 10 days mandatory

Every dog and cat arriving in New Zealand from the UK spends 10 days in a Ministry for Primary Industries-managed quarantine facility. There are no exemptions, no early releases, and no home isolation alternatives for this process.

Auckland handles the majority of UK arrivals. The quarantine facility is based in Mangere, near Auckland Airport. Christchurch has an MPI-approved facility for pets arriving via Christchurch Airport, which is the better choice if you are moving to the South Island and want to minimise onward travel stress for your pet.

The 10 days involve:

  • An MPI vet examination on arrival and again before release
  • Individual housing (dogs in kennels, cats in cat-specific housing)
  • Regular feeding, water, and basic exercise
  • Temperature monitoring and health observation

You cannot visit your pet during quarantine. MPI staff will provide updates if you call. Sending your pet’s regular food in the travel crate is worth doing to reduce gut disruption from a sudden diet change in an unfamiliar environment.

Quarantine fees in 2026 run approximately NZD 1,800 to NZD 3,200 (roughly £850 to £1,500) for a 10-day stay, depending on the size of the animal. Fees are paid to MPI in advance when you confirm your quarantine booking. Slots can fill up, particularly in summer (December to February in New Zealand) and around the June school holidays. Book the moment your travel date is confirmed.

Which airlines carry pets from the UK to New Zealand?

No airline operates a non-stop route from the UK to New Zealand that also accepts live animal cargo. Every route involves at least one transit, which adds complexity and cost.

Emirates via Dubai is widely used for this corridor. The Dubai transit involves a hold at the Emirates SkyCargo facility, which handles live animals. Singapore Airlines via Singapore is the other frequently used option; Singapore Changi Airport has established live animal transit facilities.

Before booking any of these:

  1. Contact the airline’s cargo department (not the passenger line) to confirm live animal acceptance on your specific flight dates and segments
  2. Confirm that transit hub requirements are met (some transit countries have their own documentation requirements for animals in transit)
  3. Book your pet’s cargo space before purchasing your own passenger ticket, or on the same day at minimum; cargo capacity for live animals fills independently of passenger seats

Tapeworm treatment: a requirement many owners miss

New Zealand’s biosecurity rules require dogs to be treated for tapeworm before arrival. The treatment must use a praziquantel-based product at a dose approved by MPI, administered no more than 14 days before departure and at least 24 hours before departure. Both the product name and the treatment date must be recorded in the health certificate.

This is one of the details most commonly missed by owners who read a general overview of NZ requirements but do not go through the specific IHS requirements. The APHA-accredited vet who issues your health certificate will record it, but you need to ensure the treatment happens at the right time. If your vet appointment for the health certificate is on day 13 before departure, you can do both in the same visit. If you leave it later, the 14-day window closes.

Internal parasite treatment requirements apply to cats as well. Confirm with your vet and with the current MPI IHS documentation what is required for your specific pet’s species.

The airline cargo fee is calculated by volumetric weight (a formula based on crate dimensions) rather than the animal’s actual weight. A large crate takes up a lot of cargo volume. This is the single biggest cost and the one that varies most between breeds.

Mistakes that delay UK to NZ pet imports

Starting the titre test too late. The 90-day wait from blood draw is the core constraint. If you draw blood 90 days before your travel date and the test fails, you are travelling without your pet. Draw blood at least 5 to 6 months ahead of your planned travel date.

Letting the rabies vaccination lapse before arrival. If your pet’s rabies vaccination expires during the process, you need a booster and a new titre test, resetting the clock. Keep the vaccination current throughout the entire process.

Using a non-approved laboratory for the titre test. Only MPI-approved laboratories produce results that New Zealand accepts. Confirm your lab is on the current MPI list before blood is drawn. This list is updated periodically and may change.

Not accounting for the July 2026 IHS update. If MPI’s new Import Health Standard introduced changes to approved products, health certificate formats, or parasite treatment requirements, applications prepared before the update may not meet the new standard. Verify against the current IHS document on the MPI website.

Health certificate timing errors. The certificate must be issued within 10 days of departure and endorsed by APHA. If you allow less than a week for APHA postal endorsement, you are cutting it very fine. Use the APHA in-person appointment option in Edinburgh if timing is tight.

Failing to pre-book quarantine. MPI quarantine slots are not guaranteed on arrival. If you arrive without a confirmed booking, your pet may be held in an unplanned interim facility at your expense until a slot opens. Book the quarantine slot the moment your travel date is fixed.

Key takeaways

  • The RNATT titre test blood draw must happen at least 90 days before arrival in New Zealand. That is the core timing constraint. Start 5 to 6 months ahead, not 3.
  • MPI import permit processing takes 5 to 15 working days. Apply only after you have a passing titre test result.
  • 10 days quarantine at Auckland or Christchurch is mandatory. Pre-book the slot. There are no exemptions.
  • Emirates via Dubai and Singapore Airlines via Singapore are the main cargo routes from the UK. No non-stop option exists.
  • Tapeworm treatment is a specific NZ requirement that must happen within 14 days of departure and be recorded in the health certificate.
  • Total cost for a medium dog runs approximately £4,800 to £7,200 before NZD quarantine fees.
  • The July 2026 MPI IHS update may affect approved products, certificate formats, and laboratory lists. Always verify against the current MPI documentation.
