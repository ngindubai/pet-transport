Pet Transport UK to Spain: 2026 Costs, Paperwork and Driving vs Flying

By Pet Transport Global Editorial Team  ·  4 June 2026  ·  8 min read

Need help moving your pet internationally? Get a free quote

The UK to Spain pet move is one of the most common international relocations for British owners. Spain is also the most popular destination for UK expats, so the route is well-trodden. The paperwork is genuinely straightforward compared to longer routes, but it works differently post-Brexit from how it used to, and the driving versus flying decision deserves more thought than most people give it.

Here is what you actually need to know for 2026.

What paperwork does Spain require for a UK pet in 2026?

Spain follows EU rules, under Regulation 2026/131 (which replaced Regulation 576/2013 in April 2026). For UK pets, the practical requirements are unchanged by that update. Three things are needed.

Microchip. ISO 11784/11785, 15-digit format. It must have been implanted before or on the same day as the first rabies vaccination. If your pet was chipped years ago before a vaccination, that timing requirement is already met.

Rabies vaccination. Must be current and within the manufacturer’s validity period (typically 1 or 3 years). If this is a first-ever vaccination, there is a 21-day wait before travel. A booster given within the previous vaccination’s validity period takes effect immediately, with no waiting period.

Animal Health Certificate (AHC). This replaced the EU pet passport for UK pets in January 2021. You need a new one for every trip into the EU. An Official Veterinarian (OV) registered with APHA must examine your pet and issue the certificate within 10 days of your travel date. The original document travels with you. A photocopy will be refused at the border.

Once in Spain, the AHC remains valid for 4 months of onward EU travel. If your trip takes you into Portugal, France, or elsewhere in the EU, you do not need a new certificate.

There is no import permit, no quarantine, and no rabies titre test for UK pets entering Spain. The titre test applies only to pets from non-listed third countries; the UK, post-Brexit, sits on the EU’s listed third-country register for pet travel.

One thing that consistently surprises people: Spain does not require the echinococcus tapeworm treatment. That treatment is for re-entering the UK, not for entering Spain.

The governing authority on arrival is MAPA (Ministerio de Agricultura, Pesca y Alimentacion). See the Spain pet import guide for the full regulatory breakdown.

Driving via the Eurotunnel: how does it work for pets?

For most dogs and cats, driving is the lower-stress option. Your pet stays in the car throughout the 35-minute Eurotunnel crossing. No cargo hold, no separation, no handling by strangers.

How it works:

  1. Book the crossing at eurotunnel.com. There is a pet supplement on top of the vehicle ticket (currently around £30 per pet).
  2. Arrive at Folkestone terminal. There are exercise areas and a waiting lounge.
  3. Drive onto the train, remain in your vehicle, and cross.
  4. Drive off at Calais and continue south through France.

From Calais: London to Barcelona is about 15 to 16 hours of driving. London to Madrid is 17 to 18 hours. London to Malaga is closer to 22 hours. Most people stop overnight in southern France. Your pet travels in the car, in their usual space.

Ferry routes are worth considering if you have a large dog, several pets, or an anxious animal. Brittany Ferries runs Portsmouth to Santander (around 24 hours) and Portsmouth or Plymouth to Bilbao (24 hours). Pets travel in the vehicle on the car deck or in dedicated pet areas depending on the service. These are good options for larger dogs that would otherwise need to go as cargo, and the overnight crossing removes a day of driving.

The main financial advantage of driving is that there is no cargo fee. For a medium or large dog, Eurotunnel supplement plus overnight accommodation typically comes to less than airline cargo.

When is flying actually the better option?

Flying makes clear sense in specific situations.

Small dogs and cats that can travel in cabin. Iberia, Vueling, and British Airways accept small pets in the cabin on UK-Spain routes. The pet travels in a carrier under the seat. If your pet is under the weight limit (typically around 8kg including carrier), this is easy and quick. The flight itself is 2.5 to 3 hours.

Tight deadlines. If you have a hard move date and cannot take 2 to 3 days for the drive, flying is the practical choice.

Moving to a remote Spanish destination without a car. If you are emigrating permanently and arriving by air, you may not have a car to drive. A single pet as cargo is manageable.

Practical notes if you are flying:

Ryanair and Wizz Air do not accept pets on any route. If you are booking a cheap flight for yourself, your pet needs to be on a different airline.

For larger dogs travelling as manifest cargo, the cost varies by weight, crate size, and airline, but £200 to £700 is a realistic range for UK-Spain routes. The dog travels in the pressurised hold on the same aircraft. You drop them at the live animal cargo desk before departure.

Brachycephalic breeds (Bulldogs, Pugs, French Bulldogs) are not restricted by Spanish import rules. Some airlines do restrict them from cargo holds on welfare grounds, so confirm the airline policy before booking cargo for a flat-nosed breed.

For a single medium dog moving from London to Barcelona, driving via Eurotunnel and stopping one night in France typically runs £400 to £600 all in, including the paperwork. The same move using a cargo flight would be £450 to £950 depending on the airline and dog size. The driving option is usually cheaper for dogs over 10kg. For a small cat or dog that can go in cabin, flying wins on both cost and simplicity.

What’s the timeline if I’m starting today?

Microchip and vaccination are current: Your only task is the AHC. Contact APHA-registered OVs in your area as soon as you have a travel date. You need an appointment that falls within 10 days of travel. OVs can be hard to find at short notice in some areas, so book early and then confirm the appointment timing as your travel date approaches.

Realistic time to travel: 2 to 4 weeks to get an OV appointment, then you travel.

Vaccination has lapsed (or never given): Get the vaccination now. Wait 21 days. Then book an OV appointment within 10 days of your travel date.

Minimum time from vaccination to travel: roughly 4 to 5 weeks.

No microchip yet: Get the microchip implanted first, then the vaccination at the same appointment or shortly after. The 21-day wait runs from the vaccination date. Then the OV appointment.

Minimum time: 5 to 6 weeks from the start.

This is considerably shorter than long-haul routes. UK to Australia requires 7 months because of a mandatory titre test wait and 10 days of quarantine on arrival. UK to Spain has none of that. If your pet’s paperwork is current, you can move within a few weeks.

Common Brexit-era mistakes UK owners still make

Using the EU pet passport. Red UK-issued EU passports are not valid for travel from the UK into the EU. If your passport is from a UK-based vet and was issued before January 2021, you cannot use it for this trip. You need an AHC.

Booking the OV appointment too early. The AHC is only valid for 10 days from issue. An appointment booked 3 weeks before travel produces a certificate that has expired before you get on the plane or into the car.

Booking the OV too late. The other side of the same problem. Some areas have very few registered OVs, and availability is limited. Book as soon as you have a travel date.

Assuming the tapeworm treatment is required. It is not, for entry into Spain. It is required when re-entering the UK. People coming back from Spain with their pets often get caught by this.

Expecting Ryanair to take the dog. It will not. Ryanair has no pet policy. Wizz Air is the same. Check before you book your own ticket.

Travelling with a photocopy of the AHC. Only the original signed document is accepted at the border.
