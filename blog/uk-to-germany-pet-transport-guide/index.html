Pet Transport UK to Germany: Costs, AHC and Driving vs Flying in 2026

By Pet Transport Global Editorial Team  ·  6 July 2026  ·  8 min read

Need help moving your pet internationally? Get a free quote

A dog crossing from Dover to Calais in the back of the family car costs about the same as a decent weekend away. A dog flying cargo into Frankfurt costs roughly double, sometimes more. That’s the headline most owners moving from the UK to Germany actually want answered before anything else: which way is cheaper, and does cheaper mean worse?

It doesn’t, in this case. Both routes are well-trodden, well-documented, and Germany’s paperwork is the same regardless of how your pet arrives. What changes is the price, the stress on the animal, and how much of your own time the move eats up.

This guide walks through real 2026 costs for both options, what the Animal Health Certificate actually requires, which Channel crossings will take a pet, and how long to budget from a standing start to touchdown (or touch-tarmac) in Germany.

How much does pet transport from the UK to Germany cost in 2026?

Road and ferry, door to door, for one medium dog: GBP 900 to 1,800. That figure covers fuel, one or two nights in pet-friendly accommodation en route, the Eurotunnel or ferry pet fee, and the Animal Health Certificate. It assumes you’re driving your own car and doing the trip yourself rather than paying a courier.

Cargo flight to Frankfurt or Munich: GBP 1,400 to 2,600. Nearly all UK-Germany routes carry pets as manifest cargo rather than in the passenger cabin, so you’re paying an airline cargo tariff (priced on the greater of actual or volumetric weight), an IATA-compliant crate, the AHC, and usually an agent to handle the cargo booking end to end.

Three things move the number more than anything else:

Crate size scales the airline fee fast. A Staffordshire-sized dog in a mid-range crate might sit at the lower end; a Bernese Mountain Dog in a giant crate pushes towards the top of the range and sometimes past it.

Season matters for driving. Peak summer weekends on the Dover crossings mean higher fares and longer queues; travelling midweek or off-season shaves real money and time off the trip.

Multi-pet households favour the car. Eurotunnel and the Dover ferries charge per pet, but that’s still far below a second cargo booking. Two cats and a dog driving together might add GBP 45 to 75 in pet fees on top of the vehicle fare. The same three animals flying would mean three separate cargo consignments, which is rarely worth it unless distance or timing rules driving out.

Driving and ferry vs flying: which is genuinely better?

Choose driving if: you have one dog or up to a handful of pets, you’re not in a rush, and your final German address is reachable within a day or two’s drive from a Channel port. Aachen, Cologne and Frankfurt are realistic same-day drives from Calais. Berlin and Munich are more of a two-day trip with a stopover.

Choose flying if: your pet is anxious about long car journeys, you have a tight moving date, or your destination is genuinely far inland and a two-day drive with an unfamiliar pet in the back isn’t appealing. Flying also suits owners who are already flying themselves and want the pet on a similar schedule, even though pet and owner rarely travel on the identical flight.

The driving route has one underrated advantage: your pet never leaves your sight. No cargo hold, no separate paperwork check at a different counter, no waiting to hear the flight landed safely. For a nervous cat or a dog that’s never flown, that alone can make the ferry-and-drive option worth the extra day.

What paperwork does Germany require from UK pets?

Since Brexit, a UK pet passport doesn’t work for entry into Germany, or anywhere in the EU. What replaces it is the Animal Health Certificate (AHC), and every UK pet needs one.

Your dog or cat needs, in order:

  1. An ISO 11784/11785 microchip, implanted before or on the same day as the rabies vaccination that follows it.
  2. A rabies vaccination given at least 21 days before travel, and still within its valid period on the day of entry.
  3. An AHC, examined and signed by an APHA-authorised Official Veterinarian (not every vet holds this authorisation, so check before booking) within 10 days of your travel date.

The 10-day window is the detail that trips people up. If your ferry or flight is on a Saturday, the OV appointment needs to happen no earlier than the preceding Wednesday of the week before. Book too early and the certificate expires before you travel; book too late and there may not be an appointment slot left.

One thing that does not apply on this leg: tapeworm treatment. That requirement is for dogs coming back into Great Britain from the EU, not for the outbound journey. If your move is one-way, you can skip it. If you’re likely to bring the dog home again at some point, note it for that return leg and don’t confuse the two directions.

The AHC itself is a multi-page document completed by the OV and, once signed, automatically pre-notifies German border control through the EU’s TRACES system. You don’t file anything separately; the OV’s certificate does that step for you.

Which Channel crossings allow pets in vehicles?

Three practical options, all pet-in-car, no cargo hold:

Eurotunnel Le Shuttle: your pet stays in the vehicle for the 35-minute crossing. Check in at the pet reception at Folkestone or Calais at least an hour before departure. Pricing for pets starts around GBP 24 each way. This is the fastest of the three and the one most owners default to.

DFDS (Dover-Calais, Dover-Dunkirk): pets stay in the car, secured inside a carrier, for around GBP 15 per pet each way on top of the vehicle fare. Advance booking for the pet is required; DFDS doesn’t take foot passengers on these routes, so this only works if you’re driving.

P&O Ferries (Dover-Calais): similar setup, pet remains in the vehicle, broadly comparable pricing to DFDS. Worth comparing sailing times against DFDS on your travel date since fares and departure slots shift with demand.

Whichever you pick, your pet’s documents (the AHC, vaccination record, microchip confirmation) need to be accessible, not buried in a suitcase in the boot. Staff at both the ferry terminals and the Eurotunnel pet reception will ask to see them before you board.

How long does the door-to-door journey take?

By road: Calais to Frankfurt is roughly six hours of driving; Calais to Berlin or Munich is closer to nine or ten, which most owners split across two days with an overnight stop. Add the Channel crossing itself (35 minutes for Eurotunnel, 90 minutes to 2 hours for the Dover ferries) and time at each end for loading, checks and rest breaks.

By air: the flight itself is under two hours from most UK airports to Frankfurt or Munich. What takes longer is everything around it: the cargo booking process, the AHC timing, and getting your pet from the departure cargo terminal onto the aircraft and off again at the other end. Budget three to four hours either side of the flight time itself for a cargo move, longer if there’s a connection.

Preparation timeline, either route: two to four weeks is realistic if your pet’s rabies vaccination is already current. Book the ferry, Eurotunnel slot, or cargo space four to six weeks out if you’re travelling in peak summer, since capacity on both sides gets tight. Starting from an unvaccinated pet adds at least three to four weeks for the vaccination and the 21-day wait before the AHC can even be issued.

If your route includes a connection rather than a direct flight, check the hub. Lufthansa does not carry cargo-hold animals through Munich (assistance animals excepted); Frankfurt is the airline’s main hub for pet transfers and has a dedicated Animal Lounge for handling and short-stay care. A pet routed through the wrong hub can mean a booking that has to be rearranged entirely.

What happens after you arrive in Germany?

Getting through the border is only step one if you’re relocating rather than visiting. Dogs need registering with the local residents’ registration office (Einwohnermeldeamt) and, separately, with the Hundesteuer authority, Germany’s dog tax. Rates and processes vary city by city: Berlin, Munich and Hamburg each run this independently, and the annual tax typically lands somewhere between GBP 90 and 130 depending on the dog and the municipality. Cats don’t need this registration step.

FAQs about UK to Germany pet transport

Is it cheaper to drive or fly a pet from the UK to Germany? Driving is usually cheaper: GBP 900 to 1,800 all-in against GBP 1,400 to 2,600 for a cargo flight, mostly because almost no UK-Germany routes offer in-cabin travel for pets.

Do I need an Animal Health Certificate? Yes. The old EU pet passport doesn’t work post-Brexit. You need an AHC from an APHA-authorised Official Veterinarian, issued within 10 days of travel.

Does my dog need tapeworm treatment for Germany? No. That rule applies to the return leg into Great Britain, not the journey out to Germany.

Can my pet travel with me through the Eurotunnel? Yes. Your pet stays in the car for the 35-minute crossing, with a standard pet check-in beforehand.

Why can’t my pet’s flight go through Munich? Lufthansa doesn’t accept cargo-hold live animals transiting Munich. Route through Frankfurt instead, which has a dedicated Animal Lounge.

Do I need to register my dog on arrival? Yes, if you’re settling rather than visiting. Register with the local residents’ office and pay the Hundesteuer dog tax where your new address is based.
