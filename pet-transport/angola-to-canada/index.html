Checklist

What your pet needs

Every item below must be completed and verified before your pet can travel. Expand each category for the detail.

Microchip
ISO 11784/11785 microchip strongly recommended and, in practice, expected as identification for a dog arriving from a country on CFIA's high-risk list. Source: CFIA, inspection.canada.ca, 2026.
Rabies vaccination
Required for dogs, current and documented on a certificate in English or French. Source: CFIA, inspection.canada.ca, 2026.
Rabies titre test
Not required. CFIA does not ask a personal pet dog, even one arriving from a high-risk country, for a rabies antibody titre test. This is a genuine point of difference from the CDC's approach to the same Angola-origin dog entering the United States, where the choice is a valid titre test or a 28-day facility quarantine at the owner's cost.
Quarantine
No routine quarantine for a pet arriving with correct documentation as a personal, owner-accompanied animal.
Import permit
Not required for personal pets, defined by CFIA as 1-2 animals travelling with an owner who isn't transferring them to someone else on arrival. CFIA also presumes a dog under 8 months old is a commercial import if it isn't travelling with its named owner, and commercial dogs from high-risk countries have been barred outright since 28 September 2022. Confirm your pet's status directly with CFIA if age or documentation could put this in question.
Health certificate
Veterinary health certificate confirming vaccination status, microchip and general health, issued close to travel and presented in English or French at the port of entry.
Export permit
No standalone export permit for accompanying pets has been published. The government-endorsed health certificate from the Instituto dos Serviços de Veterinária (ISV) stands in for one. Angola-to-Canada pet exports are rare enough that a standard template isn't well established, so confirm the current process with ISV directly.
Veterinary inspection
Pre-export clinical examination by a licensed Angolan veterinarian.
Government endorsement
Government endorsement by the Instituto dos Serviços de Veterinária (ISV), under Angola's Ministry of Agriculture and Forests (MINAGRIF).
Health certificate
ISV-endorsed certificate, built to CFIA's format. Canada has no equivalent to the CDC's Certification of Foreign Rabies Vaccination and Microchip form used on Angola's US route, which removes one layer of paperwork this specific corridor doesn't need.

Critical points

Angola's presence on CFIA's high-risk-for-dog-rabies list, updated 13 March 2025, does not add a titre test for a personal, owner-accompanied pet, unlike the choice the CDC imposes on the same dog entering the US. The list's real bite is the commercial dog import ban in force since September 2022.

CFIA presumes a dog under 8 months old, not travelling with its named owner, is commercial. From a high-risk country like Angola, that presumption carries real weight. Confirm your pet's status with CFIA directly if age or documentation could put this in question.

Angola's own export process for pets is uncommon enough that a standard ISV certificate format isn't well established the way it is for busier corridors. Build in extra time to confirm the certificate meets CFIA's format before the appointment, not after.

No airline flies Luanda to Canada directly. TAP Air Portugal via Lisbon, or Lufthansa via Frankfurt, are the two workable routings.

Meeting Canada's entry requirements

The path into Canada is straightforward to follow once you know the steps. Give the time-sensitive items priority and check the details below.

Advice worth following

Check the airline’s crate size and labelling rules early. An undersized or wrongly labelled crate is a frequent reason pets are refused at check-in.

Ask about temperature embargoes for your travel window. Summer and winter cargo restrictions can rule out certain routes and dates entirely.

Microchip before you vaccinate. The microchip must be in place before the rabies shot for the vaccination to count, and getting this order wrong is the most common and costly mistake.

Use an official, government-authorised vet for the health certificate, not just your usual clinic. Many destinations only accept certificates signed by an approved vet.
