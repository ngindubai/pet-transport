Requirements

Canada entry requirements

Every item below must be in place before your pet can enter. We verify and track each one.

Microchip
15-digit ISO 11784/11785 microchip mandatory for a dog arriving from a CFIA-designated high-risk rabies country, implanted before or on the same day as the rabies vaccination. Source: CFIA, inspection.canada.ca/en/animal-health/terrestrial-animals/diseases/reportable/rabies/countries-high-risk-dog, 2026.
Rabies vaccination
Valid rabies vaccination certificate, signed by a licensed vet, identifying the dog's breed, colour, weight and microchip number, along with the vaccine's stated duration of immunity.
Rabies titre test
CFIA's published guidance for a personal pet dog from a high-risk country doesn't require a rabies titre test, unlike the equivalent CDC or MOCCAE pathways. Instead, a dog must be at least 7 months old at entry if it originates in, or has resided in or transited, a country on the high-risk list within the 6 months before arrival. Ukraine is on that list, so this age rule applies to every Ukraine-origin dog.
Quarantine
No routine quarantine for a compliant personal pet. CFIA's high-risk-country entry restriction targets commercial dogs, meaning those for resale, adoption, fostering, breeding, show, exhibition or research, which have been refused entry from high-risk countries since 28 September 2022. A family pet travelling with its owner isn't classed as commercial and isn't subject to that ban.
Import permit
No import permit required for a personal pet dog or cat. A CFIA veterinarian inspection is required at the first Canadian port of entry, and the owner must arrange the appointment at least 24 hours ahead of arrival.
Health certificate
The Ukrainian-issued international veterinary certificate (see origin side), presented to CBSA and the CFIA port veterinarian on arrival, along with proof of the dog's age if the 7-month high-risk-country minimum applies.
Leaving Ukraine

Export requirements

Export permit
No standalone export permit for a personal pet. The owner obtains Veterinary Certificate Form No. 1 (VET-1) from a Ukrainian state veterinary clinic, then exchanges it at the Polish or Romanian border for an international veterinary certificate, since no operating vet post exists at any Ukrainian airport.
Veterinary inspection
Standard clinical exam, plus rabies vaccination and microchipping if either isn't already current, from a licensed vet in Ukraine.
Government endorsement
The international certificate is countersigned by an official state veterinarian at the border crossing point.
Health certificate
International veterinary certificate recording the microchip number, rabies vaccination date, and rabies titre result, completed in the general format CFIA accepts for a country without a bilateral certificate template.
Costs

What this route typically costs

Ukrainian vet fees for microchip and vaccination: roughly 650-1,000 UAH (about USD 15-25)
Overland transfer from Ukraine to Warsaw or Bucharest: USD 300-900
International certificate completion at a border-area clinic: USD 80-150
IATA-compliant travel crate: USD 100-350
Warsaw-Toronto cargo fare on LOT: USD 1,200-2,800 depending on crate size
CFIA port-of-entry inspection: arranged with the local CFIA Animal Health office; confirm whether a fee applies at the specific port before travel

Critical points

Ukraine's civilian airspace remains closed as of mid-2026, so every pet begins this route by road into Poland or Romania before any flight is possible.

Canada's CUAET-linked work permit measures, most recently extended in March 2026 to give holders until 31 March 2027 to apply for a further extension, cover people only. There is no equivalent pet fast-track: a Ukrainian family arriving under those provisions still needs the standard CFIA high-risk-country dog process described here, with no waived age rule and no shortcut on the microchip timing.

A dog younger than 7 months that has lived in or transited Ukraine within the past 6 months cannot currently meet CFIA's minimum age rule for high-risk-country entry. Check the date arithmetic carefully before booking a flight; there is no exception for puppies.

The CFIA's commercial-dog ban on high-risk countries, in force since 28 September 2022, does not apply to a personal pet travelling with its owner. It's still worth confirming that distinction with the CFIA port office in advance, since documentation from a high-risk country gets closer scrutiny on arrival.

Canada import rules, explained

Canada runs a strict import regime, so every document is checked closely on arrival. Build your plan around the fixed waiting periods, because they set the pace for everything else.

Practical tips for this move

Get your pet used to the travel crate weeks in advance. A calm, crate-familiar animal travels far better than one meeting the crate for the first time at the airport.

Confirm live animal acceptance on every flight segment before booking. Not all aircraft or connections take pets, and cargo terms can differ leg to leg.

Keep every original document together and take copies. Border officials want originals, and a missing signature or date can hold up clearance.

Work backwards from your travel date and add a buffer. Fixed waiting periods mean a single slipped appointment can push the whole move back by weeks.
