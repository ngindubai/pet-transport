Checklist

What your pet needs

Every item below must be completed and verified before your pet can travel. Expand each category for the detail.

Microchip
Required. ISO 11784/11785 standard (15-digit). Must be implanted before or on the same day as the first rabies vaccination.
Rabies vaccination
Required. Minimum age 12 weeks. 21-day wait after primary vaccination before the titre test blood draw.
Rabies titre test
Required. Rwanda is an EU unlisted third country. FAVN or ELISA titre test at an EU-approved laboratory. Minimum result 0.5 IU/ml. Blood drawn at least 30 days after primary vaccination. Mandatory 90-day wait from the date of the successful blood draw before entry into Austria. Source: EU Regulation (EU) 2020/692.
Quarantine
No routine quarantine for pets arriving with compliant documentation.
Import permit
Not required for non-commercial companion pet movements.
Health certificate
EU-format Animal Health Certificate (AHC) endorsed by the origin authority, issued within 10 days of travel to Austria.
Export permit
No formal export permit required for personal companion pets.
Health certificate
EU-format health certificate from an RAB-authorised veterinarian, endorsed by Rwanda Agriculture Board (RAB) before departure.

Critical points

The 90-day wait runs from the date blood was drawn, not from the date results were received. Getting this backwards adds three months you did not plan for.

Microchip must be implanted before the rabies vaccination. A chip fitted after the vaccination means the vaccination does not count and a new one is needed.

Two separate Austrian bodies share oversight of pet imports: AGES handles the technical checks, while the federal health ministry (BMSGPK) sits above it. If a query comes up, it may not be a single office you are dealing with.

There is no direct flight from Kigali to Vienna. Every routing involves at least one connection, so factor transit time into your pet's total journey length.

Austria entry requirements at a glance

Bringing a pet into Austria involves a fair amount of documentation. Keep everything organised and check each item against the current requirements before you book.

Getting the details right

Microchip before you vaccinate. The microchip must be in place before the rabies shot for the vaccination to count, and getting this order wrong is the most common and costly mistake.

Use an official, government-authorised vet for the health certificate, not just your usual clinic. Many destinations only accept certificates signed by an approved vet.

If this is your first international move, consider an IPATA-registered pet transport agent. They handle the documents, airline booking and crate sizing, and can troubleshoot problems.

Get your pet used to the travel crate weeks in advance. A calm, crate-familiar animal travels far better than one meeting the crate for the first time at the airport.
