Requirements

Canada entry requirements

Every item below must be in place before your pet can enter. We verify and track each one.

Microchip
Not generally required by CFIA for a personal pet dog or cat. The exception is an animal arriving from a CFIA-designated high-risk rabies country, where a 15-digit ISO microchip is mandatory. Paraguay is not on that list, so a microchip is not a federal entry requirement here, though most owners already have one from the export process and it's worth keeping regardless.
Rabies vaccination
Required for any dog or cat over 3 months old. The certificate must be in English or French, signed by a licensed vet, and must state the vaccine's trade name, serial number and expiry date, not just the date given.
Rabies titre test
Not required. CFIA's rabies antibody titre pathway and its stricter documentation apply only to dogs arriving from a country on the high-risk list. Paraguay is not on that list, last updated 13 March 2025, so the standard rabies certificate route applies instead.
Quarantine
No routine federal quarantine for a compliant pet. CBSA officers inspect documents and the animal at the border rather than holding it for a fixed period.
Import permit
No CFIA import permit required for a personal, non-commercial pet dog or cat. A CBSA border inspection fee of roughly CAD 30-55 can apply.
Health certificate
Rabies vaccination certificate in English or French, signed by a licensed vet, plus a general health certificate most airlines still require for booking even though CFIA doesn't mandate one federally for a low-risk-country pet.
Leaving Paraguay

Export requirements

Export permit
No separate export permit beyond the CVI. The certificate functions as export authorisation once SENACSA has confirmed the clinical inspection, microchip match and vaccination record.
Health certificate
Certificado Veterinario Internacional (CVI), issued by SENACSA under Tramite 2 for companion animal export. A clinical inspection must take place within 10 days of the CVI being issued, through the SENACSA Digital portal or the Certification Department in person.
Costs

What this route typically costs

SENACSA CVI and clinical inspection: fee not published online, typically a modest local charge, confirm at time of application
Rabies vaccination certificate: USD 80-180 equivalent
IATA-compliant travel crate: USD 100-300 if one is not already owned
CBSA border inspection fee: CAD 30-55
Cargo Asuncion-Panama-Canada or Asuncion-Sao Paulo-Canada for a medium dog: typically USD 2,200-4,200
Total typical range: USD 2,600-4,900 over 4-7 weeks.

Critical points

Paraguay is not on the CFIA's high-risk dog rabies list, last revised 13 March 2025, unlike Bolivia, Brazil, Colombia, Ecuador, Guatemala and several other Latin American neighbours. That removes the mandatory microchip, the stricter documentation and the 7-month minimum age CFIA applies to dogs from listed countries.

CFIA clearance is a federal minimum, not the whole story. Ontario bans the import of Pit Bull-type dogs under its own Dog Owners' Liability Act, entirely separate from anything CFIA checks at the border. Confirm the destination province's own rules before assuming federal clearance is enough.

Copa does not carry live animal cargo on weekends, since its Panama hub vet only works weekdays. This is the most common scheduling trap on this route, not a paperwork issue.

What Canada requires

Bringing a pet into Canada is one of the simpler processes. Cover the core requirements and you should have few surprises.

Avoiding the common mistakes

Ask about temperature embargoes for your travel window. Summer and winter cargo restrictions can rule out certain routes and dates entirely.

Microchip before you vaccinate. The microchip must be in place before the rabies shot for the vaccination to count, and getting this order wrong is the most common and costly mistake.

Use an official, government-authorised vet for the health certificate, not just your usual clinic. Many destinations only accept certificates signed by an approved vet.

If this is your first international move, consider an IPATA-registered pet transport agent. They handle the documents, airline booking and crate sizing, and can troubleshoot problems.
