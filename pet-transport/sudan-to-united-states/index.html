Overview

Getting a pet from Sudan to United States is a step by step process, and each stage has its own timing. This route sits at the strict end, and about 26-44 weeks of preparation is realistic. Compliant pets are not held in quarantine on arrival.

A titre test could be required in some cases, so review the destination's conditions. Start with the step by step process below and build your plan from there.

Step by step

The Sudan to United States process

1
Before any other planning step.
Confirm, in writing, whether Sudan's veterinary authority can currently issue export documentation for your specific location, and whether any carrier is currently accepting live pet cargo out of the country.

Responsible: Owner, contacting Sudan's Ministry of Animal Resources and Fisheries and prospective carriers directly

2
14-16 weeks before an intended date, dependent entirely on step 1.
Microchip, then rabies vaccination, strictly in that order, if a vet with functioning facilities is accessible.

Responsible: Sudan-based vet, where available

3
At least 30 days after vaccination, with the result needed at least 28 days before arrival.
Blood draw for the rabies titre test, sent to a CDC-approved overseas laboratory.

Responsible: Sudan-based vet, sample sent overseas

4
Once the titre result is confirmed, always before the flight.
Complete the CDC Dog Import Form and book a reservation at a CDC-registered animal care facility at the intended US arrival airport.

Responsible: Owner

5
As close to travel as reliable confirmation allows, given how recently Khartoum's international routes reopened.
Confirm current flight availability and live animal cargo acceptance directly with EgyptAir, Sudan Airways or a Gulf carrier.

Responsible: Owner or a specialist agent with current Sudan experience

6
Day of arrival.
Arrival at a CDC-registered facility airport. If the titre result was not passing, the 28-day quarantine begins here.

Responsible: CDC-registered facility staff and CBP

Checklist

United States: entry requirements

RequirementDetail
MicrochipA universal-scanner-readable microchip is required before the rabies vaccination. Because Sudan is on the CDC's high-risk list for dog rabies, a vaccine given before the chip does not count towards US entry, and the sequence has to be right first time.
Rabies vaccinationRequired for dogs, first dose no earlier than 12 weeks old. Cats face no federal CDC rabies vaccination requirement, only a general health check on arrival.
Rabies titre testRequired for a dog travelling on a Sudan-issued vaccination certificate. Blood must be drawn at least 30 days after vaccination and the result must land at least 28 days before the flight arrives in the US, tested at a CDC-approved laboratory. Sudan has no confirmed government-approved rabies serology laboratory of its own currently operating, so the sample would need to travel overseas, adding transit time on top of standard lab turnaround, itself dependent on whether a sample can reliably leave the country under current conditions. Without a passing result, a 28-day quarantine at a CDC-registered facility applies on arrival.
QuarantineNone for a dog arriving with a valid passing titre and a completed CDC Dog Import Form. Without the titre, a mandatory 28-day quarantine at a CDC-registered animal care facility applies, at the owner's cost. Cats face no CDC quarantine requirement.
Import permitCDC Dog Import Form, a free form completed online by the owner before travel, with a current photograph and, since Sudan is high-risk, a reservation at a CDC-registered animal care facility booked ahead of departure. No equivalent form exists for cats.
Health certificateCDC does not itself demand a Sudan-endorsed export certificate for entry, but airlines generally want a veterinary health certificate issued close to the travel date, and a Customs and Border Protection officer can refuse entry to any animal that looks unwell regardless of paperwork.

Leaving Sudan

Export requirements

RequirementDetail
Export permitNo functioning online export process is confirmed at the time of writing. Sudan's federal veterinary authority handles export documentation in principle, but the civil conflict, ongoing since April 2023, has severely disrupted normal administrative capacity across the country, according to a peer-reviewed 2023 assessment of Sudan's veterinary services published via the National Institutes of Health (PMC10769538, 'Veterinary services under siege'). Get current, written confirmation of what's actually operating before committing to a travel date.
Veterinary inspectionIn principle, a government or government-recognised vet examines the animal and confirms microchip and vaccination status. Access to functioning veterinary infrastructure varies sharply by region and has been affected by the conflict, so don't assume the same service level a stable country would offer.
Government endorsementEndorsement would normally sit with the Ministry of Animal Resources and Fisheries at federal level. This page's research, conducted 21 July 2026, could not confirm current, reliable operating capacity for this specific function through published sources.
Health certificateA veterinary health certificate is the standard requirement in principle. Given the disrupted state of Sudan's veterinary services since 2023, get direct, current confirmation of exactly what documentation will actually be issued and accepted, rather than relying on pre-conflict guidance found online.

Critical points

Read before you book

⚠ This is very likely the most complex route on the entire site right now: CDC high-risk status for the US side, combined with an origin airport that only reopened to scheduled international flights in February 2026 after a civil conflict closed it for nearly three years.
⚠ Khartoum International Airport lost a reported 28 aircraft to the conflict, and Sudanese airspace was suspended again in early May 2026 after a drone attack before reopening days later. Security conditions remain genuinely fragile.
⚠ No airline's live animal cargo acceptance out of Khartoum could be confirmed as currently, reliably operating as of this page's research on 21 July 2026. We're saying so plainly rather than inventing a confident-sounding process, because getting this wrong risks a stranded animal in an active conflict zone.
⚠ Sudan's CDC high-risk status means a dog without a valid titre result must be routed to one of six specific US facility airports. Combine that constraint with an unconfirmed departure routing and this becomes a two-sided planning problem, not a one-sided one.

EgyptAir's own Cairo to US network reaches several CDC-registered facility airports, but whether live pet cargo is currently and reliably moving out of Khartoum at all has not been confirmed as of this page's research. Qatar Airways
Doha connects to several of the six CDC-registered facility airports, including New York JFK and Washington Dulles, which is useful in principle for a high-risk-country dog. No confirmed current scheduled service links Khartoum to Doha directly, so a pet would likely need to reach a more stable regional airport first, such as Port Sudan. Confirm current routing and live animal acceptance directly with the carrier before booking.

How United States handles incoming pets

United States takes biosecurity seriously, and the requirements reflect that. The paperwork is exacting, so accuracy matters as much as timing.

What experienced owners do

Check the airline’s crate size and labelling rules early. An undersized or wrongly labelled crate is a frequent reason pets are refused at check-in.

Ask about temperature embargoes for your travel window. Summer and winter cargo restrictions can rule out certain routes and dates entirely.

Book the titre test laboratory well ahead. Approved labs have limited slots and the sample takes time to process, so this is not something to leave to the final weeks.

Microchip before you vaccinate. The microchip must be in place before the rabies shot for the vaccination to count, and getting this order wrong is the most common and costly mistake.
