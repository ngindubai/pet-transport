Checklist

What your pet needs

Every item below must be completed and verified before your pet can travel. Expand each category for the detail.

Microchip
Required for dogs, implanted before rabies vaccination (a vaccine given first does not count). Not a federal requirement for cats, though recommended.
Rabies vaccination
Required for dogs, given at 12 weeks or older, with the government vet-endorsed Certification of Foreign Rabies Vaccination and Microchip form. Not required for cats: no federal US health requirements apply to pet cats at all.
Rabies titre test
Required for dogs (Kyrgyzstan is on the CDC's high-risk-for-dog-rabies list), or accept a 28-day quarantine at a CDC-registered facility instead. Blood drawn at least 30 days after vaccination and at least 28 days before entry, tested at a CDC-approved laboratory. Not required for cats.
Quarantine
Required if a dog has no valid rabies serology titre report: 28 days at a CDC-registered animal care facility, paid for by the owner. Not required for dogs with a valid titre, or for any cat.
Import permit
No formal permit for dogs. The free CDC Dog Import Form, completed online before travel, serves as the registration document. Nothing at all required for cats.
Health certificate
Not a standalone certificate for dogs; the Certification of Foreign Rabies Vaccination and Microchip form covers the health attestation. No certificate required for cats.
Export permit
Not a separate formal export permit. The State Inspectorate for Veterinary and Phytosanitary Safety of the Kyrgyz Republic (Bishkek) endorses the export health certificate.
Veterinary inspection
Clinical examination by a licensed Kyrgyz veterinarian shortly before travel.
Government endorsement
The Certification of Foreign Rabies Vaccination and Microchip form, required by the CDC for dogs, must be completed by the vet and endorsed by the State Inspectorate before departure.
Health certificate
Government-endorsed veterinary health certificate recording microchip number, rabies vaccination date, and clinical health, issued by the State Inspectorate.

Carriers on this route

Not all airlines accept live animals on this route. Confirm the operating carrier's current policy for every flight segment.

Turkish Airlines
Bishkek to Istanbul, then Istanbul direct to several of the CDC's designated ports, including New York JFK, Washington Dulles (IAD), and Atlanta (ATL). This is effectively the only realistic long-haul option for this route.

Critical points

Dogs must arrive by air at the specific US airport where their reserved CDC-registered animal care facility is located. No domestic connections are allowed before that processing is complete.

Only six US airports currently have CDC-registered animal care facilities: New York JFK, Los Angeles LAX, Miami MIA, San Francisco SFO, Atlanta ATL, and Washington Dulles IAD (plus Philadelphia PHL). Your Turkish Airlines routing must terminate at one of these.

Cats face none of this. If you are only shipping a cat, the CDC's dog-specific high-risk rules do not apply and the process is far simpler.

Air Astana does not yet serve the US. A nonstop route is planned for around 2027, subject to Kazakhstan securing FAA Category 1 status, so it is not an option for this route today.

What United States requires

The requirements for United States are thorough rather than difficult. Work through them methodically and confirm anything you are unsure about with the official source.

Avoiding the common mistakes

Microchip before you vaccinate. The microchip must be in place before the rabies shot for the vaccination to count, and getting this order wrong is the most common and costly mistake.

Use an official, government-authorised vet for the health certificate, not just your usual clinic. Many destinations only accept certificates signed by an approved vet.

If this is your first international move, consider an IPATA-registered pet transport agent. They handle the documents, airline booking and crate sizing, and can troubleshoot problems.

Get your pet used to the travel crate weeks in advance. A calm, crate-familiar animal travels far better than one meeting the crate for the first time at the airport.
