Checklist

What your pet needs

Every item below must be completed and verified before your pet can travel. Expand each category for the detail.

Microchip
Required (ISO 11784/11785 standard). Must be implanted before the rabies vaccination, or the vaccination doesn't count toward UK entry. Source: GOV.UK, Bringing a pet to Great Britain, 2026.
Rabies vaccination
Required, minimum age 12 weeks. A 21-day wait after the first vaccination applies before travel would otherwise be permitted, and this period also has to elapse before the titre blood sample can be drawn. Source: GOV.UK.
Rabies titre test
Required. Cote d'Ivoire is not on the UK's Part 1 or Part 2 listed-country tables, so the standard unlisted-country pathway applies: blood drawn at least 30 days after vaccination, tested at an FAVN or ELISA-capable APHA-approved laboratory, minimum result 0.5 IU/ml, with a 90-day wait after a passing result before travel. Source: GOV.UK, 2026.
Quarantine
No routine quarantine for a pet arriving with fully compliant documentation. Penalty quarantine of up to 4 months applies if a pet arrives without the correct paperwork, unvaccinated, or via a non-approved route, at the owner's cost. Source: GOV.UK.
Import permit
Not required. The Animal Health Certificate itself is the entry document; the UK has no separate import permit system for pets.
Health certificate
Required. Animal Health Certificate (AHC) issued by the DSV-endorsed vet in Cote d'Ivoire, valid 10 days from issue to entry into Great Britain. Dogs also need a Praziquantel tapeworm treatment recorded on the certificate, given 24 to 120 hours before the scheduled UK arrival time; cats and ferrets are exempt from this step.
Export permit
No standalone export permit separate from the health certificate for a pet dog or cat. The Direction des Services Veterinaires (DSV), under the Ministry of Animal and Fisheries Resources (Ministere des Ressources Animales et Halieutiques, MIRAH), endorses the certificate that clears the animal to leave Cote d'Ivoire.
Veterinary inspection
Clinical check by a DSV-registered veterinarian close to travel, confirming the animal is fit to fly, current on its rabies vaccination, and matches its microchip record.
Government endorsement
DSV endorsement, stamp and signature, on the health certificate is required before an airline or cargo agent will accept the paperwork. DSV does not publish a fixed turnaround time or fee schedule online, so confirm both directly with a registered vet in Abidjan and build in a buffer of at least two weeks.
Health certificate
DSV-endorsed veterinary health certificate from a registered vet in Abidjan or another Ivorian city, dated within 10 days of travel to meet airline and UK entry expectations.

Airlines

Carriers on this route

Not all airlines accept live animals on this route. Confirm the operating carrier's current policy for every flight segment.

Royal Air Maroc
Routes Abidjan to Casablanca (CMN), then on to London Heathrow on Royal Air Maroc's own network, a genuine single-carrier one-stop. Cabin pets are capped at 8kg including the carrier, and a long list of brachycephalic dog and cat breeds is excluded from both cabin and hold.
Cabin and Cargo
Ethiopian Airlines
Connects Abidjan to Addis Ababa (ADD), then Addis to London Heathrow on Ethiopian's own daily service, another single-carrier option. Cabin limit is 8kg including the box; larger pets travel via Ethiopian Cargo, which does not accept snub-nosed breeds in the hold.
Cabin and Cargo
Air France
Operates up to two flights a day between Abidjan and Paris-Charles de Gaulle, a major cargo hub with onward European connections. Small pets under 8kg travel in the cabin; larger animals go via Air France Cargo, which excludes snub-nosed breeds from the hold.
Cabin and Cargo
Brussels Airlines
Flies Abidjan to Brussels only once a week, on Saturdays, so this route needs booking well ahead of any tight deadline. Dogs and cats travel in the hold under IATA Live Animal Regulations, with a separate short-haul leg needed onward to London.
Cargo Only

What this route typically costs

DSV endorsement and vet documentation in Cote d'Ivoire: FCFA 60,000-150,000 (roughly USD 100-250)
FAVN or ELISA titre test at an APHA-approved laboratory, plus international courier: GBP 150-300
UK Animal Health Certificate from the DSV-endorsed vet: FCFA 40,000-90,000 (roughly USD 65-150)
Tapeworm treatment for dogs, done locally in Cote d'Ivoire: GBP 15-40 equivalent
IATA-compliant travel crate: GBP 100-350
Cargo or cabin fare, Abidjan to London via Casablanca, Addis Ababa, Paris or Brussels: GBP 1,200-2,800 depending on routing, crate size and season
Total typical range: GBP 1,800-4,200

Critical points

Cote d'Ivoire's unlisted status makes the titre test non-negotiable, and its 30-day-then-90-day clock sets the real floor for this route. Nothing shortens it.

DSV does not publish a fixed fee or turnaround schedule for its endorsement. Confirm both directly with a registered vet in Abidjan before committing to a flight date, and build in extra time.

Brussels Airlines flies Abidjan to Brussels only once a week, on Saturdays. Missing that single weekly slot pushes the whole itinerary back seven days.

Snub-nosed breeds face restrictions across every airline on this route: Royal Air Maroc, Ethiopian Airlines and Air France all limit or exclude brachycephalic dogs and cats from the hold.

The rules for bringing a pet into United Kingdom

United Kingdom asks for several pieces of paperwork, and they all need to line up. A checklist and a clear timeline are your best tools here.

How to keep this move on track

Check the airline’s crate size and labelling rules early. An undersized or wrongly labelled crate is a frequent reason pets are refused at check-in.

Ask about temperature embargoes for your travel window. Summer and winter cargo restrictions can rule out certain routes and dates entirely.

Book the titre test laboratory well ahead. Approved labs have limited slots and the sample takes time to process, so this is not something to leave to the final weeks.

Microchip before you vaccinate. The microchip must be in place before the rabies shot for the vaccination to count, and getting this order wrong is the most common and costly mistake.
