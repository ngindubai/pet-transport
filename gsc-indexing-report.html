What "Crawled - Currently Not Indexed" Actually Means Diagnosis

This status means Google's crawler visited the URL successfully (no errors, no blocks in robots.txt, page loaded fine) but the indexing pipeline — a separate system — evaluated the page and decided not to include it in the search index.

This is very different from a crawl error. The page is reachable. The problem is the content or signals attached to it. Google will re-evaluate these pages on future crawl passes; if nothing changes, they'll stay out of the index indefinitely.

The most likely causes for this site specifically

1. Programmatic content at scale — thin content signal (Highest risk) With 5,342 route pages generated from templates, Google's "helpful content" system is likely running similarity checks. Even with variation in regulatory details and copy, pages that share a skeletal structure, similar section headings, and near-identical FAQ patterns may be flagged as offering insufficient unique value. This is the single biggest risk factor for a programmatic SEO site of this size on a one-year-old domain.
2. Crawl budget vs. indexing decision — authority lag Google allocates crawl budget based on domain authority and PageRank. At one year old, pettransportglobal.com is still building authority. When Google encounters a large batch of new pages (yesterday's upload), it will crawl them quickly to check if they exist — but it won't necessarily index them all. It holds a percentage back and re-evaluates over weeks as the domain earns more trust signals (backlinks, engagement, dwell time).
3. Internal PageRank dilution 5,342 route pages is a large number for the current domain authority. The internal PageRank is spread very thin across them. Pages with almost no inbound internal links — especially P3/P4 routes for low-traffic corridors — have weak signals and are candidates for this status.
4. The batch upload timing (lower urgency — wait and see) You uploaded a large batch yesterday. The spike visible in the GSC graph is entirely consistent with Google crawling a fresh batch — it will assess and index progressively over the coming weeks. Some of those 1,215 pages will move into the index without any action on your part.
5. "Alternate page with proper canonical tag" (23 pages) — needs investigation Separate from the main issue, 23 pages are being treated as duplicates by Google despite having canonical tags. This suggests either incorrect canonical tags (pointing to a different URL than intended), or two pages with near-identical content where Google is ignoring your stated canonical. These are worth investigating directly.

What this is NOT

  • Not a manual penalty — a penalty shows under "Manual Actions" in GSC, not coverage
  • Not a robots.txt block — those show as "Blocked by robots.txt"
  • Not a server error — those show as "Server error (5xx)"
  • Not caused by noindex tags — those show as "Excluded by noindex tag"

How to Find Out Which Pages Are Indexed Diagnosis Tools

There are four approaches, from lowest to highest effort, depending on what you need to know.

Method 1 — GSC Coverage export (recommended starting point)

  1. Go to Google Search Console > Indexing > Pages
  2. Click "Crawled - currently not indexed" to filter to that group
  3. Scroll down to the URL list — click "Export" (top right) and download as CSV
  4. Repeat for "Indexed" to get the full indexed URL list
  5. Open both CSVs in Excel — the non-indexed list will tell you which sections (routes vs. hubs vs. blog) dominate the problem
What to look for in the export: If non-indexed pages cluster heavily in /routes/, that confirms thin content is the issue. If they're spread across all sections, authority lag is more likely. If blog and hub pages are also excluded, there may be a site-wide quality signal being applied.

Method 2 — Google site: operator (quick estimate)

In Google search, type: site:pettransportglobal.com

This returns an approximate count of indexed pages and lets you browse them. It is not perfectly accurate (Google deliberately undercounts), but it gives a directional read. You can narrow it with: site:pettransportglobal.com/pet-transport/routes/ to see how many route pages are indexed.

Method 3 — GSC URL Inspection (for specific pages)

In GSC, paste any individual URL into the URL Inspection tool. This gives you the exact status: whether it's indexed, when it was last crawled, what the rendered HTML looked like to Googlebot, and whether there's a canonical issue. Use this for:

  • Checking your highest-priority P1 routes (the original 89)
  • Spot-checking pages from the "not indexed" CSV export
  • Verifying the 23 "alternate canonical" pages

Method 4 — Python script via GSC API (bulk audit)

For a comprehensive view, a Python script using the Google Search Console API can pull the coverage status of all known URLs programmatically. This approach requires setting up OAuth credentials in Google Cloud Console (service account with GSC read access), but once set up it gives you a full database of indexed vs. non-indexed pages you can analyse locally.

This is worthwhile if the manual export (Method 1) reveals the problem is widespread across sections, as it lets you cross-reference against content quality metrics automatically. It is probably overkill if the issue is clearly concentrated in /routes/.
